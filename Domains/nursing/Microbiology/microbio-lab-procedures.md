## Aseptic Technique

### Why It Matters
Three risks if aseptic technique fails:
1. Contaminate your culture with unwanted organisms
2. Contaminate the environment with your culture
3. Contaminate yourself with your culture

### Disinfection
- Wipe bench with disinfectant (Lysol, 70% ethanol, or lab-provided solution) at START and END of every lab
- Disinfectants do NOT sterilize — they reduce microbial load on surfaces
- Autoclave is used for sterilization (121°C, 15 psi, 15 min)

### Loop / Needle Flaming
- Use inoculating loop or needle — flame in Bunsen burner until glowing red (entire metal portion)
- After flaming: allow to cool a few seconds before contacting culture — hot loop kills cells and causes aerosols
- Cool by touching the inside of the agar tube (not the culture) or touching non-inoculated area
- Reflame loop between each quadrant streak

### DO NOT Rules
- Do NOT set loop/needle down after flaming and before use
- Do NOT leave culture tubes uncapped
- Do NOT talk, cough, or sneeze over open cultures
- Do NOT flame a wet loop (aerosol risk — biohazard)

### Culture Tube Technique
- Hold tube in non-dominant hand at an angle (~45°)
- Flame the mouth of the tube BEFORE and AFTER removing cap
- Remove cap with pinky finger of dominant hand — hold it, do not set it down
- Insert loop, collect culture, remove loop, reflame tube mouth, recap

### Quadrant Streak Plate (Isolation Streak)
Goal: dilute bacteria across plate to get isolated single colonies

**Jackson's annotations: 2 loopfuls → 2 pulls → 1 pull**

Procedure:
1. Flame loop, let cool
2. Inoculate Quadrant 1 — streak back and forth across ~1/4 of plate (2 loopfuls of inoculum)
3. Flame loop, rotate plate 90°
4. Streak through end of Q1 into Q2 (2 pulls through Q1 lines)
5. Flame loop, rotate plate 90°
6. Streak through end of Q2 into Q3 (1 pull through Q2 lines)
7. Flame loop, rotate plate 90°
8. Streak through end of Q3 into Q4 (no new inoculum — just diluting)
9. Do NOT go back to Q1 — unidirectional only
10. Invert plate for incubation (agar side UP) to prevent condensation dripping on colonies

**Expected result:** Single isolated colonies in Q3 or Q4

### Petri Dish History
- Julius Petri (1887) — invented the petri dish while working under Robert Koch
- Angelina Fanny Hesse — suggested using agar as solidifying agent (her mother-in-law used it for jam)
- Before agar: gelatin was used, but it liquefies at incubation temperature and is digested by many bacteria

## Gram Staining

### History
- Developed by Hans Christian Gram in 1884
- Differential stain — distinguishes Gram+ from Gram- bacteria
- Based on cell wall structure — specifically peptidoglycan thickness

### Cell Wall Difference
| Feature | Gram-Positive | Gram-Negative |
|---------|---------------|---------------|
| Peptidoglycan | Thick (20–80 nm) | Thin (2–7 nm) |
| Outer membrane | None | Present (lipopolysaccharide) |
| Result | Purple/violet | Pink/red |
| KOH test | No strings | Strings (DNA releases) |

### Gram Stain Steps (timing critical)
1. **Crystal violet** — primary stain — 1 minute — rinse with water
2. **Gram's iodine (mordant)** — 1 minute — rinse with water (iodine fixes CV to peptidoglycan)
3. **95% Ethanol (decolorizer)** — 5–10 seconds (critical step) — rinse immediately
   - Gram+: thick peptidoglycan retains CV-iodine complex → stays purple
   - Gram-: thin peptidoglycan + outer membrane dissolves → loses CV → colorless
4. **Safranin (counterstain)** — 1 minute — rinse with water — blot dry (do NOT rub)
   - Gram- cells take up safranin → pink/red
   - Gram+ cells already purple → may appear slightly darker but still purple

### Smear Preparation
1. Place small drop of water on slide (or use broth culture directly)
2. Add small amount of culture with loop — spread thin
3. Allow to air dry completely
4. Heat fix: pass through flame 3× quickly (or use slide warmer)
   - Heat fix kills cells, sticks them to slide, denatures proteins for stain uptake
5. Proceed with staining steps above

### Cell Morphology Types
- **Cocci:** round/spherical
  - Diplococcus: pairs
  - Streptococcus: chains
  - Staphylococcus: clusters (grape-like)
  - Tetrad: groups of 4
  - Sarcina: groups of 8
- **Rods (Bacilli):** elongated
  - Single rods, diplobacillus (pairs), streptobacillus (chains)
- **Spirals:**
  - Vibrio: comma-shaped
  - Spirillum: rigid spiral
  - Spirochete: flexible spiral

### Microscopy Types Used in Course
- **Brightfield (used in BIOL 3201):** light passes through specimen; staining required for contrast
  - Components: ocular lens (10×), objective lenses (4×, 10×, 40×, 100× oil), condenser, iris diaphragm, coarse/fine focus
  - Total magnification = ocular × objective
  - Oil immersion lens (100×): requires immersion oil to increase resolution
- **Phase contrast:** uses phase ring to enhance contrast without staining; good for live cells
- **Darkfield:** light hits specimen from sides; bright objects on dark background; good for motility/spirochetes
- **Fluorescent (fluorescence):** UV light + fluorescent dyes or antibodies; used in clinical ID and research

## Primary Tests

### Overview
Primary tests narrow bacterial ID to the family level before secondary (biochemical) tests.

**The 4 Primary Tests:**
1. Gram stain
2. Catalase
3. Oxidase
4. KOH (potassium hydroxide)

### Catalase Test
- **Principle:** catalase enzyme breaks down H₂O₂ → H₂O + O₂ (bubbles)
  - 2 H₂O₂ → 2 H₂O + O₂ (SOD enzyme also involved)
- **Procedure:** place drop of 3% H₂O₂ on slide, add colony with loop
- **Positive result:** immediate vigorous bubbling
- **Negative result:** no bubbles
- **Clinical significance:**
  - Catalase+ → Staphylococcaceae (Gram+ cocci)
  - Catalase- → Streptococcaceae (Gram+ cocci)
  - All Gram- rods = Catalase+

### Oxidase Test
- **Principle:** detects cytochrome C oxidase in electron transport chain
- **Procedure:** place oxidase test strip (or Kovac's oxidase reagent) on filter paper, add colony
- **Positive result:** color change yellow → blue/purple within 30 seconds
- **Negative result:** no color change within 30 seconds (or change after 30 sec = negative)
- **CRITICAL:** Do NOT use metal loop — nickel oxidizes and gives false positive; use wooden stick or plastic loop
- **Clinical significance:**
  - Oxidase+ Gram- rod → Pseudomonadaceae
  - Oxidase- Gram- rod → Enterobacteriaceae

### KOH Test (3% Potassium Hydroxide)
- **Principle:** KOH lyses thin Gram- cell walls, releasing DNA → viscous strings
- **Procedure:** mix colony with drop of 3% KOH on slide, use loop to stir/lift
- **Positive result:** strings form when loop lifted (like egg whites) = Gram-negative confirmed
- **Negative result:** no strings = Gram-positive
- **Use:** rapid confirmation of Gram stain result; especially useful when Gram stain is ambiguous

### Oxygen Requirements
| Type | O₂ Requirement | Example |
|------|----------------|---------|
| Strict (obligate) aerobe | O₂ required | Pseudomonas aeruginosa |
| Facultative anaerobe | O₂ preferred, can survive without | Most Enterobacteriaceae, Staphylococcus |
| Aerotolerant anaerobe | No O₂ needed, but tolerates it | Streptococcus |
| Strict (obligate) anaerobe | O₂ toxic | Clostridium |
| Microaerophile | Low O₂ needed | Campylobacter |

### Media Types
- **Selective media:** inhibits certain organisms, allows others
  - MSA (Mannitol Salt Agar): high salt → selects for Staphylococcus
  - EMB (Eosin Methylene Blue): selects for Gram- (inhibits Gram+); E. coli → metallic green sheen
  - Bile esculin agar: selects for Enterococcus/Streptococcus bovis group
- **Differential media:** distinguishes organisms based on metabolic reaction
  - Blood agar (SBA): differentiates hemolysis patterns (alpha, beta, gamma)
  - MacConkey agar: Gram- only; lactose fermenters turn pink
- **Selective AND differential:**
  - MSA is both: selects Staph AND shows mannitol fermentation (yellow halo = positive)

### Bacterial ID Flowchart — Primary Tests

```
GRAM STAIN
├── GRAM+ COCCI
│   ├── Catalase (+) → Q2: STAPHYLOCOCCACEAE
│   └── Catalase (-) → Q1: STREPTOCOCCACEAE
├── GRAM+ ROD → Q3: BACILLACEAE
└── GRAM- ROD
    ├── Oxidase (+) → Q4: PSEUDOMONADACEAE
    └── Oxidase (-) → Q5: ENTEROBACTERIACEAE
```

## Dilution Problems

### Core Formula
**CFU/mL = (Number of colonies counted) / (Volume plated × Dilution factor)**

Or equivalently: **CFU/mL = Colony count × (1/dilution factor) / volume plated (mL)**

### Countable Range
- Valid count: **30–300 colonies** on a plate
- <30 = TFTC (Too Few To Count) — statistically unreliable
- >300 = TNTC (Too Numerous To Count) — plates overgrown, can't distinguish colonies

### 3-Step Process
1. Identify the dilution factor at each step
2. Find total dilution at the tube/plate you counted
3. Apply formula: CFU/mL = colonies ÷ (volume plated × total dilution)

### Serial Dilution Math
- Each 1:10 dilution = multiply dilution factor by 10⁻¹
- Example series: undiluted → 10⁻¹ → 10⁻² → 10⁻³ → 10⁻⁴
- Transfer 1 mL into 9 mL = 1:10 dilution each step
- Transfer 0.1 mL into 9.9 mL = 1:100 dilution each step

### Worked Example
- Count: 71 colonies
- Dilution: 10⁻³ (three 1:10 serial dilutions)
- Volume plated: 1 mL

**CFU/mL = 71 / (1 mL × 10⁻³) = 71 × 10³ = 7.1 × 10⁴ CFU/mL**

### Scientific Notation Rules
- Move decimal left → positive exponent
- Move decimal right → negative exponent
- 7,100 = 7.1 × 10³
- 0.0071 = 7.1 × 10⁻³

### Dilution Factor When Plating Less Than 1 mL
- If you plate 0.1 mL from a 10⁻³ dilution:
- Additional dilution factor = 0.1 = 10⁻¹
- Total dilution = 10⁻³ × 10⁻¹ = 10⁻⁴
- CFU/mL = colonies / 10⁻⁴ = colonies × 10⁴

## Lab 5: Environmental Factors & Antibiotic Sensitivity

### Temperature Effects Experiment
Organisms tested: *Pseudomonas fluorescens*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Serratia marcescens*

Temperature conditions:
- **5°C** (refrigerator) — cold
- **25°C** (room temp) — ambient
- **37°C** (incubator) — body temp
- **41°C** (incubator) — elevated/fever temp

Observations:
- *Serratia marcescens* produces red pigment (prodigiosin) at 25°C but NOT at 37°C — pigment production is temperature-regulated
- *Pseudomonas* spp. grow optimally at 25–30°C; still grow at 37°C; limited at 41°C
- Growth = turbidity in broth tube or colonies on plate

### Oxygen Requirements Experiment
- Use anaerobic jar (GasPak system)
- GasPak generates H₂ + CO₂ → catalyst (palladium) combines H₂ + O₂ → H₂O → removes O₂
- Methylene blue indicator strip: blue = aerobic, colorless = anaerobic
- Obligate anaerobes only grow in anaerobic jar (not on regular plates)
- Facultative anaerobes grow both aerobically and anaerobically

### Kirby-Bauer Antibiotic Sensitivity Testing
**Principle:** Measure zones of inhibition around antibiotic-impregnated discs on Mueller-Hinton agar

**Procedure:**
1. Make 0.5 McFarland standard bacterial suspension
2. Swab entire surface of Mueller-Hinton agar plate (lawn)
3. Place antibiotic discs with forceps (flame forceps first)
4. Press discs gently to ensure contact
5. Invert plate, incubate 18–24h at 35–37°C
6. Measure zone diameter in mm (include disc diameter)

**Interpretation (compare to CLSI breakpoints):**
- S = Susceptible (zone ≥ threshold)
- I = Intermediate
- R = Resistant (zone < threshold or no zone)

### Secondary Biochemical Tests — Detailed Procedures

**SIM (Sulfur-Indole-Motility)**
- Stab inoculate into SIM deep tube
- Incubate 24–48h at 37°C
- H₂S: black precipitate (FeS) along stab line = +
- Indole: add 5 drops Kovac's reagent → red ring at top = + (presence of tryptophanase)
- Motility: diffuse cloudiness radiating from stab line = + (flagella present)

**MRVP (Methyl Red / Voges-Proskauer)**
- Inoculate MRVP broth, incubate 48–72h at 37°C
- Split into two tubes after incubation
- **Methyl Red:** add 5 drops methyl red indicator → RED = positive (mixed acid fermentation, pH <4.5)
- **Voges-Proskauer:** add 0.6 mL alpha-naphthol + 0.2 mL 40% KOH → RED/PINK after 15 min = + (acetoin production, butanediol fermentation)
- MR and VP are usually opposite (E. coli = MR+/VP-, Klebsiella = MR-/VP+)

**Simmons Citrate**
- Streak citrate slant
- Incubate 24–48h at 37°C
- BLUE = positive (organism uses citrate as sole carbon source → alkaline products)
- Green/unchanged = negative

**Urea Hydrolysis**
- Inoculate urea broth
- Incubate 24–48h at 37°C
- HOT PINK/MAGENTA = positive (urease breaks urea → ammonia → alkaline)
- Yellow/no change = negative

**Phenylalanine Deaminase**
- Streak PA agar slant
- Incubate 24h at 37°C
- Add 4–5 drops 10% FeCl₃ → GREEN = positive (Proteus, Providencia, Morganella)
- No color change = negative

**EMB (Eosin Methylene Blue) Agar**
- Selective for Gram-; inhibits Gram+
- Lactose/sucrose fermenters produce acid → dye precipitates
- E. coli: metallic green sheen (strong acid production)
- Other Gram-: dark pink (moderate)
- No growth = Gram+ (or non-enteric Gram-)

**Coagulase (tube)**
- Mix colony into rabbit plasma
- Incubate 4h at 37°C
- Check for clot (tip tube gently)
- If no clot at 4h: leave at room temp overnight, check again
- Clot = positive → S. aureus (97%)
- Note: coagulase-negative Staph = S. epidermidis or S. saprophyticus

**MSA (Mannitol Salt Agar)**
- 7.5% NaCl selects for Staphylococcus
- Mannitol fermentation → yellow pH indicator halo = +
- No fermentation = stays red/pink

**Bile Esculin Agar**
- Bile (ox bile) selects for bile-tolerant organisms
- Esculin hydrolysis → esculetin + iron → BLACK precipitate = positive
- Enterococcus and S. bovis group = +; most other Strep = -

**Hemolysis on SBA (Sheep Blood Agar)**
- **Beta hemolysis:** complete clear zone (RBC completely lysed) → S. pyogenes, S. agalactiae, S. aureus
- **Alpha hemolysis:** partial/green zone (RBC partially lysed → methemoglobin) → S. pneumoniae, viridans Strep
- **Gamma hemolysis:** no hemolysis → Enterococcus (mostly), E. faecalis

**Nitrate Reduction**
- Inoculate nitrate broth, incubate 24–48h
- Add sulfanilic acid + DMCA (Griess reagents)
- RED = positive (NO₃ → NO₂ detected)
- No color: add zinc dust
  - Red after zinc = truly negative (zinc reduced any remaining nitrate — no reduction occurred)
  - No color after zinc = positive (organism reduced all NO₃ beyond NO₂, to N₂ gas)

**Starch Hydrolysis**
- Streak starch plate, incubate 24–48h
- Flood plate with Gram's iodine
- CLEAR HALO around colony = positive (starch hydrolyzed by amylase)
- Blue-black all around colony = negative

**O/F Glucose**
- Two tubes of O/F glucose media, one sealed with paraffin (anaerobic), one open (aerobic)
- **Oxidative:** yellow only in open tube
- **Fermentative:** yellow in BOTH tubes
- **Inert (non-reactive):** no yellow in either tube (alkaline = blue/green)

**Phenol Red Carbohydrate Tubes**
- Phenol red indicator: yellow = acid (fermentation), red = neutral/alkaline
- Gas production: bubble in Durham tube = gas+
- Tests specific sugars (glucose, lactose, sucrose, mannitol, etc.)

**Kirby-Bauer** — see Lab 5 section above

**Novobiocin**
- Disc test for Staphylococcus
- Sensitive (zone ≥16mm) = S. epidermidis
- Resistant (zone ≤12mm) = S. saprophyticus

**Bacitracin**
- Disc test for Streptococcus
- Sensitive = S. pyogenes (Group A Strep)
- Resistant = most other Strep species

**Optochin**
- Disc test for alpha-hemolytic Strep
- Sensitive (zone ≥14mm) = S. pneumoniae
- Resistant = viridans Streptococcus

### Biosafety Levels
| BSL | Risk Level | Examples | Required Protection |
|-----|-----------|----------|---------------------|
| BSL-1 | Minimal | E. coli K12, Bacillus subtilis | Lab coat, gloves, eye protection |
| BSL-2 | Moderate | Staphylococcus aureus, Salmonella, Hepatitis A/B | BSL-1 + limited access, biohazard signs |
| BSL-3 | High | Mycobacterium tuberculosis, West Nile virus, SARS | BSL-2 + N95/respirator, negative-pressure room |
| BSL-4 | Extreme | Ebola, Marburg, smallpox | BSL-3 + full pressurized suit, airlock entry |

All organisms used in BIOL 3201 are BSL-1 or BSL-2.

### Ubiquity of Microbes
- Microorganisms are everywhere — air, skin, surfaces, soil, water
- Key concept: microbes outnumber human cells ~10:1
- Normal flora: mutualistic/commensal microbes that colonize skin, GI tract, respiratory tract
- Opportunistic pathogens: normally harmless, cause disease when immune system compromised

### Lab History
- **Julius Petri (1887):** invented petri dish while working with Robert Koch
- **Angelina Fanny Hesse (Lina Hesse):** suggested agar as solidifying agent (from cooking; was lab assistant to her husband Walther Hesse, who worked with Koch)
- **Hans Christian Gram (1884):** developed Gram stain
- **Joseph Lister:** first to use antiseptics in surgery; developed aseptic technique in clinical setting
- **Louis Pasteur:** germ theory, pasteurization, swan-neck flask experiment
- **Robert Koch:** Koch's postulates, culture techniques, identified TB and cholera causative agents

### Quadrant Streak Review
- Purpose: isolate single colonies from mixed culture
- Flame loop before EVERY quadrant
- Must rotate plate between quadrants
- Isolated colonies = pure culture for secondary testing
- Incubate INVERTED to prevent condensation on colonies

### Gram Stain Steps — Timing Summary
| Step | Reagent | Time | Rinse |
|------|---------|------|-------|
| 1 | Crystal violet (primary) | 1 min | Water |
| 2 | Gram's iodine (mordant) | 1 min | Water |
| 3 | 95% Ethanol (decolorizer) | 5–10 sec | Water immediately |
| 4 | Safranin (counterstain) | 1 min | Water, blot dry |

Common Gram stain errors:
- Over-decolorize → Gram+ appears Gram-
- Under-decolorize → Gram- appears Gram+
- Old culture → Gram+ may stain Gram- (cell wall degrades)
- Too thick smear → uneven staining

### Primary Test Flow (Exam Ready)
```
Gram+ Cocci
  → Catalase+ → Staph family (coagulase, MSA, novobiocin)
  → Catalase- → Strep family (bile esculin, BHI NaCl, hemolysis, optochin, bacitracin)

Gram+ Rod → Bacillus family (endospores, hemolysis, starch, nitrate)

Gram- Rod
  → Oxidase+ → Pseudomonas family (pyocyanin, fluorescein, temp growth, O/F glucose)
  → Oxidase- → Enterobacteriaceae (SIM, MRVP, citrate, urea, phenylalanine, EMB)
```

### KOH Interpretation
- KOH 3% lyses Gram- cell walls (thin peptidoglycan + outer membrane dissolves)
- DNA released → viscous strings when loop is lifted
- Strong + = strings 1–2 cm
- Gram+ = no stringing (thick peptidoglycan resists KOH)
- Faster/easier than Gram stain when stain result is ambiguous

### Key Test Details for Exam

**MSA (Mannitol Salt Agar):**
- 7.5% NaCl = selective for Staphylococci
- Phenol red indicator → yellow = mannitol fermented (acid)
- S. aureus: grows AND ferments (yellow halo)
- S. epidermidis: grows but no fermentation (stays red)
- S. saprophyticus: grows but no fermentation

**SBA (Sheep Blood Agar) Hemolysis:**
- Beta = clear zone; Alpha = green; Gamma = no zone
- S. aureus → beta
- S. epidermidis → gamma
- S. pyogenes → beta
- S. pneumoniae → alpha
- Enterococcus → gamma (usually)

**Coagulase:**
- Tests for clot-forming enzyme
- S. aureus = +; CNS (S. epidermidis, S. saprophyticus) = -
- 97% sensitivity for S. aureus
- Tube coagulase > slide coagulase (definitive test)

**Bile Esculin:**
- Selects for bile-tolerant, differentiates esculin hydrolyzers
- Enterococcus and S. bovis = + (black)
- S. pyogenes, S. agalactiae, S. pneumoniae = -

**BHI + 6.5% NaCl:**
- Tests salt tolerance
- Enterococcus = + (halotolerant)
- Most Strep = -

**Nitrate Reduction:**
- Add Griess reagent after incubation
- Red = + (NO₃ → NO₂)
- No red → add zinc → red = truly negative; no red = + (went past NO₂ to N₂ gas)

### Streptococcus vs Staphylococcus Quick Compare
| Feature | Streptococcus | Staphylococcus |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Arrangement | Chains | Clusters |
| Catalase | - | + |
| Oxygen | Aerotolerant anaerobe | Facultative anaerobe |
| Virulence | Varies by species | S. aureus highly virulent |
| Key pathogen | S. pyogenes (GAS), S. pneumoniae | S. aureus (MRSA) |

### Enterobacteriaceae Secondary Test Summary for Exam
High-yield patterns:
- E. coli: Indole+, MR+, VP-, Citrate-, EMB metallic green sheen
- Klebsiella: Indole-, MR-, VP+, Citrate+, Urea+, non-motile
- Salmonella: H₂S+, Indole-, MR+, VP-, Citrate+, Motile
- Proteus: H₂S+, Urea+, Phenylalanine+, SWARMS on agar
- Serratia: Red pigment at 25°C, VP+, Citrate+, no H₂S
