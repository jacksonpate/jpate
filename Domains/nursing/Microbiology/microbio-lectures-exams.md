## 1. Environmental Effects I — Temperature, Oxygen, Antimicrobials
**Source:** `Environmentall+Effects+I.pdf`

### Temperature Groups
- **Psychrophiles:** -5 to 20°C (optimal ~15°C)
- **Mesophiles:** 20–50°C (most human pathogens)
- **Thermophiles:** 50–80°C
- **Hyperthermophiles:** >80°C
- **Psychrotrophs:** Mesophiles that can grow at refrigerator temp (4°C) — e.g., *Listeria monocytogenes*, *Campylobacter*
- *Serratia marcescens* produces red pigment **prodigiosin** at 25°C (not at 37°C)

### Oxygen Requirement Groups
| Group | O2 Requirement | Examples |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| Obligate aerobe | Requires O2 | *Micrococcus* |
| Microaerophile | 2–10% O2 | *H. pylori*, *Campylobacter* |
| Facultative anaerobe | Grows with or without O2 | *E. coli*, *Salmonella* |
| Aerotolerant anaerobe | Doesn't use O2 but tolerates it | *Streptococci* |
| Obligate anaerobe | Cannot tolerate O2 | *Clostridium* |

**Toxic O2 species and neutralizing enzymes:**
- Catalase: H2O2 → O2 + H2O
- Peroxidase: neutralizes H2O2
- Superoxide dismutase: neutralizes superoxide radicals

### Media for Oxygen Testing
- **FTM (Fluid Thioglycollate Medium):** glucose + cystine + sodium thioglycollate + resazurin dye (turns pink in presence of O2)
- **Brewer's Anaerobic Agar**
- **Anaerobic jar with GasPak:** 2H2 + O2 → 2H2O; palladium pellets as catalyst; methylene blue indicator strip (colorless = anaerobic)

### Antimicrobials
- **Antibiotics:** low molecular weight compounds produced by microorganisms (e.g., penicillin, streptomycin)
- **Semi-synthetics:** chemically altered antibiotics
- **Synthetics:** lab-made (e.g., sulfa drugs)
- **MRSA:** methicillin-resistant *Staphylococcus aureus* — major problem
- **HAIs:** healthcare-associated infections

### Kirby-Bauer Disk Diffusion Method
- Medium: Mueller-Hinton agar
- Antibiotic-impregnated disks placed on lawn of bacteria
- Measure zone of inhibition in mm
- Interpretation: **Resistant / Intermediate / Sensitive**

### Table: Antibiotic Modes of Action
| Antibiotic | Spectrum | Target |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Penicillin | G+/G- | Cell wall — β-lactamase/peptidoglycan synthesis |
| Vancomycin | G+ | Peptidoglycan |
| Bacitracin | G+ | Peptidoglycan monomer transport |
| Isoniazid | *M. tuberculosis* | Mycolic acid synthesis |
| Fluoroquinolones | G+/G- | DNA topoisomerase (gyrase) |
| Rifamycins | G+/some G- | RNA polymerase |
| Tetracyclines | G+/G- | 30S ribosomal subunit |
| Streptomycin | G+/G- | 30S ribosomal subunit |
| Chloramphenicol | G+/G- | 50S ribosomal subunit |
| Sulfa drugs | G+/G- | PABA/folic acid synthesis |

### Antiseptics vs Disinfectants
- **Antiseptics:** applied to living tissue (e.g., alcohol, betadine/iodine)
- **Disinfectants:** applied to inanimate objects (e.g., bleach)
- **Bacteriostatic:** inhibits growth but doesn't kill
- **Bactericidal:** kills bacteria
- **Sanitizers:** reduce microbial load
- **Sterilants/Sporocides:** kill all life including endospores (e.g., ethylene oxide gas)

## 4. Exam 2 Study Guide (Handwritten — BIOL3200/3207)
**Source:** `Exam+2-studyguide.pdf`

### Unit 5-1/5-2: Nutrition and Growth

**Essential Nutrients:**
- Macronutrients: C, H, O, N, P, S; also Mg2+, Ca2+, Fe2+, K+
- Micronutrients: Co, Cu, Mn, Zn, Mo, Ni

**Carbon Acquisition:**
- Heterotrophs: use organic molecules
- Autotrophs: fix CO2
- Lithotrophs: use inorganic minerals

**Nitrogen Cycle:**
- Nitrification: NH4+ → NO3- (nitrifiers: *Nitrosomonas*, *Nitrobacter*)
- Denitrification: NO3- → N2 (denitrifiers: *Paracoccus*)
- Nitrogen fixation: N2 → NH4+ (fixers: *Rhizobium*, *Azotobacter*)

**Transport Systems:**
- Simple diffusion: no energy, no protein
- Facilitated diffusion: no energy, uses protein channel (high→low concentration)
- Symport: two molecules move in same direction (e.g., lactose + H+)
- Antiport: two molecules move in opposite directions (e.g., Na+/H+ antiporter)
- ABC transporters: energy-dependent; found in all 3 domains

**Bacterial Division:**
- Binary fission (standard)
- Exceptions: budding (*Hyphomicrobium*), multiple fission (some cyanobacteria/Gram+)

**Growth Curve (Batch Culture = Closed System):**
1. **Lag:** adapting to environment, synthesizing cell machinery
2. **Log (Exponential):** rapid cell division, straight line on semi-log graph
3. **Stationary:** growth rate = death rate, stress responses activated
4. **Death:** cells dying faster than growing

**Continuous Culture:**
- Chemostat: adds fresh medium + removes culture to maintain steady growth rate
- Maintains all nutrients, keeps cells in log phase

**Counting Methods:**
- Direct microscopic count: all cells (live + dead)
- Viable cell count (plate count): live cells only
- Spectrophotometry (turbidity): all cells

### Unit 6-1: Environmental Effects on Growth

**Normal growth ranges (mesophiles):** 1–2°C to 40°C, pH 4–7, 5×10^-4 to 1% O2

**Temperature Groups:**
- Psychrophiles: 0–20°C
- Mesophiles: 15–45°C (most human pathogens)
- Thermophiles: 40–80°C
- Hyperthermophiles: 65–121°C
- *Thermus aquaticus* discovered in hot springs → Taq polymerase for PCR

**Pressure Groups:**
- Barosensitive: killed by high pressure
- Barotolerant: survive high pressure
- Barophiles: require high pressure (deep-sea organisms)

**pH Groups:**
- Acidophiles: pH 0–5
- Neutrophiles: pH 5–8 (most human pathogens)
- Alkaliphiles: pH >9

**Oxygen Groups:**
- Strict aerobes: require O2
- Microaerophiles: require low O2
- Facultative anaerobes: grow with or without O2
- Aerotolerant anaerobes: grow without O2, tolerate its presence
- Strict anaerobes: cannot survive in presence of O2

**Two reasons an organism can grow in O2:**
1. Use O2 as terminal electron acceptor
2. Produce enzymes that neutralize reactive oxygen species (catalase, superoxide dismutase)

### Unit 6-2: Catabolism and Energy

**Catabolism vs Anabolism:**
- Catabolism: breaks down complex molecules, generates energy
- Anabolism: builds complex molecules, requires energy

**Energy and Carbon Sources:**
- Chemotrophs: chemical compounds as energy source → feeds ETC
- Phototrophs: light as energy source
- Heterotrophs: organic carbon
- Autotrophs: CO2 as carbon source

**ATP Transfer Mechanisms:**
1. Hydrolysis (ATP → ADP + Pi): builds molecules
2. Hydrolysis (ATP → AMP + PPi): breaks down molecules
3. Phosphorylation of organic molecule (glucose → glucose-6-phosphate)

**Substrate-Level vs Oxidative Phosphorylation:**
- Substrate-level: ATP made directly from organic molecule (glycolysis, TCA)
- Oxidative: ATP made via electron transport chain + proton gradient

**Glycolysis Pathways:**
| Pathway | ATP yield | NADH | NADPH | Pyruvates |
|---------|-----------|------|-------|-----------|
| EMP (Embden-Meyerhof-Parnas) | 2 | 2 | 0 | 2 |
| ED (Entner-Doudoroff) | 1 | 1 | 1 | 2 |
| PPP (Pentose Phosphate) | 1 | 2 | — | — (produces 5C sugars) |

**EMP Phases:**
- Energy Investment Phase: 2 ATP used
- Energy Payoff Phase: 4 ATP produced
- Net: 2 ATP

**Complete glucose oxidation (EMP) products:**
- 4 CO2, 6 NADH, 2 FADH2, 2 ATP (before ETC)

**Fermentation Types:**
- Homolactic: glucose → lactic acid (muscle cells, *Lactobacillus*)
- Heterolactic: glucose → lactic acid + CO2 + ethanol
- Ethanolic: glucose → ethanol + CO2 (yeast)
- Mixed acid: lactic acid + ethanol + acetic acid + CO2 (*E. coli*)

**Total ATP yields:**
- Aerobic respiration: 36–38 ATP
- Anaerobic respiration (NO3-, SO4-2, CO2): 30–32 ATP
- Fermentation: 2 ATP only

### Unit 7-1: TCA Cycle

**Transition Step (Pyruvate Oxidation):**
- Pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA via PDC (pyruvate dehydrogenase complex)
- Loses 1 CO2, produces 1 NADH per pyruvate
- Per glucose: 2 Acetyl-CoA + 2 CO2 + 2 NADH

**TCA Cycle Products (per Acetyl-CoA):**
- 2 CO2, 3 NADH, 1 FADH2, 1 ATP/GTP

**Per glucose (×2):**
- 4 CO2, 6 NADH, 2 FADH2, 2 ATP

**Key TCA intermediates for biosynthesis:** isocitrate, glyoxylate, malate

**Glyoxylate Bypass:**
- Used when glucose is absent/scarce
- Incorporates Acetyl-CoA via glyoxylate shunt
- Pros: efficient carbon conservation
- Cons: less ATP, less FADH2, less NADH

### Unit 7-2: Electron Transport System (ETS)

**ETS Flow:** NADH/FADH2 donate electrons → ETC → build proton gradient → protons flow back through ATP synthase → ATP

**E. coli ETS components:** dehydrogenases → quinones → terminal oxidases/reductases

**Mitochondrial ETS:**
- Complex I: NADH dehydrogenase
- Complex II: succinate dehydrogenase
- Complex III: ubiquinol-cytochrome c oxidoreductase
- Complex IV: cytochrome c oxidase (O2 → H2O)

**Proton Motive Force (PMF):** created when H+ pumped across membrane; drives ATP synthase

**Aerobic:** O2 as terminal electron acceptor → H2O
**Anaerobic (prokaryote-specific):** NO3-, SO4-2, CO2 as terminal electron acceptors

### Unit 8: Phototrophy

**Bacteriorhodopsin (halophilic archaea):** single protein, light-driven proton pump

**Photosystems:**
- Anaerobic PS I: doesn't produce O2, generates proton gradient; electron donors: H2S, succinate, Fe2+
- Anaerobic PS II
- Oxygenic Z-scheme: produces O2; electron donor = H2O

**All photosystems share:** light-harvesting complex, ETS, energy carriers (ATP/NADPH)

## 5. Lab — Brightfield Microscopy and Gram Staining
**Source:** `3201-2.pdf`

### History of Microscopy
- ~1590: First microscope, Middleburg, Holland
- 1609: Galileo
- 1660s: Robert Hooke
- 1670s: Antonie van Leeuwenhoek (first observed bacteria)
- 1933: First electron microscope

### Types of Microscopy
| Type | Principle | Stain needed? | Best for |
|------|-----------|--------------|---------|
| Brightfield | Transmitted light | Yes | Routine staining |
| Phase contrast | Refractive index differences | No | Living cells |
| Darkfield | Oblique illumination | No | External morphology only |
| Fluorescent | Fluorophores emit colored light | Fluorophore | Specific targets |
| TEM | Electrons through specimen | — | Internal structures |
| SEM | Electrons scan surface | — | External structures (3D) |

### Key Microscopy Terms
- **Parfocal:** stays in focus when switching objectives
- **Numerical aperture:** light-gathering ability of objective
- **Resolving power:** ability to distinguish two close objects as separate
- **Working distance:** space between objective lens and slide

### Brightfield Microscope Components
- **Framework:** arm + base
- **Light source:** halogen or LED, voltage control dial
- **Stage:** holds slide; stage adjustment knobs
- **Lens system:** oculars (10X) + objectives (10X, 40X, 100X oil immersion) on nosepiece/turret; condenser focuses light, has diaphragm
- **Focusing knobs:** coarse (outer, only use with 10X), fine (inner)
- **Total magnification = ocular × objective** (e.g., 10×100 = 1000X)
- **Immersion oil:** same refractive index as glass; used only with 100X objective

### Gram Staining
**Developed by Hans Christian Gram, 1884 (Danish physician)**

**Why stain:** Bacteria are ~80% water and transparent without stain

**Procedure:**
1. Crystal violet: both G+ and G- turn purple
2. Iodine (mordant): fixes crystal violet
3. Decolorization: G- loses purple (colorless); G+ retains purple
4. Safranin counterstain: G- turns pink/red

**Why G+ retains stain:** thick peptidoglycan layer traps crystal violet-iodine complex
**Why G- loses stain:** thin peptidoglycan, outer membrane washed away by decolorizer

**Culture age:** must be 18–24 hours old for reliable results

### Common Morphologies
| Gram | Shape | Arrangement | Example |
|------|-------|-------------|---------|
| G+ | Cocci | Pairs (diplococci) | *Enterococcus* |
| G+ | Cocci | Tetrads | *Micrococcus* |
| G+ | Cocci | Chains | *Streptococcus* |
| G+ | Cocci | Clusters | *Staphylococcus* |
| G- | Cocci | Pairs/tetrads | *Moraxella* |
| G+ | Rods | Singly | *Bacillus subtilis* |
| G+ | Rods | Chains | *Bacillus cereus* |
| G- | Rods | Singly (straight) | *Pseudomonas* |
| G- | Rods | Curved | *Vibrio* |
| G- | Rods | Spiral | *Campylobacter* |

## 10. Lecture — Bacterial Growth (Week 5, Exam 2 Prep)
**Source:** `Exam2-1.pdf`

### Microbial Nutrition
**Macronutrients:** C, O, H, N, P, S; Mg2+, Ca2+, Fe2+, K+
**Micronutrients:** Co, Cu, Mn, Zn, Mo, Ni

**Energy/Carbon source combinations:**
- Photoheterotroph: light energy + organic carbon
- Photoautotroph: light energy + CO2
- Chemoheterotroph: chemical energy + organic carbon
- Chemoautotroph (lithoautotroph): chemical energy + CO2

**Nitrogen cycle key organisms:**
- Nitrogen fixers: *Rhizobium*, *Azotobacter*
- Nitrifiers: *Nitrosomonas* (NH4+ → NO2-), *Nitrobacter* (NO2- → NO3-)
- Denitrifiers: *Paracoccus* (NO3- → N2)

### Nutrient Uptake
**Passive (no energy):**
- Simple diffusion
- Facilitated diffusion (e.g., aquaporin GlpF for water and glycerol) — high→low concentration, no energy

**Active (energy required):**
- Symport: two molecules same direction (e.g., lactose + H+)
- Antiport: opposite directions (e.g., Na+/H+ antiporter)
- ABC transporters: found in all 3 domains; uptake (nutrients) and efflux (multidrug resistance) types
  - Process: solute binds binding protein → complex binds membrane transporter → ATPase opens channel → solute moves in

### Bacterial Growth
**Binary fission:** one parent → two equal daughter cells
**Exceptions:** *Hyphomicrobium* (budding), some cyanobacteria (multiple fission)

**Growth Math:**
- Nt = N0 × 2^n
- G (generation time) = t / n
- n = (log10(Nt/N0)) / 0.301 = log2(Nt/N0)
- t = G × n

**Example problems:**
- *E. coli* gen time 20 min, 1 cell → 1000 cells: n ≈ 10 generations, t ≈ 200 min
- 4 E. coli → 128: n = 5 generations (2^5 = 32; 4×32 = 128), t = 100 min (if G = 20 min)

### Growth Curve Phases
1. **Lag:** Preparing cell machinery; adapting to environment; no net growth
2. **Log (Exponential):** Straight line on semi-log graph; fastest growth
3. **Stationary:** Growth rate = death rate; stress responses activated; carbon source depleted
4. **Death:** Negative exponential; half-life-like kinetics

**Interesting facts:**
- Hot spring bacteria double in ~10 min
- Deep-sea sediment microbes: generation time ~100 million years

### Continuous Culture
- **Chemostat:** adds fresh medium + removes equal volume → maintains log phase
- Human GI tract functions like a natural chemostat

### Not All Bacteria Are Microscopic — Exceptions Exist
**Common shapes:**

### Light and Microscopy

### Types of Mutations
- **Nonsense:** one base change → STOP codon → **no protein**
- **Silent:** one base change → same amino acid → **no change**
- **Missense:** one base change → different amino acid → **altered protein**
- **Frameshift:** base inserted or deleted → reading frame shifts → **usually nonfunctional protein**

## 13. Exam 3 Study Notes — Transcription, Genetics, DNA
**Source:** `exam 3 .pdf`

### Transcription
**Definition:** Reading DNA to make an RNA copy

**Enzyme:** RNA Polymerase (RNA Pol) — the enzyme that performs transcription

**RNA Pol has two components:**
1. **Sigma factor (σ):** recognizes the promoter
2. **Core polymerase (α2, β, β'):** does the actual synthesis
- Together they form the **holoenzyme**

**Every bacterium has a "default" sigma factor = the "housekeeping" sigma factor**
- In *E. coli*: sigma-70 (σ70)
- Its job: recognize the promoter

**Sigma-70 specifically recognizes:**
- **-35 region** (consensus: TTGACA)
- **-10 region** (consensus: TATAAT) — where promoter is closest to transcription start site
- Both are upstream (before) the transcription start site
- Transcription starts at **+1**

**The Promoter:** DNA signal where a gene begins

**Operator:** where regulators bind to block or allow transcription
- **Repressor:** like a bouncer — blocks the door (blocks transcription)
- **Activator:** like someone holding the door open — promotes transcription (also helps initiation by recruiting chromatin-modifying proteins)

### 3 Phases of Transcription
1. **Initiation:** RNA Pol + σ bind to promoter; DNA unwinds (one helical turn); creates transcription bubble
2. **Elongation:** RNA Pol moves along template; forms 17bp transcription bubble; builds RNA at ~45 bases/sec; creates positive supercoils ahead (removed by DNA topoisomerases)
3. **Termination:** RNA Pol detaches; transcript releases; forms stem-loop structure
   - **Two methods:**
     - **Rho-Independent:** inverted DNA repeat sequences → stem-loop structure forms → causes termination
     - **Rho-Dependent:** relies on Rho protein; contact between Rho + RNA Pol causes termination

### Classes of RNA
- **mRNA (messenger RNA):** encodes proteins; carries the protein-coding message
- **rRNA (ribosomal RNA):** integral/structural part of ribosomes
- **tRNA (transfer RNA):** shuttles amino acids during translation

### DNA Template vs RNA Product
| DNA template | DNA complement | DNA | RNA |
|-------------|---------------|-----|-----|
| A | T | A | U |
| T | A | T | A |
| G | C | G | C |
| C | G | C | G |

**Antibiotics must:** kill/slow pathogen; must NOT harm the host

### DNA Properties
- At high temps (50–90°C) DNA **denatures** (strands separate)

### Topoisomerases
- **Type I topoisomerase:** cuts ONE strand; relieves negative supercoils
- **Type II topoisomerase (DNA gyrase):** cuts BOTH strands; introduces negative supercoils
- **DNA gyrase** is targeted by **quinolone antibiotics** (fluoroquinolones)

### DNA Transmission
- **Vertical transmission:** parent → offspring (normal cell division)
- **Horizontal Gene Transfer (HGT):** DNA moves between cells

**3 mechanisms of HGT:**
1. **Transformation:** uptake of naked DNA from environment
2. **Transduction:** uses viruses (bacteriophages) to transfer DNA between cells
3. **Conjugation:** requires a pilus; donor cell (F+) transfers DNA to recipient cell (F-)
