## Source 2 — `exam 2 micro.pdf` (9 pages)

Handwritten notes — partial text extracted via OCR layer.

**Chapter 1: Bacterial Growth**

### Essential Nutrients
- Macronutrients (required in large quantities): C, O, H, N, P, S; ions for protein function: Mg2+, Ca2+, Fe2+, K+
- Micronutrients (required in small quantities): trace elements for enzyme function — Co, Cu, Mn, Zn, Mo, Ni
- All Earth life-forms are carbon-based
  - Heterotrophs use preformed molecules → release CO2
  - Autotrophs fix CO2 → assemble into organic molecules (mainly sugars)
  - Phototrophy: uses light energy
  - Lithotrophy: oxidation of minerals

### Membranes & Transport
- Passive Transport (no energy): Simple diffusion, Facilitated diffusion
- Active Transport (requires energy): Coupled transport, ABC transporters
- Facilitated diffusion: moves solutes from high → low concentration (e.g., Aquaporin family — transports water/polar molecules like glycerol)
- Active Transport — Coupled Transport:
  - Symport: two molecules travel in same direction
  - Antiport: actively transported molecule moves opposite to driving ion
- ABC Transporters: found in all 3 domains of life
  - Uptake ABC transporters: transport nutrients
  - Efflux ABC transporters: multidrug efflux pumps

### The Growth Cycle
- Most bacteria divide by binary fission (splits into 2)
- Some divide asymmetrically (into more than 2)
- Never lasts indefinitely — limited resources
- Batch culture: liquid medium within a closed system
- Continuous culture: cells achieve steady state → allows detailed study of bacterial physiology (like a chemostat)
- Human GI tract is engineered like a chemostat

### Growth Calculations
- Number of generations: n
- Total cells: 2^n (starting from 1 cell)
- Nt = No × 2^n (starting from No cells)

### Culture Media
- Only 0.1% of all microorganisms are cultured
- Liquid broth: useful for studying growth characteristics of pure culture
- Solid (gelled with agar): useful to separate mixed cultures
- Complex media: nutrient rich but poorly defined
- Synthetic media: precisely defined
- Enriched media: complex media + specific blood components

### Selective vs. Differential Media
- Selective: favors growth of one organism over another
- Differential: exploits differences between two species that grow equally

### Environmental Limits on Growth
- "Normal" conditions: sea level, 20–40°C, neutral pH
- Psychrophiles: 0–28°C
- Mesophiles: 15–45°C
- Thermophiles: 40–80°C
- Hyperthermophiles: 65–121°C
  - Ex: Thermus aquaticus — first source of high-temp DNA polymerase used for PCR

### pH Ranges
- Neutralophiles: pH 5–8
- Acidophiles: pH 0–5
- Alkaliphiles: pH 9–11 (found in soda lakes, e.g., Lake Magadi, Kenya — pink color from Spirulina)
- Helicobacter pylori: most enzymes operate best between pH 5–8.5

### Oxygen Requirements
- Strict aerobes: can only grow in oxygen
- Microaerophiles: grow only at lower O2 levels
- Strict anaerobes: die in the least bit of oxygen
- Aerotolerant anaerobes: grow in oxygen while retaining fermentation-based metabolism
- Facultative anaerobes: can live with or without oxygen (have both fermentative and respiratory ability)

### Sterilization & Control
- Sterilization: killing of all living organisms
- Disinfection: killing/removal of pathogens from inanimate objects
- Antisepsis: killing/removal of pathogens from surface of living tissues
- Sanitation: reducing microbial population to safe levels

### High Temperature Methods
- Boiling water (100°C): kills most cells
- Pasteurization:
  - LTLT: 63°C / 30 min
  - HTST: 72°C / 15 sec
  - UHT: 134°C / 2 sec
- Cold (low temp): slows growth; preserves strains
  - 4–8°C: refrigeration for food preservation
  - Listeria monocytogenes: loves to live in cold and can multiply
  - Long-term storage: glycerol at –70°C; lyophilization (freeze-drying)
- Filtration: micropore filters 0.2 µm — removes microbial cells, NOT viruses
- Disinfectants: Ethanol, Iodine, Chlorine, Ethylene oxide

## Source 3 — `Exam2-1.pdf` (38 pages) — Week 5: Lecture 1 — Bacterial Growth

### 1. Microbial Nutrition
- Essential nutrients must be supplied
- Macronutrients (large quantities): C, O, H, N, P, S; Mg2+, Ca2+, Fe2+, K+
- Micronutrients (small quantities): trace elements for enzyme function — Co, Cu, Mn, Zn, Mo, Ni
- Heterotrophs: use preformed organic molecules → release CO2
- Autotrophs: fix CO2 → assemble into organic molecules (mainly sugars)
  - Phototrophy: use light energy
  - Lithotrophy: energy from oxidation of minerals
- The Carbon Cycle and Nitrogen Cycle govern how microbes build biomass

### 2. Nutrient Uptake
- Membranes separate inside from outside
- Passive transport (no energy): simple and facilitated diffusion
- Active transport (requires energy): coupled transport, ABC transporters
- Facilitated diffusion: solutes move from high → low concentration (no energy)
  - Ex: Aquaporin family transports water and small polar molecules (glycerol)
- Coupled transport systems:
  - Symport: two molecules travel in the same direction
  - Antiport: actively transported molecule moves opposite to driving ion
  - Ex: Lactose symporter (lactose + H+); Na+/H+ antiporter (H+ out, Na+ out)
- ABC transporters (ATP-binding cassette superfamily):
  - Found in all three domains of life
  - Uptake ABC transporters: transport nutrients
  - Efflux ABC transporters: multidrug efflux pumps (multi-drug resistance)
  - Mechanism: solute binds binding protein → complex binds membrane transporter → ATPase opens channel → solute moves into cell

### 2. The Growth Cycle
- Binary fission: one parent → two equal daughter cells
- Some divide asymmetrically (e.g., Hyphomicrobium divides by budding)
- Others split into more than two cells
- Growth rate is exponential: 2^n where n = number of generations
- Some cyanobacteria divide by multiple fission

### Exponential Growth
- Total number Nt = 2^n (from 1 cell)
- Exponential growth never lasts indefinitely; resources are always the limiting factor
- Batch culture: liquid medium within a closed system

### Bacterial Growth Curves
Four phases: Lag → Logarithmic → Stationary → Death

### Continuous Culture
- Chemostat: ensures logarithmic growth by constantly adding and removing equal amounts of culture media
- All cells achieve a steady state — allows detailed study of bacterial physiology
- Note: human GI tract is engineered much like a chemostat

### Generation Time Calculations
- G = t/n (t = time interval, n = number of generations)
- Nt = No × 2^n
- n = log2(Nt/N0) = (log10(Nt/N0)) / 0.301
- t = G × n

**Practice problems:**
- E. coli generation time = 20 min. Starting from 1 cell → ~1,000 cells takes ~200 min
- Virus producing 800 progeny: growth proportional to 800^n
- 4 E. coli → 128 cells: n=5 generations, t=100 min
- Hawaiian bobtail squid (Vibrio fischeri): 95% expelled at daybreak, 5% remains (2.5×10^7), repopulates to 5×10^8 in 12 hours → ~4.32 generations → generation time ~180 min

### Chapter Summary
- Microbes require essential macronutrients and micronutrients
- Classified on basis of carbon and energy acquisition
- Transport: passive (facilitated diffusion) and active (coupled transport, ABC transporters)
- Growth cycle in batch culture: Lag, logarithmic, stationary, death
- Can be cultured on solid or liquid media
- Counted directly or indirectly

## Source 4 — `Exam2-2.pdf` (26 pages) — Week 5: Lecture 2 — Bacterial Culture and Count

### 3. Culturing Bacteria
- Microbes in nature exist in complex multispecies communities; for detailed studies must be grown in pure culture
- Only 0.1% of all microorganisms are cultured
- Bacteria grown in culture media:
  - Liquid/broth: useful for studying growth characteristics of pure culture
  - Solid (gelled with agar): useful to separate mixed cultures

### Types of Media
- Complex media: nutrient rich but poorly defined
- Synthetic media: precisely defined
- Enriched media: complex + specific blood components (fastidious organisms — "requiring blood like a vampire")
- Selective media: favor growth of one organism over another
- Differential media: exploit differences between two species that grow equally well
  - Ex: MacConkey medium — E. coli vs. Salmonella; lactose + peptones + neutral red indicator; red = acid (E. coli ferments lactose → red colonies; Salmonella does not)

### Isolating Pure Colonies
- Streak plate method: drag loop across agar surface → 24-hour incubation → isolated colonies
- Spread plate method: serial dilutions of liquid culture → small amount plated → plate with isolated colonies used → also used to count colonies

### 4. Counting Bacteria
- Multiple techniques; each measures different physical or biochemical aspect of growth
- Direct microscopic count: counting chamber (special microscope slide) — counts live and dead cells
  - Chamber: 1mm × 1mm × 0.2mm deep → calculate cells/mm3, cells/µL, cells/mL
  - 1 µL = 1 mm3; 1 mL = 1 cm3
- FACS (Fluorescence-Activated Cell Sorter): fluorescent cells through small opening past a laser; detects light scatter, particle size, particle shape
- Viable cell count: counts replicating and colony forming cells
  - Pour plate method: tenfold serial dilutions → plate each → count plate with 30–300 CFU
- Optical density (spectrophotometer): fastest way to measure cell density (indirect method)

### Pour Plate / CFU Calculation
Steps:
1. Calculate total dilution at each tube
2. Find plate with 30–300 colonies
3. Determine total volume transferred to that plate
4. CFU/mL = colonies / (dilution × volume plated)

**Practice problems:**
- 63 colonies from tube #5 (10^-5 dilution) = 6.3 × 10^6 CFU/mL
- Answer: 4.5 × 10^4 CFU/mL
- Answer: 1.28 × 10^9 CFU/mL
- 37 colonies at 1:100,000 dilution, plating 1 mL → 3.7 × 10^6 CFU/mL in soap
- Same soap, 100 mL total: 3.7 × 10^8 CFU/100mL
- 210 colonies from 0.1 mL of 10^-6 dilution → 2.1 × 10^9 CFU/mL
- Bobtail squid: N0 = 2.5×10^7, Nt = 5×10^8, n = 4.32 generations, G = 180 min

### Chapter Summary
- Growth cycle: Lag, logarithmic, stationary, death
- Bacteria cultured on solid or liquid media
- Counted directly or indirectly

## Source 5 — `exam2-3.pdf` (27 pages) — Week 6 — Environmental Influence and Control of Microbial Growth

### Environmental Limits on Growth
- "Normal" growth conditions: sea level, 20–40°C, neutral pH (~7), 0.9% salt, ample nutrients
- Outside this window = "extreme"; organisms = extremophiles

### Temperature Adaptation
- Psychrophiles: ~0–20°C
- Mesophiles: ~15–45°C
- Thermophiles: ~40–80°C
- Hyperthermophiles: ~65–121°C
- E. coli: grows 21–49°C, optimum ~37°C, survives refrigeration as low as –80°C
- Thermus aquaticus: first source of high-temp DNA polymerase for PCR; optimum 70°C, max 79°C, min ~40°C; Taq polymerase optimum 72°C

### Pressure Adaptation
- Barosensitive: organisms die as pressure increases
- Barotolerant: grow up to certain pressure, die at higher
- Barophiles: require high pressure to grow, die at higher pressures
- Mariana trench: 15,945 PSI at bottom

### pH Adaptation
- Neutralophiles: pH 5–8 (include most human pathogens)
- Acidophiles: pH 0–5 (often chemoautotrophs)
- Alkaliphiles: pH 9–11 (soda lakes — Lake Magadi, Kenya — pink color from Spirulina)
- Helicobacter pylori: majority of enzymes operate best between pH 5–8.5

### Oxygen and Electron Acceptors
- Many microorganisms grow in O2; some use it as terminal electron acceptor (TEA) in ETC → aerobic respiration
- Oxygen is toxic to cells that lack enzymes to destroy Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) — anaerobes
- Strict aerobes: can only grow in oxygen
- Microaerophiles: grow only at lower O2 levels
- Strict anaerobes: die in least bit of oxygen
- Aerotolerant anaerobes: grow in oxygen, retain fermentation-based metabolism
- Facultative anaerobes: can live with or without oxygen; have both fermentative metabolism and respiration (anaerobic + aerobic)
- Oxygen-related growth zones in a standing test tube (top to bottom): strict aerobe → facultative anaerobe → aerotolerant → strict anaerobe
- Factors determining O2 use: (a) ability to use O2 as TEA, (b) ability to have enzymes that neutralize ROS

### Control of Microbial Growth
- Sterilization: killing of all living organisms
- Disinfection: killing or removal of pathogens from inanimate objects
- Antisepsis: killing or removal of pathogens from surface of living tissues
- Sanitation: reducing microbial population to safe levels

### Physical Agents That Kill Microbes
- High temperature (moist heat more effective than dry heat)
  - Boiling water (100°C): kills most cells
  - Spores/thermophiles require high pressure + temperature
  - Autoclave: 121°C at 15 psi for 20 minutes for most resistant spores
- Pasteurization:
  - LTLT: 63°C / 30 min
  - HTST: 72°C / 15 sec
  - UHT: 134°C / 2 sec (mainly for milk and cream)
- Cold temperature:
  - Slows growth; preserves strains
  - 4–8°C: refrigeration for food preservation
  - Listeria monocytogenes loves cold and can multiply
  - Long-term: glycerol at –70°C; lyophilization (freeze-drying)
- Filtration: micropore filters 0.2 µm — removes microbial cells, not viruses
- Irradiation:
  - UV light: poor penetrating power → surface sterilization only
  - Gamma rays, electron beams, X-rays: high penetrating power → irradiate foods and heat-sensitive items

### Chemical Agents
- Disinfectants/Antiseptics: Ethanol (70%), Iodine (Wescodyne, Betadine), Chlorine, Ethylene oxide (gas sterilant — for heat/moisture-sensitive materials)
- These damage proteins, lipids, and/or DNA
- Antibiotics: compounds made by one microbe that kill/inhibit others
  - Penicillin mimics peptidoglycan of bacterial cell wall → prevents cell wall formation
  - Other targets: protein synthesis, DNA replication, cell membranes
- Biological agents (Biocontrol):
  - Probiotics: microbes that restore balance to intestinal flora (Lactobacillus)
  - Phage therapy: treat infectious diseases with virus targeted to pathogen; alternative to antibiotics

### Chapter Summary
- Microbes classified by temperature: psychrophiles, mesophiles, thermophiles
- Barophiles grow at very high pressures; halophiles require high salt
- pH: acidophiles, neutralophiles, alkaliphiles
- O2: aerobes, facultative, microaerophiles, anaerobes
- Physical agents: autoclaving, pasteurization, refrigeration, filtration, irradiation
- Chemical agents: antiseptics, disinfectants
- Antibiotics selectively control microbial growth
- Biocontrol: probiotics and phage therapy
- Next: Microbial Nutrition and Metabolism

## Source 6 — `exam2-6.pdf` (27 pages) — Week 8-1 — Respiration: Electron Transport Systems

### Overview
- Electron transport systems
- Respiratory ETS
- E. coli ETS
- Mitochondrial (eukaryotic) ETS
- Proton motive force (PMF)
- ATP synthase
- Anaerobic respiration

### Where NADH and FADH2 Go
- Electrons donated to bacterial ETS → generate ATP by oxidative phosphorylation

### Oxygen and Electron Acceptors
- Aerobic respiration: use O2 as terminal electron acceptor (TEA) in ETC — common to all three domains
- Anaerobic respiration: unique to prokaryotes — use alternative electron acceptors

### Electron Transport Chain
- Changes in reduction potential allow electron flow
- Electron carriers: reduction potentials favor flow from NADH to O2
- NADH (–320 mV) → O2 (+860 mV) — low to high electron affinity

### E. coli Electron Transport System
Components:
1. NADH dehydrogenase: NADH → NAD+ + e–
2. Electron carrier: Q (quinone) → QH2 (quinol)
3. Terminal oxidase: O2 → H2O
- Inner membrane, periplasm, cytoplasm arrangement
- For each NADH oxidized: up to 8–10 H+ pumped across membrane
- Generates electrochemical gradient of protons = proton motive force (PMF)

### Mitochondrial Respiration
- Mitochondrial ETS differs from E. coli:
  1. Possesses intermediate ubiquinol:cytochrome c oxidoreductase complex for electron transfer
  2. Pumps 12 H+ per NADH (vs. E. coli: 8 H+/NADH)

### The Proton Motive Force
- H+ pumped through proton pump → generates proton motive force
- Drives conversion of ADP → ATP through ATP synthase
- Process = chemiosmotic theory (Peter Mitchell, Nobel Prize 1978)
- ATP synthase: harvests energy from PMF to synthesize ATP
  - 10 H+ pumped out per NADH → 1 NADH produces 3 ATP
  - 6 H+ pumped out per FADH2 → 1 FADH2 produces 2 ATP

### Summary of Aerobic Respiration ATP Yield
- 10 H+ out → 3 ATP made per NADH
- 6 H+ out → 2 ATP made per FADH2
- Eukaryotic cells: theoretical maximum 36 ATP from 1 glucose (2 ATP spent moving intermediates across mitochondrial membrane)

### ATP Synthesis by Chemiosmosis
- Similar in bacteria, mitochondria, and chloroplasts
- Bacteria: protons pumped from cytosolic → exoplasmic face
- Mitochondria: protons pumped from matrix → intermembrane space
- Chloroplast: protons pumped from stroma → thylakoid lumen
- All: protons flow down electrochemical gradient through ATP synthase (F0F1 complex)

### 14.4. Anaerobic Respiration
- Prokaryotes use alternative terminal electron acceptors besides O2:
  - Metals
  - Oxidized ions of nitrogen
  - Sulfur
- Occurs in environments where O2 is scarce: wetland soil, human digestive tract
- Anaerobic respiration is unique to prokaryotes
  - Some bacteria use nitrate: NO3– → NO2–
  - Some use sulfur: SO4^2– → SO3^2–
- Nitrate Reduction Test: differentiates bacteria based on ability to reduce nitrate

### Chapter Summary
- ETS: membrane-embedded proteins transferring electrons from initial electron donor to TEA
- ETS complexes generate PMF → drives ATP synthesis and other cell functions
- Electron carriers contain metal ions and/or conjugated double-bonded ring structures
- ETS includes: substrate dehydrogenase, mobile electron carrier, terminal oxidase
- F1Fo ATP synthase: 3 protons drive each cycle → 1 ATP
- Anaerobic respiration: alternative electron acceptors
- Next: Phototrophy

## Source 7 — `Exam 2-7.pdf` (24 pages) — Week 7-2 — Phototrophy (Chapter 14.2 and 14.3)

### Overview
- Phototrophy (aka photosynthesis)
- Bacteriorhodopsin
- Photosystems:
  1. Anaerobic photosystem I
  2. Anaerobic photosystem II
  3. Oxygenic Z pathway

### RECAP: Electron Transport System
- Microbes transfer energy by moving electrons
- Electrons move from reduced food molecules → energy carriers → membrane protein carriers → oxygen or oxidized minerals
- ETS drives protons across membrane → proton motive force (PMF) → powers ATP synthesis

### Phototrophy Summary
- Energy from photoexcitation of a light-absorbing pigment
- Photoexcitation → photolysis: light-driven separation of electron from donor molecule (H2O or H2S)
- Each electron transferred to ETS
- ETS generates proton potential + reduced coenzyme NADPH (instead of NADH)
- Proton potential drives ATP synthesis through F1Fo ATP synthase

### Phototrophy — Light to Chemical Energy
- Most of Earth's photosynthetic production (especially oceans) comes from microbes
- Frigid seas of Antarctica support phototrophic algae and bacteria at –2°C beneath pack ice

### Bacteriorhodopsin (Archaea)
- Most photosynthesis is based on chlorophyll
- Some prokaryotes use simpler, more ancient phototrophy: bacteriorhodopsin
  - Single-protein, light-driven proton pump
  - Found in halophilic archaea
  - Homolog: proteorhodopsin (marine proteobacteria)

### Bacterial Photosynthetic Membranes
- Purple bacteria: efficiency of photon uptake increased by thylakoid-like membranes
  - Extensive backfolding of photosynthetic membranes in oval pockets
- Purple bacteria and chloroplasts contain hollow disks with tubular interconnections (thylakoids)

### The Photolytic Electron Transport System
- All forms of photolysis share common design:
  1. Light harvesting complex
  2. Electron transport system
  3. Energy carriers

### Photosystem I (PS I) — Anaerobic
- Found in Chlorobia ("green sulfur" bacteria)
- Electron source: H2S, organic electron donor (succinate), or reduced iron (Fe2+)
- Electrons ultimately transferred to NAD+ or NADP+
- Reduced carrier (NADH or NADPH) provides reductive energy for CO2 fixation and biosynthesis
- Also generates net proton gradient → ATP synthesis
- Products: NADPH, proton gradient → ATP

### Photosystem II (PS II) — Cyclic, Anaerobic
- Found in alphaproteobacteria ("purple sulfur/nonsulfur" bacteria)
- Electron donor: bacteriochlorophyll (the pigment that absorbs light)
- Electrons transferred to ETS
- Electron acceptor: bacteriochlorophyll itself (cyclic)
- This generates ATP → called cyclic photophosphorylation
- PS II unlike PS I provides no direct way to make NADH or NADPH → uses reverse ETS to make NADH/NADPH
- Products: proton potential → ATP (cyclic)

### Oxygenic Photolysis — "Z" Pathway
- Found in cyanobacteria and chloroplasts of plants
- Includes homologs of both PS I and PS II
- Electron donor: 2H2O → releases O2 to atmosphere
- Forms 3 ATP + 2 NADPH per 2 H2O photolyzed and O2 produced
- ATP and NADPH used to fix CO2 into biomass via Calvin cycle
- Mechanism:
  - PS II: 2e– from each H2O molecule (4e– total) → quinone pool → cytochrome bf
  - Cytochrome bf: pumps 4×2H+ across membrane → transfers 4e– via plastocyanin to PS I
  - PS I: photon excites more e– → transfer to NADP+ → NADPH

### Concept Checks
- PS I electron source: H2S/organic donors; end: NAD+/NADP+; products: NADPH
- PS II electron source: bacteriochlorophyll; end: bacteriochlorophyll (cyclic); products: proton potential → ATP
- Oxygenic photosynthesis electron source: water; end: NADP+ → NADPH; products: NADPH + O2

### Chapter Summary
- Energy for photosynthesis from photoexcitation of light-absorbing pigment
- Photoexcitation → photolysis: light-driven separation of electron from donor
- Phototrophy: harnessing photoexcited electrons to power cell growth
- Simplest form: bacteriorhodopsin
- Complex forms: anaerobic PS I, PS II, oxygenic Z pathway

## Source 9 — `Tyler+week+4+slides+Spring+2026.pdf` (15 pages) — Lab #4: Gram Positive Cocci and Rods

### Lab 4 Steps
- Step 1 (Lab 3 — last week): Identify family of Gram positive unknown using primary tests
- Step 2 (Lab 3 — last week): Perform secondary tests to identify Gram positive unknown
- Step 3 (Lab 4 — this week): Interpret results from Lab 3 to identify Gram positive unknown

### Selective Media
- Allows only certain microorganisms to grow while inhibiting others
- Does NOT mean only one species or genus will grow
- Example: MSA (Mannitol Salt Agar)
  - Contains 7.5% salt → selects for salt-tolerant microorganisms (Staphylococcus)
  - Other organisms can grow (Bacillus)
  - E. coli, Enterobacter aerogenes, Proteus mirabilis usually do NOT grow

### Differential Media
- Causes bacteria to take on appearance that distinguishes them
- Color change in bacteria, agar, or both
- Example: MSA
  - Contains mannitol + pH indicator
  - Differentiates between organisms that can/cannot produce acid from mannitol fermentation
  - S. aureus: ferments mannitol (yellow colonies)
  - S. epidermidis: does NOT ferment (colorless)
- Example: SBA (Sheep Blood Agar)
  - Contains 5% sheep blood
  - Differentiates hemolytic activity:
    - β-hemolysis (complete hemolysis/clearing): S. pyogenes
    - α-hemolysis (incomplete/greening): S. pneumoniae
    - γ-hemolysis (none): E. faecalis
  - Almost all organisms will grow on SBA

### Biochemical Tests

**BHI with 6.5% NaCl:**
- Tests ability to grow in 6.5% NaCl
- Positive: growth (turbidity); Negative: no growth
- Good for Streptococcus and Enterococcus (Staphylococcus and some gram-negative also grow)

**Bile Esculin Hydrolysis:**
- Tests ability to hydrolyze esculin to esculetin
- Positive: blackening of tube; Negative: no color change or <1/2 slant black
- Good for Streptococcus and Enterococcus

**Coagulase:**
- Detects coagulase excreted by S. aureus → fibrin clot formation
- Positive: coagulation/clot; Negative: no coagulation
- Good for Staphylococcus

**Hemolysis on SBA:**
- γ (gamma)/non-hemolytic: no hemolysis or discoloration
- α (alpha): incomplete hemolysis or greening
- β (beta): complete hemolysis or clearing
- Good for Streptococcus and Bacillus

**MSA (Mannitol Salt Agar):**
- Positive: growth + acid (yellow colonies); Negative: growth + no acid or no growth
- Good for Staphylococcus and Bacillus

**Nitrate Reduction:**
- Tests ability to reduce nitrate to nitrite or ammonia or gas
- Positive: red coloration after Nitrate A + B reagents
- Positive (Complete): no color change after zinc
- Negative: red coloration after zinc
- Good for Staphylococcus, Bacillus, Enterobacteriaceae

**Phenol Red Carbohydrate Tubes:**
- Differentiates based on carbohydrate fermentation
- Positive: yellow; Negative: red
- Good for all bacteria; minimum 48-hour incubation

**Starch Hydrolysis:**
- Tests ability to hydrolyze starch
- Positive: dark agar with clear zone around streak line
- Negative: dark agar up to streak line
- Good for Bacillus; allow plate to sit after adding iodine

### 1.5 The Microbial Family Tree
- Carolus Linnaeus (1707–1778) called the microbial world "chaos" — so little was known; microbes don't fit classic species definition (group of organisms that interbreed)
- Two challenges for early taxonomists:
  1. Resolution of light microscope was too low (overcome via advances in biochemistry and microscopy)
  2. Microbial species are hard to define (working definition: >95% similarity of DNA sequence)

### Early Microbial Classification
- Ernst Haeckel (1860s): microbes are neither plant nor animal → third kind of life called Protista (Monera)
- Herbert Copeland (1966): divided Monera into two groups:
  - Eukaryotic protists (protozoa and algae) = have nucleus
  - Prokaryotic bacteria = no true nucleus
- Robert Whittaker (1969): added Fungi as fifth kingdom of eukaryotic microbes
- Whittaker five-kingdom system modified by Lynn Margulis (1960s): proposed mitochondria and chloroplasts evolved by endosymbiosis from prokaryotic cells engulfed by pre-eukaryotes
  - Endosymbiosis theory was controversial (implied species evolved from more than one ancestry)

### Evidence of Endosymbiosis
- Mitochondrial and Chloroplast DNA
- Mitochondrial and Chloroplast ribosomes
- Size and morphology

### Archaea Differ from Bacteria and Eukaryotes
- 1977: Carl Woese studied prokaryotes living in hot springs that produce methane
- Analysis of DNA coding for SS rRNA revealed these were a distinct form of life
- He and George Fox called them archaea
- Based on rDNA sequence, archaea are similar to eukarya

### Small Subunit rRNA Gene as a Molecular Clock
- Mutations build up in DNA at a reliable rate over millions of years
- rRNA gene sequence: ~1,550 bp long; composed of variable and conserved regions

### Three-Domains of Life
- Woese's discovery grouped five kingdoms into three distinct groups (Domains):
  I. Bacteria
  II. Archaea
  III. Eukarya
- In three-domain model:
  - Mitochondria derived from proteobacteria (aka Pseudomonadota)
  - Chloroplasts from cyanobacteria

### Thought Question
1. Arguments for Archaea as third domain: rDNA sequence, distinct biochemistry
2. Arguments for archaea + bacteria together as prokaryotes: both lack true nucleus

### 1.6 Cell Biology and the DNA Revolution
- More than 99% of what we know about microbes was discovered after 1900
- Two transformative instruments:
  i. Electron microscope (Ernst Ruska): revealed internal structure of cells
  ii. Ultracentrifuge (Theodor Svedberg): enabled separation of subcellular parts

### 2. DNA and RNA Revolution
- 1928: Frederick Griffith discovered transformation in bacteria
- 1944: Oswald Avery and colleagues showed transforming substance is DNA
- 1953: Rosalind Franklin used X-ray crystallography → DNA is a double helix
- James Watson, Francis Crick, and Maurice Wilkins discovered:
  - Complementary bases
  - Antiparallel nature of DNA
- DNA work first done in bacteria and bacteriophages
- Discovery of heat-stable DNA polymerase from Thermus aquaticus (Yellowstone hot spring)
- Kary Mullis: amplified DNA via PCR

### Biotechnology and Bioremediation
- Discovery of antibiotics (penicillin, etc.)
- Vaccination
- Industrial microbiology: use bacteria to clone and produce insulin
- Bioremediation: clean human wastes and pollutants (e.g., 2010 Gulf oil spill — 200 million gallons)

### Chapter Summary
- Eukaryotic organelles (mitochondria, chloroplasts) evolved by endosymbiosis from prokaryotes
- Three domains: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya
- Advances in microscopy and biochemistry revealed cell structure/function
- DNA revolutionized classification and study of microorganisms

## Source 11 — `Week+10-2.pdf` (36 pages) — Week 11-1: Bacterial DNA Replication & PCR

### 7.3 DNA Replication
- Microbial DNA needs to replicate accurately and quickly
- E. coli: doubling time ~20 minutes, but takes ~40 minutes to copy its chromosome
- Solution: before a round of replication is over, bacterium has already initiated the next one (or two)

### Overview of Bacterial DNA Replication
- Semiconservative: daughter cell receives one parental + one newly synthesized strand
- Bidirectional: starts at fixed origin, progresses in opposite directions
- DNA grows only on the 3' end (DNA polymerase can only add nucleotides to the 3' end)

### The Leading and Lagging Strand Problem
- Polymerases synthesize DNA only in 5'→3' direction
- Two backbones are antiparallel → problem: how are both strands synthesized simultaneously?
- Answer (Tsuneko and Reiji Okazaki):
  - Leading strand: synthesized continuously
  - Lagging strand: synthesized discontinuously → creates Okazaki fragments (later fused)

### 8 Key Enzymes in DNA Replication
1. DnaA: initiator protein (starts at oriC)
2. DnaB (helicase): unwinds double helix
3. DNA primase: synthesis of RNA primer
4. DNA Pol III: major replication enzyme; adds nucleotides to growing strand
5. DNA gyrase: relieves DNA supercoiling
6. DNA Pol I: replaces RNA primer with DNA
7. DNA ligase: joins Okazaki fragments
8. Topoisomerase IV: separates concatenated chromosomes

### Replication in Bacteria
- Begins at origin (oriC)
- Replicates bidirectionally
- New strand always synthesized 5'→3'
- Leading strand: continuous
- Lagging strand: discontinuous (Okazaki fragments)

### Terminating Replication
- Up to ten terminator sequences (ter) on E. coli chromosome
- Tus (terminus utilization substance) protein binds ter sequences → acts as counter-helicase
- Ringed catenanes formed at completion → separated by topoisomerase IV and XerCD proteins

### When Does Replication Begin?
- Controlled by:
  1. SeqA
  2. DnaA

### Plasmids
- Two kinds of extragenomic DNA: horizontally transferred plasmids + bacteriophage genomes
- Plasmids encode functions contributing to cell physiology (e.g., antibiotic resistance)
- Much smaller than chromosomes
- Found in archaea, bacteria, and eukaryotic microbes
- Usually circular; need host proteins to replicate
- Replicate through Rolling Circle mechanism

### DNA Replication in Eukaryotes
- Genomes are linear, larger, and more complex
- DNA replication starts from multiple origins of replication
- DNA polymerase epsilon (Pol ε): copies leading strand
- DNA polymerase delta (Pol δ): copies lagging strand
- Telomeres at ends of chromosomes; telomerase extends them (contains RNA template TERC)

### Comparison: Bacterial vs. Eukaryotic Replication
- Archaea: single circular chromosome; single origin (some multiple); DNA pol B and Fen1; Pol B and Pol D

### PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)
- Produces >1 million-fold copy of target DNA within a few hours
- Source of Taq polymerase: Thermus aquaticus (tolerates high temperature)
- PCR uses heat (not helicase) to denature/separate strands
- No Okazaki fragments in PCR
- Helicase cannot replace thermal cycler
- DNA synthesized in PCR looks like human DNA (determined by template, not polymerase)

### Chapter Summary
- DNA replication: three phases — Initiation (oriC), Elongation (replication forks), Termination (ter)
- Each phase requires different proteins
- Plasmids are autonomously replicating extragenomosomal DNA elements → benefit host under certain conditions
- PCR copies organism DNA outside the cell

## Source 12 — `Week+11-1+Bacterial+Transcription,+Regulation+and+RNA.pdf` (28 pages) — Week 11: Lecture 1 — Bacterial Transcription, RNA, and Prokaryotic Gene Regulation

### Chapter Overview
- Transcription: DNA → RNA
- RNA polymerases and sigma factors
- Genetic code, ribosomes, tRNAs
- Process of Transcription
- Prokaryotic Gene Regulation
- Antibiotics that affect bacterial transcription

### The Central Dogma
- DNA template → RNA copy (transcription)
- RNA → protein (translation)

### RNA Structure
- Usually single-stranded
- RNA-RNA double strand = hairpins
- Contains ribose sugar (not deoxyribose)
- Uracil replaces thymine

### Classes of RNA
- mRNA (messenger RNA): encodes proteins
- rRNA (ribosomal RNA): integral part of ribosomes
- tRNA (transfer RNA): shuttles amino acids
- sRNA (small RNA): regulates transcription or translation
- tmRNA: frees ribosomes stuck on damaged mRNA
- Catalytic RNA: carries out enzymatic reactions

### Bacterial Genome Organization
- A gene is the basic physical and functional unit of heredity; can operate independently or in tandem (operon)
- Monocistronic operon: one promoter → one gene → one RNA transcript
- Polycistronic operon: one promoter → multiple genes → all transcribed together
- Operator: regulatory region where repressors bind

### Transcription: Big Picture
- RNA polymerase (the enzyme that does the work) reads the template strand of DNA
- RNA is made: only difference from DNA is the ribose/uracil substitutions

### Transcription
- Synthesis of RNA strand from DNA template by RNA polymerase (RNA pol)
- Begins when RNA pol binds to promoter on DNA
- Promoter: sequence upstream of the transcription start site (+1)

### RNA Polymerases and Sigma Factor
- RNA pol: complex enzyme that makes RNA copies (transcripts) of DNA template strand
- In bacteria, RNA pol is composed of:
  - Sigma factor (σ): required for the initiation phase; recognizes promoter
  - Core polymerase (α2, β, β′): required for the elongation phase
- Every cell has a "housekeeping" sigma factor
- E. coli: sigma-70 (σ70) — recognizes consensus sequences at –10 and –35 positions upstream of +1
- Single bacterial species can make several different sigma factors
- All sigma factors compete for the same core RNA polymerase

### Transcription: Three Phases
1. Initiation: RNA pol (with sigma factor) binds to promoter → melting of helix → synthesis of first nucleotide
2. Elongation: RNA chain extended; DNA unwinds ahead (17-bp transcription bubble); RNA pol synthesizes RNA at ~45 bases/sec; positive supercoils ahead removed by topoisomerases
3. Termination: RNA pol detaches from DNA after transcript is made

### Termination Signals (Two Types)
1. Rho-dependent: relies on Rho protein; contact between Rho and RNA pol causes termination
2. Rho-independent: relies on inverted repeat sequence on DNA template → forms stem-loop structure

### Prokaryotic Gene Regulation
- Structural genes of related function organized together as operons
- Regulatory region includes promoter and operator
- If repressor binds operator → structural genes NOT transcribed
- If activator binds regulatory region → genes are transcribed

### Repressible Operon: trp operon
- Genes are normally ON
- Tryptophan (trp) accumulates in cell → binds trp repressor → changes shape → repressor binds trp operator → RNA synthesis blocked (genes turn OFF)
- Key concept: repressible operon = genes normally ON, product turns them OFF

### Inducible Operon: lac operon
- Genes are normally OFF
- Encodes 3 genes for hydrolysis of lactose into glucose and galactose
- In absence of lactose: lac repressor bound to operator → physically prevents RNA pol from transcribing
- When lactose present: lactose binds repressor → changes shape → repressor falls off operator → genes turn ON
- Maximal activation requires: (1) glucose absent (allows CAP activator to bind) AND (2) lactose present (releases Lac repressor)
- Low glucose + no lactose: no transcription; low glucose + lactose: high transcription

### Eukaryotic Transcription Regulation
- Activator protein bound to distant enhancer → attracts RNA pol and general transcription factors to promoter
- Eukaryotic activators also recruit chromatin-modifying proteins

### Antibiotics That Affect Transcription
- Rifamycin B (from Amycolatopsis mediterranei): selectively binds to bacterial RNA pol → inhibits transcription (bacterial RNA pol is different from eukaryotic → selective)
- Actinomycin D (actinomycete): non-selectively binds to DNA → inhibits transcription; used in cancer treatment but harms humans → NOT used as antibiotic

### Chapter Summary
- Transcription: DNA template → RNA transcript, carried out by RNA polymerase
- Sigma factor recognizes promoter; core polymerase elongates RNA until termination signal
- Many classes of RNA; mRNA, rRNA, and tRNA directly involved in protein synthesis
- Prokaryotes regulate transcription by multiple ways:
  - Repressible genes: regulated by amount of product in cell (trp operon)
  - Inducible genes: regulated by amount of substrate in cell (lac operon)

## Source 13 — `Week+11-2+Bacterial+Translation+and+Proteins.pdf` (34 pages) — Week 11: Lecture 2 — Bacterial RNA Translation to Proteins

### Chapter Overview
- Translation: mRNA → amino acid sequence
- Genetic code, ribosomes, tRNAs
- Process of Translation (Initiation, Elongation, Termination)
- Coupling of Bacterial Transcription and Translation
- Post-translational modifications
- Antibiotics that target bacterial translation

### Translation of RNA to Protein
- mRNA message arranged in triplets of nucleotides (codons) → represent individual amino acids
- Ribosomes: machines that read mRNA language and translate it into protein (via genetic code)
- tRNA decoder molecules: convert language of RNA into protein language

### The Genetic Code
- 64 possible codons (4^3)
- 61 specify amino acids (include start codons)
- 3 are stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA
- Code is degenerate/redundant: multiple codons can encode the same amino acid
  - Ex: GCU, GCC, GCA, GCG all code for Alanine

### tRNA
- Clover leaf shape
- Two functional regions:
  - Anticodon: hydrogen bonds with mRNA codon specifying an amino acid
  - 3′ (acceptor) end: binds the amino acid
- Contains many unusual, modified bases
- Anticodon consists of three nucleotides; binds mRNA codon in antiparallel fashion
- tRNA is "charged" with amino acid attached to 3' end
- Cell has 20 types of charged tRNA — one for each amino acid

### The Ribosome
- Two subunits, each containing rRNA and proteins
- Prokaryotes: 30S and 50S subunits → combine to form 70S ribosome
  - 30S subunit contains 16S rRNA (1,540 nucleotides in E. coli)
  - 5'-GAUCACCUCCUUA-3' = 3' end of 16S rRNA (binds Shine-Dalgarno)
- *S = Svedberg unit (rate of sedimentation in centrifugation, not size)
- Carl Woese used small subunit rDNA to construct tree of life
- 16S rRNA gene sequence serves as molecular clock for bacterial species

### Finding the Right Reading Frame
- Every mRNA has three potential reading frames
- Ribosome finds correct frame via Shine-Dalgarno sequence:
  - Purine-rich consensus 5'-AGGAGGU-3'
  - Located 4–8 bases upstream of start codon (E. coli)
  - Complementary to sequence at 3' end of 16S rRNA subunit

### Three Stages of Protein Synthesis

**1. Initiation:**
- Two ribosomal subunits come together
- First amino acid (Met/fMet) placed at P site
- Start codon: AUG
- Requires protein factors and GTP

**2. Elongation:**
- Second codon pairs with tRNA carrying second amino acid at A site
- First amino acid joins to second by peptide bond (attached to tRNA in P site)
- Ribosome moves along mRNA → second tRNA now in P site → next codon brought to A site → first tRNA in E site
- First tRNA released from E site
- New amino acids continuously added to polypeptide

**3. Termination:**
- Ribosome reaches stop codon → polypeptide released
- Last tRNA released
- Ribosome comes apart
- Released polypeptide forms new protein

### Bacterial Transcription and Translation are Coupled
- Different ribosomes bind simultaneously to start of each cistron within polycistronic mRNA
- Before RNA pol even finishes making mRNA, ribosomes bind to 5' end and begin translating = coupled transcription and translation
- In eukaryotic cells: coupling of transcription and translation is IMPOSSIBLE (transcription in nucleus, translation in cytoplasm)

### Post-Translational Modifications
1. After translation: proteins fold to their functional structure
2. Incorrectly made or folded proteins get degraded
3. Others secreted via protein traffic control:
   - Transmembrane proteins
   - Protein export to periplasm
   - Secretion of proteins outside cell

### Protein Traffic Control / Secretion
- Proteins for bacterial cell membrane/envelope regions require special export systems
- Proteins for cell membrane tagged with hydrophobic N-terminal signal sequences (15–30 amino acids)
  - Bound by Signal Recognition Particle (SRP)
  - Undergo co-translational or post-translational export
- Transmembrane proteins: inserted directly OR via Sec system
- Periplasmic proteins: delivered via SecA-dependent general secretion pathway
  - SecB: keeps protein unfolded
  - SecA: moves proteins to SecYEG
  - SecYEG: exports proteins across cell membranes
- Gram-negative bacteria: need to export proteins completely outside cell
  - Seven secretion systems: Type I–VII
  - Type I: export proteins to outside environment

### Antibiotics That Affect Translation
- Streptomycin: inhibits 70S ribosome formation
- Tetracycline: inhibits translation by binding to 30S subunit
- Chloramphenicol and Erythromycin: bind to 23S rRNA of the 50S ribosomal subunit

### Chapter Summary
- Ribosomes composed of two subunits
- Translation: Initiation → Elongation → Termination
- Proteins may be modified and folded
- Cells possess protein-degrading machines
- Proteins can be secreted via different processes
- Bacterial translation is a very good target for different antibiotics
