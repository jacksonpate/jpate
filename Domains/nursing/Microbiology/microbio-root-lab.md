## Source 1 — 3201-2.pdf: Brightfield Microscopy & Gram Stain (Annotated Notes)

### Microscopy Key Terms

- **Parfocal** — stays in focus when switching objectives
- **Numerical aperture** — light-gathering ability of the lens
- **Working distance** — space between objective and slide

### Microscope Types

| Type | Stain Required? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Brightfield | Yes | Standard lab scope; uses transmitted/reflected light |
| Phase contrast | No | Converts refractive index differences into contrast; shows morphology + some internal structures |
| Darkfield | No | Bright specimen on dark background; shows morphology + external structures only |
| Fluorescent | Yes (fluorophores) | High-intensity illumination; excites fluorophores; emission at longer wavelength |

### Brightfield Microscope Components

- **Framework** — arm + base
- **Light source** — halogen or LED; knurled wheel adjusts intensity
- **Stage** — horizontal platform with slide holder and adjustment knobs
- **Lens system**: oculars (10X each), objectives (10X, 40X, 100X oil), condenser (under stage; doesn't affect magnification; diaphragm controls light)
- **Focusing knobs** — coarse (outer, use with 10X only); fine (inner, use with 40X and 100X)
- **Immersion oil** — same refractive index as glass; fills gap at 100X only to reduce light refraction and increase resolution. NEVER use with 10X or 40X
- **Total magnification** = ocular power x objective power (e.g., 10X x 100X = 1000X)

### Why Stain Bacteria

Bacteria are ~80% water — essentially transparent under brightfield without stain. Staining reveals:
- Cell morphology
- Cell arrangement
- Internal storage materials

### Smear Preparation

- Always from solid media (plate or slant)
- Mix bacteria with sterile water on a slide
- Most common error: too many bacteria on the slide

### Gram Stain — History

Named after Hans Christian Gram (Danish physician, 1884). Original goal: differentiate bacteria from eukaryotic nuclei. Noticed some bacteria retained crystal violet; others did not.

### Gram Stain — Mechanism

- **Deciding factor**: amount of peptidoglycan in cell wall
- **Gram positive** → thick peptidoglycan → retains crystal violet → **purple**
- **Gram negative** → thin peptidoglycan → loses crystal violet after decolorization → counterstain with safranin → **pink/red**
- Cultures must be **18-24 hours old** for accurate results
- Gram stain = **differential stain**

### Gram Stain — Color Sequence

1. Crystal violet → both G+ and G- look purple
2. Iodine (mordant) → both still purple
3. Decolorizer → G- loses purple (goes colorless/black briefly)
4. Safranin (counterstain) → G- goes pink; G+ stays purple

### Gram Stain — Cell Morphologies

| Type | Color | Shape | Arrangement Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| G+ cocci | Purple | Spherical | Pairs (Enterococcus), tetrads (Micrococcus), chains (Streptococcus), clusters (Staphylococcus) |
| G- cocci | Pink | Spherical/bean | Pairs or tetrads (Moraxella) |
| G+ rods | Purple | Rod | Singles (B. subtilis), chains (B. cereus) |
| G- straight rods | Pink | Rod | Singles (Pseudomonas) |
| G- curved/spiral rods | Pink | Curved/spiral | Curved singles (Vibrio), spiral singles (Campylobacter) |

## Source 3 — Primary+Tests lab.pdf: Primary & Biochemical Tests

### Catalase Test

**Purpose:** Differentiates bacteria by ability to produce catalase (breaks H₂O₂ → H₂O + O₂)

**Oxygen categories:**
- **Strict aerobes** — require O₂; use aerobic respiration
- **Facultative anaerobes** — grow with or without O₂; switch between aerobic respiration and fermentation
- **Aerotolerant anaerobes** — no O₂ for energy; fermentation only; tolerate O₂; lack catalase but have peroxidases (e.g., NADH peroxidase)
- **Strict anaerobes** — killed by O₂; lack catalase and peroxidases

**Key:** Strict aerobes + facultative anaerobes → catalase positive
Aerotolerant anaerobes → catalase negative (use peroxidases instead)
Strict anaerobes → catalase negative (most sensitive to O₂)

**Also relevant:** Superoxide dismutase (SOD) converts superoxide radicals → H₂O₂ + O₂. Most aerobes and aerotolerant anaerobes have SOD.

**Procedure:** Add 3% H₂O₂ to bacteria on dry slide. Rapid bubbling = positive.
**Example:** Staphylococcus = catalase +; Streptococcus/Enterococcus = catalase -

### Oxidase Test

**Purpose:** Detects cytochrome C oxidase (part of electron transport chain where O₂ is terminal electron acceptor)

**Reagent:** N,N,N',N'-tetramethyl-p-phenylenediamine (turns yellow → blue when electrons transfer from reduced cytochrome C)

**Critical:** Read within **30 seconds**. Reagent auto-oxidizes and gives false positives over time.

**Procedure:** Apply 2-3 drops oxidase reagent to swab with inoculum; observe color change ≤30 seconds.

### KOH Test

**Purpose:** Verifies Gram stain result. 3% KOH lyses Gram negative cell walls (thin peptidoglycan), releasing DNA → viscous strings.

- G- cells → lyse → DNA strings form → positive (strings visible)
- G+ cells → thick peptidoglycan → no lysis → no strings → negative

**Note:** KOH is NOT a replacement for Gram stain — only verifies it. Old G+ cultures may give false G- results due to cell wall degradation.

### Selective and Differential Media

- **Selective medium** — allows only certain organisms to grow (uses antibiotics, dyes, inhibitors). Example: MSA uses 7.5% NaCl to select for Staphylococcus
- **Differential medium** — differentiates organisms by visible change. Example: MSA — S. aureus ferments mannitol → yellow; non-fermenters → red stays. SBA — hemolysis patterns (alpha, beta, gamma)

**Hemolysis types:**
- **Beta** — complete lysis, clear zone
- **Alpha** — incomplete lysis, greenish discoloration
- **Gamma** — no hemolysis

### Phenol Red Carbohydrate Tubes

Detect carbohydrate fermentation via pH change. Phenol red: red at alkaline/neutral → **yellow at acidic** (acid produced from fermentation). Carbohydrates used: mannitol, lactose, sucrose, arabinose, glucose, trehalose, etc.

### Starch Hydrolysis

Bacteria secreting amylase break starch → maltose + glucose + dextrins. After incubation, flood plate with Gram's iodine. Iodine = black/brown with starch; **clear zone around growth = positive** (starch degraded).

### Bile Esculin Hydrolysis

Selective + differential. Bile salts inhibit most G+ except Enterococcus and group D Streptococcus. If organism hydrolyzes esculin → esculetin + dextrose; esculetin + ferric citrate → **dark brown/black complex**.
**Positive = more than half the slant is dark.**

### Coagulase Test

**Best definitive test for S. aureus** (97% are coagulase positive). Inoculate rabbit plasma; incubate 37°C/24hrs. Positive = clot (fibrin coagulation). Valid ONLY for G+ staphylococcus-like bacteria.

### Biochemical Tests Summary Table

| Test | Positive Result | Negative Result |
|---|---|---|
| Catalase | Bubbles | No bubbles |
| Oxidase | Blue/purple within 30 sec | No color change |
| KOH | Strings (G-) | No strings (G+) |
| Coagulase | Clot in plasma | No clot |
| Hemolysis SBA | Alpha/Beta clearing or greening | Gamma = no change |
| MSA | Yellow colonies (G+/A+) | Colorless or no growth |
| Bile Esculin | >½ slant black | No/minimal color change |
| Nitrate Reduction | Red after A+B (incomplete); no red after zinc (complete) | Red after zinc = negative |
| Phenol Red Carb | Yellow (fermentation) | Red (no fermentation) |
| Starch Hydrolysis | Clear zone after iodine | Dark up to streak |
| BHI 6.5% NaCl | Turbidity (growth) | Clear (no growth) |

### Biochemical Test Procedures (Labs 6/7)

**Catalase** — 3-5 colonies on dry slide + 1 drop H₂O₂. Positive = bubbles.

**Citrate** — Zig-zag slant streak. 24hr/37°C loose cap. Positive = intense blue color (ammonia → alkaline → bromothymol blue shifts green → blue). Negative = no growth, stays green.

**EMB (Eosin Methylene Blue)** — Streak for isolation. 24hr/37°C.
- G+/A++ = metallic green sheen (vigorous lactose fermenter; primarily E. coli)
- G+/A+ = purple (moderate fermenter)
- G+/A- = pink/colorless (no fermentation)

**KOH** — Loopful bacteria + 1 drop 3% KOH; mix 5-10 sec; lift loop slowly. Strings = G-.

**Methyl Red (MR)** — Inoculate MRVP broth. Incubate 3-5 days/37°C. Withdraw 1 mL → add 3-4 drops methyl red. Red (immediate) = positive (stable acid from glucose fermentation).

**Nitrate Reduction** — Inoculate nitrate broth. 24-48hr/37°C. Add reagent A + B → red = reduced to nitrite (positive incomplete). No color → add zinc → red = negative (failed to reduce). No red after zinc = positive complete (reduced to ammonia/N₂ gas).

**O/F Glucose** — Stab inoculate 2 tubes. One open (oxidative), one sealed with mineral oil (fermentative). 48hr at appropriate temp.
- Yellow in open tube only = oxidative
- Yellow in both = fermentative
- Green in both = neither

**Oxidase** — Swab inoculum + 2-3 drops oxidase reagent. Blue/purple ≤30 sec = positive.

**Phenylalanine** — Zig-zag slant streak. 24hr/37°C. Add 5-10 drops ferric chloride; shake. Wait 1-5 min. Green = positive (phenylpyruvic acid produced). Yellow = negative.

**SIM (H₂S, Indole, Motility)** — Stab inoculate 2/3 down tube. 48hr/37°C loose cap.
- H₂S: blackening along stab line = positive
- Indole: add 5 drops Kovac's reagent → red at top = positive
- Motility: diffuse/turbid growth outward from stab = positive; growth only along stab = negative

**Starch Hydrolysis** — One streak across starch agar plate. 24hr/37°C. Flood with Gram's iodine. Clear zone around streak = positive.

**Urea** — Inoculate urea broth. 24hr/37°C loose cap. Intense pink-red = positive (urease → ammonia → alkaline → phenol red shifts).

**VP (Voges-Proskauer)** — Inoculate MRVP broth. 3-5 days/37°C. Withdraw 1 mL → add 5 drops VP-A (alpha-naphthol) + 5 drops VP-B (40% KOH); shake. 30 min room temp. Red = positive (2,3-butanediol produced from glucose fermentation).

### Experiment 3 — Kirby-Bauer Antimicrobic Sensitivity Testing

**Materials:** TSA plate, sterile swab, broth cultures (S. aureus or E. coli), 4 chosen antibiotic disks

**Procedure:**
1. Label plate with organism + 4 antibiotics (one per quadrant)

### Full Biochemical Test Procedures (Unknown Packet Version)

**Citrate** — Zig-zag slant streak. 24hr/37°C. Positive = intense blue + growth.

**Coagulase** — Loopful into rabbit plasma tube. 24hr/37°C. Positive = clot.

**EMB** — Streak for isolation. 24hr/37°C. Metallic green = vigorous lactose fermenter. Purple = moderate. Pink/colorless = no fermentation.

**Growth at 5°C** — Streak TSA; incubate 5°C walk-in cooler 3-5 days. Positive = growth in Q3/Q4.

**Growth at 41°C** — Streak TSA; incubate 41°C for ONLY 24 hours. Positive = growth in Q3/Q4.

**Growth at 45°C** — Streak SBA; incubate 45°C for ONLY 24 hours. Positive = growth in Q3/Q4.

**Hemolysis on SBA** — Streak SBA for isolation. ONLY 24hr/37°C. Hold up to light. Alpha = green. Beta = clear. Gamma = no change.

**KOH** — Loopful bacteria + 1 drop KOH; mix 5-10 sec; lift loop. Strings = G-.

**Methyl Red** — Inoculate MRVP. 3 days/37°C. Withdraw 1 mL + 5 drops methyl red. Red (immediate) = positive.

**MSA** — Streak for isolation. 24hr/37°C. Yellow = G+/A+. Colorless = G+/A-.

**Nitrate Reduction** — Inoculate nitrate broth. 48hr/37°C. Add 3-5 drops reagent A + B. Red after A+B = incomplete positive. No color → add zinc. Red after zinc = negative. No red after zinc = complete positive.

## Source 11 — Dilution problem.pdf: Working Dilution Problems

### Purpose

Determine microbial counts (CFUs) for liquid or solid specimens via serial dilutions + pour plates or spread plates. 30-300 colonies per plate = valid countable range.

### Standard Formula

```
CFU/mL = (colony count) × (dilution factor) / (amount plated)
```

Or equivalently:
```
CFU/mL = colony count on plate × (1 / total dilution) ÷ amount plated
```

### Steps

**Step 1 — Identify the countable plate**
- Find plate with 30-300 colonies (preferably)
- >300 or <30 = high error margin
- Duplicate plates from same dilution: average the counts

**Step 2 — Calculate total dilution**

For each tube:
```
dilution of tube = amount of sample transferred / (amount transferred + amount of diluent in tube)
```

Total dilution = multiply each individual dilution together:
- 1 mL into 9 mL = 1/10
- After 3 tubes: 1/10 × 1/10 × 1/10 = 1/10³ = 10⁻³

Dilution factor = 1/dilution (e.g., if total dilution = 1/10³, dilution factor = 10³)

**Step 3 — Determine amount plated**
Value given in problem or procedure (e.g., 1 mL or 0.1 mL).

### Example

- Countable plate: 71 colonies
- Total dilution from 3rd tube: 1/10³
- Amount plated: 1 mL

```
CFU/mL = 71 × 10³ / 1 = 7.1 × 10⁴ = 71,000/mL
```

### Scientific Notation Rules

For scientific notation (e.g., 5.67 × 10⁵):
1. Coefficient must be ≥1 and <10
2. Base must be 10
3. Exponent = number of decimal places moved
4. Negative exponent = decimal moves left (number < 1)

## Source 13 — Environmentall+Effects+I.pdf: Environmental Effects I

### Temperature and Bacterial Growth

| Category | Optimal Temp | Habitat |
|---|---|---|
| Psychrophiles | -5°C to 20°C | Arctic/Antarctic waters |
| Mesophiles | 20°C to 50°C | Most bacteria; pathogens grow 35-40°C |
| Thermophiles | 50°C to 80°C | Hot soils, compost piles >60-65°C |
| Hyperthermophiles | >80°C | Deep-sea thermal vents, volcanic hot springs |

**Psychrotrophs:** Mesophiles that can grow at 4°C (refrigerator temp). Examples: Proteus, Pseudomonas, Leuconostoc, Listeria monocytogenes, Campylobacter.

**Why temperature matters for bacteria:**
- Enzymes have minimum, optimal, maximum activity temperatures
- Above maximum → enzymes denature
- Below minimum → chemical activity slows
- Temperature affects membrane fluidity → nutrient transport
- High temp → membrane lipid destruction → cell death
- Ribosomes cease to function at excessive temps

**Serratia marcescens** produces red pigment **prodigiosin** — antibiotic; tested in clinical trials for pancreatic cancer treatment. Prodigiosin only produced within a specific temperature range.

### Oxygen Requirements

| Category | Oxygen Need | Energy Source |
|---|---|---|
| Obligate aerobe | Must have O₂ | Aerobic respiration (O₂ = terminal e⁻ acceptor) |
| Microaerophile | Prefers 2-10% O₂ | Respiration at low O₂ |
| Facultative anaerobe | Works with or without | Aerobic respiration OR fermentation |
| Aerotolerant anaerobe | Tolerates O₂, doesn't use it | Fermentation only |
| Obligate anaerobe | Killed by O₂ | Fermentation (uses nitrates/sulfates as e⁻ acceptors) |

**Examples:**
- Obligate aerobe: Pseudomonas, Micrococcus, many Bacillus
- Microaerophile: Helicobacter pylori, Campylobacter
- Facultative anaerobe: E. coli, Salmonella
- Aerotolerant anaerobe: Streptococcus (cheese/yogurt), S. pyogenes
- Obligate anaerobe: Clostridium

**Toxic oxygen defense:**
- **Catalase** → H₂O₂ → H₂O + O₂
- **Peroxidase** → H₂O₂ degradation
- **Superoxide dismutase (SOD)** → O₂⁻ → H₂O₂ + O₂ (then catalase acts)
- Most obligate anaerobes lack catalase AND SOD

### Anaerobic Media

**FTM (Fluid Thioglycollate Medium):**
- Supports aerobic AND anaerobic growth
- Contains glucose, cystine, sodium thioglycollate (reduces oxidation potential)
- Contains resazurin dye — turns pink in presence of oxygen (pink at top, colorless below)
- Small agar amount → localizes organisms, favors anaerobic bottom

**Brewer's Anaerobic Agar:**
- Solid medium for anaerobes
- Contains thioglycollate (reducing agent) + resazurin (oxygen indicator)
- Used with anaerobic jars + GasPak (produces H₂ → combines with O₂ → water)
- Anaerobic indicator strip: colorless = anaerobic; blue = O₂ present

### Antimicrobic Sensitivity Testing: Kirby-Bauer Method

**Key History:**
- Fleming (1929): Penicillium inhibits S. aureus → discovery of penicillin
- Florey, Heatley, Chain: purified penicillin for clinical use
- Current problem: antibiotic-resistant strains (MRSA, resistant Pseudomonas, resistant TB, resistant Enterococcus, resistant N. gonorrhoeae)
- MRSA → requires vancomycin (VRSA strains also emerging)
- 2002 CDC estimate: 1.7 million HAIs in U.S.

**Definitions:**
- **Antimicrobials** — kill or inhibit microorganisms
- **Antibiotics** — naturally produced by microorganisms (low MW); examples: penicillin, streptomycin
- **Semi-synthetics** — chemically altered natural antibiotics
- **Synthetics** — chemically synthesized in lab (e.g., sulfa drugs)
- **Bacteriostatic** — inhibits growth; reversible if agent removed
- **Bactericidal** — kills bacteria

**Antiseptics vs. Disinfectants:**
- **Antiseptics** — safe for living tissue (alcohol, betadine); inhibit or kill; do NOT destroy endospores
- **Disinfectants** — applied to inanimate objects; harsher; some are sterilants/sporocides (kill endospores; e.g., ethylene oxide for heat-sensitive plastics)
- **Sanitizers** — reduce microbial numbers to safe level but don't eliminate all

**Antibiotic Modes of Action (Table 1 in original):**
- Cell wall synthesis: penicillin
- DNA/RNA synthesis: ciprofloxacin, rifampin
- Protein synthesis (70S ribosomes): tetracyclines, streptomycin (broad spectrum — all bacteria have 70S ribosomes)
- Vitamin (folic acid) synthesis: sulfa drugs (narrow spectrum)
- G- outer membrane acts as permeability barrier → restricts some antibiotic entry

**Kirby-Bauer Procedure:**
1. Uniform lawn streak standardized inoculum on Mueller-Hinton medium
2. Place antibiotic disks on surface
3. Antibiotic diffuses → concentration gradient
4. 16-18hr incubation
5. Measure zone of inhibition (mm) — area of no growth around disk
6. Compare to reference table (ATCC values) → designate: Resistant / Intermediate / Sensitive

**Antiseptic/Disinfectant Testing (Modified Kirby-Bauer):**
- Streak test organism on suitable medium
- Dip filter paper disks in antiseptic/disinfectant; place on inoculated plate
- Incubate 48 hours
- Measure zone of inhibition to compare relative efficacy
