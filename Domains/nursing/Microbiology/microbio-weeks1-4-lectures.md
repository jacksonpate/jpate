### Biosafety Levels

**BSL 1** — Microorganisms not known to cause disease in healthy adults.
- Ex. *Bacillus subtilis*
- We DO work with these.

**BSL 2** — Indigenous microorganisms that can lead to diseases of varying severity in healthy adults.
- Ex. *Staphylococcus aureus*
- We DO work with these.

**BSL 3** — Indigenous or exotic microorganisms that cause serious or potentially lethal disease through respiratory transmission.
- Ex. *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*
- We NEVER work with these.

**BSL 4** — Microorganisms that are dangerous and exotic with high risk of aerosol transmitted infections. Rarely have treatments or vaccines. Diseases frequently fatal.
- Ex. Ebola virus
- We NEVER work with these.

### Ubiquity of Microorganisms

- Microbes are found in ALL environments: soil, water, plants, animals
- Majority found in soil and oceans
- Some found 11–31 miles up in atmosphere
- Found in deep ice core samples from glaciers
- Found in a lake half a mile under a glacier in Antarctica
- **Archaea** = microbes that live in extreme environments; found in deep ocean vents

---

### Contamination

**Definition:** Contamination is the undesired introduction of impurities like microbes into or onto an item (countertop, food, water, skin, etc.)

- In the lab, contamination leads to mixed cultures → erroneous test results
- Example: Correct Phenol Red Lactose reaction (negative/red) for *Proteus mirabilis* becomes incorrect (positive/yellow) if contaminated with *E. coli*

**How contamination of pure cultures can occur:**
- Failure to properly sterilize solid (agar) or liquid (broth) media
- Failure to properly sterilize inoculating loop or needle
- Touching a non-sterile object to agar surface
- Inserting a non-sterile object into broth
- Leaving a Petri plate open to air
- Failure to properly flame the mouth of a tube
- Skin cells or hair falling on agar or in broth
- Breathing on agar or in broth
- We cannot eliminate contamination — but we can REDUCE it

---

### Aseptic Technique

**Definition:** A set of specific practices and procedures performed under carefully controlled conditions with the goal of minimizing contamination by microbes.

Critical to master — without it, results will be contaminated. Especially important for Unknown Lab.

**Includes:**
- Hair pulled back
- Gloves
- Lab coat
- Disinfection of your work area
- Sterilization of inoculating loops
- Culture tube flaming and inoculation
- Proper Petri dish inoculation and handling of media
- Final disinfection of your work area

**Flaming the loop:** Heat the loop in a Bunsen burner flame until red-hot before and after use.

**Flaming culture tubes:**
- While loop is cooling (~15 seconds), sterilize the mouth of your culture tube
- Done BEFORE and AFTER inserting the loop
- Move the mouth of the tube (cap removed) through the flame twice
- Do not overheat the glass (risk of breaking)

**Practicing aseptic technique:**
- Plate to slant: aseptically transfer *S. aureus* from plate to sterile slant using zigzag motion while withdrawing loop; incubate at 37°C
- Plate to broth: transfer from plate to sterile broth tube; twist loop back and forth to disperse bacteria; incubate at 37°C

---

### Plate Setup & Labeling

**Label the AGAR SIDE (NOT the lid) with:**
- First initial + full last name
- Lab day/time (e.g., M 13)
- Incubation temperature
- Row # and Station # (e.g., R1 S1)
- Sample type (skin or surface)

**Streak plate practice:**
- Divide plate into 4 quadrants
- Use 2 loopfuls of bacteria
- Flame loop between quadrant pulls

**Before leaving lab:**
1. Dispose of microscope slides in sharps container
2. Empty stain waste bin into drum in back of lab
3. Put inoculated plates at front for incubation
4. Put away lab coat and supplies in shared drawer
5. Lysol benchtop
6. Wash hands

#### Bacterial Smear Prep

**Procedure (performed once per organism):**
1. Place 2 slides on slide rest
2. On slide 1 (water slide) place DI water
3. Sterilize inoculating loop
4. Transfer a loopful of DI water to slide 2 (smear slide)
5. Sterilize loop
6. Inoculate slide 2 with bacterial culture
7. Mix bacteria + water on slide 2 across the length of the slide
8. Allow slide 2 to air dry
9. Repeat with second organism on a new slide (can reuse slide 1)

- Each student completes 2 smear prep slides
  - One for *S. aureus*
  - One for *P. aeruginosa*
- Heat fixing must be completed before Gram staining

**Slide labeling:** Use the frosted section; put both labels on the same side of the slide.

---

#### Gram Stain Procedure

1. Air dry and heat fix smear prep slides first
   - Pass slide back and forth through flame 3–4 times (~3 inches)
2. Add Crystal Violet to entire slide — wait 60 seconds
3. Wash front and back of slide to remove excess Crystal Violet
4. Add Iodine to entire slide — wait 60 seconds
5. Wash front and back to remove excess Iodine
6. **STOP — wait for decolorizing step explanation**
7. Add two droppers full of ethanol — let it run off slide; **IMMEDIATELY** wash off with DI water
8. Add Safranin to entire slide — wait 60 seconds
9. Wash front and back to remove excess Safranin
10. Place slide in Bibulous paper book to dry

**Gram Stain Interpretation:**
- Gram Positive Cocci: prominent bold purple
- Gram Positive Rod: bold purple
- Gram Negative Cocci: pink/red
- Gram Negative Rod: pink/red

#### Microscopy (Lab Overview)

**Brightfield microscopes:**
- Simplest form of microscopy — light passes through or reflects off specimen
- Requires use of stains (simple stain, Gram stain)

**Phase-contrast microscopes:**
- Converts differences in optical density (refractive index) into different shades of brightness
- Allows visualization of morphology, external structures, some internal structures
- Stains NOT required

**Darkfield microscopes:**
- Contrast created by bright specimen on dark background
- Reveals morphology and external structures
- Stains NOT required

**Fluorescent microscopes:**
- Uses high intensity illumination to excite fluorophores
- Fluorescence emanates from the sample (not the illumination source)

**Focusing protocol:**
- Focus at 10X before moving to 40X using COARSE adjustment
- Focus at 40X using ONLY FINE adjustment
- Add ONE drop of immersion oil before trying 100X
- Focus on 100X using ONLY FINE adjustment

**Cleaning:** Wipe oculars, stage, and objectives (10X, 40X, 100X) with lens paper

#### 1.5 The Microbial Family Tree

- **Carolus Linnaeus (1707–1778)** called the microbial world "chaos"
  - So little was known about microbial life
  - Microbes do not fit the classic definition of a species (group of organisms that interbreed)

**Challenges for early taxonomists:**
1. Resolution of light microscope was too low → overcome via advances in biochemistry and microscopy
2. Microbial species are hard to define → working definition: **>95% similarity of DNA sequence**

---

#### Early Microbial Classification Timeline

- **Ernst Haeckel (1860s):** Microbes are neither plant nor animal → proposed a third kind of life called **Protista (Monera)**
- **Herbert Copeland (1966):** Divided Monera into two groups:
  - Eukaryotic protists (protozoa and algae) = have nucleus
  - Prokaryotic bacteria = no true nucleus
- **Robert Whittaker (1969):** Added Fungi as a **fifth kingdom** of eukaryotic microbes → Whittaker Five-Kingdom Classification

---

#### Endosymbiosis Theory

- **Lynn Margulis (1960s):** Proposed that mitochondria and chloroplasts evolved by **endosymbiosis** from prokaryotic cells engulfed by pre-eukaryotes
- Highly controversial — implied species evolved from more than one ancestry

**Evidences of endosymbiosis:**
- Mitochondrial and Chloroplast DNA
- Mitochondrial and Chloroplast ribosomes
- Size and morphology similarities to prokaryotes

---

#### Archaea — A Distinct Domain

- **Carl Woese (1977):** Studying recently discovered prokaryotes that live in hot springs and produce methane
- Analysis of DNA coding for **small subunit (SS) rRNA** revealed these prokaryotes were a distinct form of life
- He and George Fox called them **Archaea**
- Based on rDNA sequence, archaea are actually more similar to Eukarya than Bacteria

**Small subunit rRNA gene as a molecular clock:**
- Mutations build up in DNA at a reliable rate over millions of years
- rRNA gene = ~1,550 bp long; composed of variable AND conserved regions

---

#### Three Domains of Life

**Woese's discovery grouped all life into 3 equally distinct Domains:**
1. **Bacteria**
2. **Archaea**
3. **Eukarya**

In the three-domain model:
- Mitochondria derived from **proteobacteria** (also known as Phylum Proteobacteria / Pseudomonadota)
- Chloroplasts derived from **cyanobacteria**

**Thought question:**
- Arguments for Archaea as third domain: unique rDNA sequences, membrane lipids, cell biology
- Arguments for grouping Archaea with Bacteria as Prokaryotes: both lack nucleus, similar size

---

#### 1.6 Cell Biology and the DNA Revolution

More than 99% of what we know about microbes was discovered after 1900.

**Two key areas:**
1. Structure and function of cell membranes and macromolecules
2. DNA and RNA revolution (recombinant DNA, genetic engineering)

**Key instruments:**
- **Electron microscope** (Ernst Ruska) → revealed internal cell structure
- **Ultracentrifuge** (Theodor Svedberg) → enabled separation of subcellular parts

---

#### DNA and RNA Revolution

- **1928 — Frederick Griffith:** Discovered transformation in bacteria
- **1944 — Oswald Avery et al.:** Showed the transforming substance is DNA
- **1953 — Rosalind Franklin:** Used X-ray crystallography to determine DNA is a double helix
- **James Watson, Francis Crick, Maurice Wilkins:** Discovered complementary base pairing and antiparallel nature of the double helix

---

#### Microbial Genetics Advances

- DNA work first done in bacteria and bacteriophages
- Bacteria can recombine DNA from unrelated organisms
- Discovery of heat-stable bacterial DNA polymerase from *Thermus aquaticus* (isolated from Yellowstone hot springs)
- **Kary Mullis:** Amplified DNA via the **polymerase chain reaction (PCR)**

---

#### Biotechnology and Bioremediation

- Discovery of antibiotics (penicillin and others)
- Vaccination
- Industrial microbiology: using bacteria to clone and produce insulin
- Bioremediation: cleaning human wastes and pollutants (e.g., 2010 Gulf oil spill — 200 million gallons)

---

#### Chapter Summary (Week 2 Lecture)

- Eukaryotic organelles (mitochondria, chloroplasts) evolved by endosymbiosis from prokaryotic cells
- The three domains of life: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya
- Advances in microscopy and biochemistry revealed cell structure and function
- DNA revolutionized the classification and study of microorganisms

#### 2.1 Observing Microbes — Resolution

**Resolution:** The smallest distance by which two objects can be separated and still be distinguished.
- Human retina resolution: ~150 µm (1/7 mm)
- Compound microscope resolution: 0.2 µm

**Standard Metric Units:**
- 1 mm = 10⁻³ meter
- 1 µm = 10⁻⁶ meter
- 1 nm = 10⁻⁹ meter
- 1 pm = 10⁻¹² meter

Bacterium size measured in µm | Viruses measured in nm

**Resolution vs. Detection:**
- Detection = ability to determine the PRESENCE of an object
- Resolution = ability to distinguish two objects as separate
- Individual cells are resolved through magnification

---

#### Microbial Size

- **Prokaryotes** (bacteria, archaea): 0.4–10 µm
- **Eukaryotic microbes** (protozoa, algae, fungi): 10–100 µm
- Few bacteria are large enough to be seen by the unaided eye

---

#### Cell Morphology (Shapes)

- Prokaryotic cell structures are generally simpler than eukaryotes
- Common shapes:
  - **Bacilli** = rods
  - **Cocci** = spheres
  - **Spiral forms:**
    - **Spirochetes** — flexible cell wall
    - **Spirilla** — rigid cell wall

---

#### Physics of Light and Magnification

Light is part of the electromagnetic radiation spectrum.
- Visible light range: 400–750 nm wavelength

**Resolution depends on:**
1. Contrast between object and its medium
2. Wavelength smaller than the object
3. Magnification

**Refraction:** Bending of light as it enters a substance that slows its speed → refraction magnifies an image

---

#### Compound Microscope

- A system of multiple lenses to magnify small objects
- **Ocular lens:** 10X
- **Objective lens:** 4X, 10X, 20X, 100X
- **Total magnification** = ocular magnification × objective magnification
- **Parfocal:** Objectives can be changed with minimal or no refocusing

**Numerical Aperture (NA):**
- NA = n × sin θ (where n = refractive index, θ = angle of light cone)
- Higher NA → greater resolution
- **Refractive index:** Measure of bending of a light ray passing from one medium to another (e.g., using immersion oil)

---

#### 1. Bright-Field Microscopy

- Generates a dark image over a light background
- To increase resolution:
  - Use shorter wavelength light
  - Increase contrast
  - Gather as much light as possible (use immersion oil)
  - Use higher Numerical Aperture (NA) lens

**Wet mount preparation:**
- Place microbes in a drop of water on slide with coverslip
- Advantages: observe cells in natural state
- Disadvantages: little contrast between cell and background; sample may dry out quickly

**Fixation and Staining:**
- **Fixation:** Cells made to adhere to slide in fixed position (chemical or heat fixation)
- **Staining:** Cells given a distinct color
- Most stains have conjugated double bonds or aromatic rings, and one or more positive charges

**Types of stains:**
1. **Simple stain:** Adds dark color specifically to cells (e.g., methylene blue)
2. **Differential stain:** Stains one kind of cell one color, other cells a different color or no color
   - Gram stain
   - Acid-fast stain

---

#### 2. Dark-Field Microscopy

- Microbes visualized as halos of bright light against darkness
- Opposite of bright-field
- Light shines at oblique angle — only scattered light reaches objective
- Resolves very thin objects (e.g., flagella, thin organisms like spirochetes)

---

#### 3. Phase-Contrast Microscopy

- Refracted and transmitted light shifted out of phase
- Reveals differences in refractive index
- Can be used to view live cells and cellular organelles (no staining required)

---

#### 4. Fluorescence Microscopy

- Specimen absorbs shorter wavelength light, then emits longer wavelength light
- Light absorbed by a **fluorophore** (fluorescent chemical)
- **Excitation wavelength < Emission wavelength**

**Immunofluorescence:**
- Fluorophore combined with antibodies against a specific type of bacterium
- E.g., Test for syphilis (VDRL): *Treponema pallidum* shows up as green cells on dark background

---

#### 5. Electron Microscopy

- Electrons behave like light waves; very high frequency → very high resolution (up to few nanometers)
- Sample must absorb electrons (coated with heavy metal)
- Electron beam and sample are in a vacuum
- Lenses are magnetic fields

**Two major types:**

**Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM):**
- Electrons pass THROUGH the specimen
- Resolution: as close as 10 pm (picometer)
- Magnification: up to 100,000X
- Shows **internal** structures in 2D

**Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM):**
- Electrons scan the specimen SURFACE
- Specimen coated in gold → secondary electrons ejected → captured by detector
- Generates a **3D image**
- Resolution: 10 nm; Magnification: up to 10,000X
- Shows **external** structures in 3D

---

#### Chapter Summary (Week 3)

- When observing microbes, resolution and magnification are paramount
- Different kinds of microscopes required for different scales:
  - **Bright-field:** employs various stains
  - **Dark-field:** detects unresolved/very thin objects
  - **Phase-contrast:** exploits differences in refractive indices; views live cells
  - **Fluorescence:** employs fluorophores for labeling
- Electron microscopes use beam of electrons instead of light
  - **TEM:** internal details, 2D
  - **SEM:** external details, 3D

### Lecture: Prokaryotic Cell Membrane and Cell Wall

#### Chapter Overview
- The cell membrane and transport
- Archaea and Bacteria membrane lipids
- The cell wall and other outer layers
- Gram-positive cell wall
- Gram-negative cell wall
- Special cases: Mycobacterial and Mycoplasma cell envelopes

---

#### Introduction

**Most bacteria share fundamental traits:**
- Thick, complex outer envelope
- Compact genome
- Tightly coordinated cell functions

**Archaea:** Like bacteria, are prokaryotes — but have unique membrane and envelope structures.

**Eukaryotic cells:** Have a nucleus and extensive membranous organelles.

---

#### Bacterial Cell Components

- **Cell membrane** = encloses the cytoplasm
- **Cell wall** = covers the cell membrane
- **Cytoplasm** = gel-like network
- **Nucleoid** = non-membrane-bound area of cytoplasm that contains the chromosome
- **Other specialized structures:** Capsule, S-layer, and more
- **Flagellum** = external helical filament whose rotary motor propels the cell

---

#### The Cell Membrane and Transport

- Cell membrane is the structure that DEFINES the cell
- Also used for energy production (similar to mitochondria of eukaryotic cells)

**Cell membrane composition:**
- Phospholipids (two layers = leaflets)
- Proteins

---

#### Bacterial Cell Membrane

- Made of **phospholipid bilayer**
- Phospholipids are **ester-linked** (key distinction from archaea)

---

#### Mycobacterial Cell Envelopes

- *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* and *M. leprae* have very complex cell envelopes
- Contain unusual lipids: **mycolic acids**
- Contain unusual sugars: **arabinogalactans**
- Very hard to stain with standard methods
- Stained using **Acid-fast stain** (requires application of heat)

---

#### Archaea Membrane Lipids

Archaea have the most extreme variations in phospholipid side-chain structures:
- **Ether links** between glycerol and fatty acids (NOT ester links like bacteria)
  - Helps survive at high temperatures
- Hydrocarbon chains are **branched terpenoids (isoprenoid)**
- Can form a **fused bilayer (monolayer)** structure for extreme temperature stability

---

#### Membrane Proteins

Membrane proteins serve numerous functions:
- Structural support
- Detect environmental signals
- Ion transport and energy storage
- Secretion (Updated version)

Have hydrophilic AND hydrophobic regions that lock the protein in the membrane.

---

#### Transport Across the Membrane

**Polar and charged molecules require specific protein transporters.**

- **Passive transport:** Molecules move ALONG their concentration gradient (no energy required)
- **Active transport:** Molecules move AGAINST their concentration gradient
  - Requires energy — usually in the form of **ATP hydrolysis**

---

#### Cell Wall and Outer Layers

How do prokaryotes protect their cell membrane? → **The rigid cell wall.**

**Exception:** A few prokaryotes (e.g., mycoplasmas) have no cell wall.
- Mycoplasmas can still cause pneumonia ("walking pneumonia")
- Must maintain membrane strength through other mechanisms (e.g., cholesterol incorporation)

---

#### Peptidoglycan (PG)

- Most bacterial cell walls are made of **peptidoglycan**
- PG molecule consists of:
  - **N-acetylglucosamine (NAG)**
  - **N-acetylmuramic acid (NAM)**
  - PG layers bound to each other by a **peptide of 4–6 amino acids**

**PG is unique to bacteria → excellent antibiotic targets:**
- Penicillin
- Vancomycin
- Antibiotic resistant strains very common: **MRSA** (methicillin-resistant *S. aureus*), **VRSA** (vancomycin-resistant *S. aureus*)

---

#### Gram-Positive Cell Wall

- Multiple PG layers (thick)
- Threaded by **teichoic acids**
  - Teichoic acid attaches to NAM or the D-alanine component of the cell wall
- Has a **periplasm**

**PG cross-bridge in Gram-positive (*S. aureus*):**
- Uses **L-Lys** (lysine)
- **Pentaglycine interbridge**

---

#### Gram-Negative Cell Wall

- Consists of up to **three sheets** of peptidoglycan (thin) covered by an **outer membrane**

**Outer membrane structure:**
- **Outward-facing leaflet contains:**
  - **Lipopolysaccharides (LPS)**
  - **Porins:** serve as channels for ions and small molecules
- **Inward-facing leaflet includes:**
  - Lipoprotein
  - Phospholipid

**LPS — medically significant:**
- **O-polysaccharide side chain** = Antigen (used for serotyping)
- **Lipid A** = **Endotoxin** (triggers immune response; responsible for septic shock)

**PG cross-bridge in Gram-negative (*E. coli*):**
- Uses **DAP** (diaminopimelic acid)
- Peptide bond only (no interbridge)

---

#### Gram Stain Procedure (Lecture)

The Gram stain procedure distinguishes:
- **Gram-positive** (thick PG wall) → retains crystal violet → **PURPLE**
- **Gram-negative** (thin PG + outer membrane) → loses crystal violet, takes safranin → **PINK/RED**

---

#### Gram-Positive vs. Gram-Negative Summary

| Feature | Gram-Positive | Gram-Negative |
|---|---|---|
| PG layer | Thick, multiple layers | Thin, 1–3 sheets |
| Teichoic acids | Yes | No |
| Outer membrane | No | Yes |
| LPS | No | Yes (endotoxin) |
| Porins | No | Yes |
| Gram stain result | Purple | Pink/Red |

---

#### Capsule and S-Layer (Gram-positive and some Gram-negative)

**Capsule:**
- Made of polysaccharide and glycoprotein
- **Protects cells from phagocytosis**
- Also found in some Gram-negative bacteria
- Appears as halo around the cell under microscopy

**S-layer:**
- Found in free-living bacteria and archaea
- Consisting of protein or glycoprotein
- Functions: cell shape and protection from osmotic stress

#### Selective Media

- Allows only certain microorganisms to grow while inhibiting others
- Does NOT mean only ONE species will grow
- Example: **MSA (Mannitol Salt Agar)**
  - Contains 7.5% salt → selects for salt-tolerant microorganisms (like *Staphylococcus*)
  - *Bacillus* can also grow; *E. coli*, *Enterobacter aerogenes*, *Proteus mirabilis* usually do NOT grow

---

#### Differential Media

- Causes some bacteria to take on an appearance that distinguishes them from others (color change)

**Examples:**
- **MSA (Mannitol Salt Agar):** Contains mannitol + pH indicator
  - *S. aureus* = POSITIVE (ferments mannitol → yellow)
  - *S. epidermidis* = NEGATIVE

- **SBA (Sheep Blood Agar):** Contains 5% sheep blood
  - *Streptococcus pyogenes* → Beta (complete hemolysis/clearing)
  - *Streptococcus pneumoniae* → Alpha (incomplete hemolysis/greening)
  - *Enterococcus faecalis* → Gamma (no hemolysis/no change)
  - Almost all organisms will grow on SBA

---

#### Biochemical Tests

**BHI with 6.5% NaCl:**
- Tests ability to grow in 6.5% NaCl
- Positive: growth in broth (turbidity)
- Negative: no growth (no turbidity)
- Good for Streptococcus and Enterococcus (but Staphylococcus and some Gram-negatives will also grow)

**Bile Esculin Hydrolysis:**
- Tests ability to hydrolyze esculin to esculetin
- Positive: blackening of the tube/agar
- Negative: no color change or <1/2 of slant is black
- Good for Streptococcus and Enterococcus

**Coagulase:**
- Detects coagulase excreted by *S. aureus* → causes fibrin clot formation
- Positive: coagulation of plasma (clot formation)
- Negative: no clot
- Good for Staphylococcus

**Hemolysis on SBA:**
- Gamma (γ): no hemolysis/no discoloration
- Alpha (α): incomplete hemolysis/greening
- Beta (β): complete hemolysis/clearing
- Good for Streptococcus and Bacillus

**MSA:**
- Positive: Growth+ / Acid+ → yellow colonies (mannitol fermented → acid)
- Negative: Growth+ / Acid- → colorless colonies (no acid)
- Good for Staphylococcus and Bacillus

**Nitrate Reduction:**
- Tests ability to reduce nitrate to nitrite/ammonia/nitrogenous gas
- Positive: red coloration after Nitrate A and B reagents
- Positive (Complete): no color change after zinc addition
- Negative: red coloration after zinc addition
- Good for Staphylococcus, Bacillus, Enterobacteriaceae

**Phenol Red Carbohydrate Tubes:**
- Tests carbohydrate fermentation
- Positive: Yellow
- Negative: Red
- Must incubate minimum 48 hrs

**Starch Hydrolysis:**
- Tests ability to hydrolyze starch
- Positive: dark agar with CLEAR zone around streak line
- Negative: dark agar up to the streak line
- Good for Bacillus (let plate sit a few minutes after adding iodine)
