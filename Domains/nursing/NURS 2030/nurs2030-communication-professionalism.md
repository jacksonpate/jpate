# ISBARR — Giving Handoff (AUCON Template)

## Overview
ISBARR is the standard handoff communication framework used at AUCON.
- Used for: nurse-to-nurse handoff, nurse-to-prescriber communication, or reporting to healthcare team

## Structure

### I — Introduction
- Your name, unit, role
- Patient name, age, room number
- Attending physician

### S — Situation
- Current reason for contact / chief complaint
- What is happening RIGHT NOW with the patient

### B — Background
- Admitting diagnosis
- Relevant medical history
- Code status
- Isolation precautions
- Allergies

### A — Assessment (12 Categories)
1. **Vitals** — BP, HR, RR, Temp, SpO2, Pain score
2. **Neuro** — LOC, orientation (person/place/time/event), GCS, pupils, motor/sensory
3. **Cardiac** — Heart sounds, rhythm, peripheral pulses, capillary refill, edema
4. **Respiratory** — Breath sounds, O2 delivery method and rate, effort, secretions
5. **GI** — Bowel sounds, last BM, diet, tolerance, nausea/vomiting, abdominal assessment
6. **GU** — Urine output, color/clarity, Foley catheter present/date placed, continence
7. **Musculoskeletal** — Mobility level, fall risk score, activity orders, assistive devices
8. **Skin** — Wounds, pressure injuries (stage), dressings, IV site assessment
9. **Access** — IV type (peripheral/central), location, gauge, date placed, patency
10. **Medications** — Current medications, last PRN given, drips running
11. **Labs** — Most recent relevant lab values, any critical values
12. **Social** — Support system, discharge needs, patient/family concerns

### R — Recommendation
- What you need from the person you are calling
- Orders needed, clarification, transfer request, etc.

### R — Read Back
- Receiver reads back orders or key information to confirm accuracy
- Especially important for verbal/telephone orders

# CJMM, Benner's Theory & SMART Goals (Class Activity)

## Clinical Judgment Measurement Model (CJMM)

### Part 1 — Unfolding Scenario (Respiratory Patient)

**Stage 1 → Stage 2 → Stage 3: Progressive respiratory decline**

#### Step 1: Recognize Cues (3 cues identified)
1. Increased respiratory rate
2. Difficulty speaking / fatigue
3. Orthopneic positioning (sitting upright to breathe)

#### Step 2: Analyze Cues
Respiratory status worsened over time, evidenced by:
- Increased breathing rate
- Difficulty speaking
- Fatigue
- Anxiety
- Orthopneic positioning

Changes may indicate declining respiratory function and inadequate oxygenation. Early recognition is important to prevent further deterioration.

#### Step 3: Prioritize Hypotheses
**Priority: BREATHING** (circled)

Using ABC framework: Airway → Breathing → Circulation
Breathing is the priority because oxygenation is compromised.

## Benner's Novice to Expert Theory

Five stages of nursing skill acquisition:

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1. Novice** | No experience. Follows rules without context. Needs step-by-step instruction. Cannot deviate from protocol. |
| **2. Advanced Beginner** | Some experience. Recognizes meaningful patterns. Needs guidance in prioritizing. Still relies on rules. |
| **3. Competent** | 2–3 years experience. Conscious deliberate planning. Feels more organized and efficient. Lacks speed/flexibility of higher stages. |
| **4. Proficient** | Perceives situations as a whole, not just parts. Learns from experience. Decision-making is less labored. Sees deviations from normal quickly. |
| **5. Expert** | No longer relies on rules. Intuitive grasp of situations. Deep background understanding. Highly analytical in unfamiliar situations. |

# Domain 9 — Professionalism (Module 9 PPT Notes)

## State Practice Acts & Scope of Practice

### Nurse Practice Act
- Each state has its own Nurse Practice Act
- Regulated by the State Board of Nursing (SBON)
- Defines legal boundaries of nursing practice

### Scope of Practice — The 5 W's
| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| **Who** | Registered Nurse (RN) |
| **What** | Specific tasks and procedures |
| **Where** | Any environment with a healthcare consumer |
| **When** | Whenever there is a need for nursing |
| **Why** | Client safety |

| Principle | Definition |
|-----------|-----------|

1. Identify the problem or dilemma
2. Gather relevant information
3. Identify all options/alternatives
4. Consider ethical principles involved
5. Apply ethical frameworks
6. Make a decision
7. Implement the decision
8. Evaluate the outcome

## Informed Consent

- Patient must be:
  - Competent to make decisions
  - Given adequate information
  - Voluntary (no coercion)
- Nurse's role: witness signature, ensure understanding, notify provider of concerns
- Physician/provider is responsible for obtaining informed consent

## Implied Consent

- Assumed in emergency situations when patient cannot consent
- Example: unconscious trauma patient in ER

---

## Advanced Directives

### Living Will
- Written document specifying care wishes if patient becomes unable to speak for themselves
- Covers: resuscitation, ventilation, artificial nutrition, organ donation

### Durable Power of Attorney (Healthcare Proxy)
- Names another person to make healthcare decisions on patient's behalf
- Activates when patient is incapacitated

## Sentinel Events

Unexpected events that result in serious harm or death to a patient.

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Patient falls | Fall resulting in injury or death |
| Medication errors | Wrong drug, wrong dose, wrong patient |
| Surgical errors | Wrong site, wrong procedure, wrong patient |
| Suicide | Patient suicide in inpatient setting |
| Transfusion errors | Wrong blood type administered |
| Abduction | Infant abduction |

---

## Incidents to Report

- Any unexpected event that affects patient safety
- Near misses (close calls) — also reportable
- Completed via incident/variance report (separate from patient chart)
- Document facts only — do NOT document that an incident report was filed in the chart

---

## Client Abandonment

- Defined as: nurse accepts a patient assignment and then leaves without:
  - Ensuring proper handoff to another qualified nurse
  - Notifying the appropriate supervisor
- Can result in: disciplinary action, license revocation

---

## Staffing & Delegation

- Nurses must follow SBON rules on delegation
- Delegate based on:
  - Patient stability
  - Complexity of task
  - Competency of delegatee
- **5 Rights of Delegation:** Right task, Right circumstance, Right person, Right direction, Right supervision

---

## Breach of Nursing Practice / Falsification

- Falsifying records = grounds for license revocation
- Examples: charting care not given, altering records, documenting ahead
- Nurses are legally and professionally accountable for all documentation

## HIPAA — Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act

Federal law protecting patient health information.

### PHI — Protected Health Information

**Direct Identifiers:**
- Name
- Address (street, city, county, zip)
- Dates (birth, death, admission, discharge — except year)
- Phone numbers
- Fax numbers
- Email addresses
- SSN (Social Security Number)
- Medical record numbers
- Health plan beneficiary numbers
- Account numbers
- Certificate/license numbers
- Vehicle identifiers (VINs, license plates)
- Device identifiers and serial numbers
- Web URLs
- IP addresses
- Biometric identifiers (fingerprints, voice prints)
- Full face photographs
- Any other unique identifying number or code

### HIPAA Rules

| Rule | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Privacy Rule** | Governs use and disclosure of PHI |
| **Security Rule** | Protects electronic PHI (ePHI) |
| **Breach Notification Rule** | Requires notification when PHI is breached |
| **Enforcement Rule** | Sets penalties for violations |

## Confidentiality Violations (5 Types)

1. Accessing records of patients not under your care
2. Sharing PHI with unauthorized individuals
3. Leaving patient information visible/accessible
4. Social media violations (posting patient info)
5. Discussing patient info in public areas (elevators, cafeterias, hallways)

## Types of Documentation

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Source-Oriented** | Each discipline documents in separate section |
| **Problem-Oriented** | Organized around patient problems; uses numbered problem list |
| **SOAP** | Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan |
| **PIE** | Problem, Intervention, Evaluation |
| **Focus Charting** | Data, Action, Response (DAR format); focuses on patient concerns |
| **CBE (Charting by Exception)** | Only documents deviations from established norms/standards |

---

## FACT Charting (EHR Documentation Standard)

| Letter | Meaning | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **F** | Factual | Document what you observe, not opinions |
| **A** | Accurate | Correct spelling, correct data, correct patient |
| **C** | Complete | Full assessment, all relevant info included |
| **T** | Timely | Document as soon as possible after care is given |

**Key rules:**
- Never leave blank lines
- Use only approved abbreviations
- Never chart ahead of time
- Correct errors properly (single line through error, initials, date)
- Never use white-out

---

## Medication Administration & EHR

### NPSG — National Patient Safety Goals
- Use at least two patient identifiers before administering medications
- Improve safety of high-alert medications

### Medication Reconciliation
- Process of comparing patient's medication list to orders at each transition of care
- Reduces medication errors across care settings

### Adverse Drug Event (ADE)
- Any injury resulting from medical intervention related to a drug

### Medication Error
- Any preventable event that may cause or lead to inappropriate medication use or patient harm

### 4 Phases of Error
1. **Error occurrence** — mistake happens
2. **Error detection** — mistake is caught (by whom: nurse, pharmacist, patient)
3. **Error reporting** — incident report filed
4. **Error analysis** — root cause analysis, prevention plan

---

## Documentation in EHR

- Nurses spend approximately **40% of their shift** on documentation
- Documentation is both:
  - **Professional necessity** — communication, continuity of care
  - **Legal necessity** — "if it wasn't charted, it wasn't done"
- Must be objective, accurate, and timely
- Supports billing, quality monitoring, and legal protection
