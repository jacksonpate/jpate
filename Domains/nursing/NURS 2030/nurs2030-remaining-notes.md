## Domain 4 — Scholarship for the Nursing Discipline

Source: `2030-4.pdf`

### Domain Overview
- 4.1 Advance the scholarship of nursing
- 4.2 Integrate best evidence into nursing practice
- 4.3 Promote the ethical conduct of scholarly activities

### Nursing Research vs. Evidence-Based Practice

**Research**
- Creates novel evidence through systematic investigation
- Generation of knowledge or experimentation to discover new treatments, interventions, or best practices

**Evidence-Based Practice (EBP)**
- Integration of current evidence and clinical expertise with individual and family preferences
- NOT the same as research generation

### Essential Elements of Evidence-Based Practice

1. **Spirit of Inquiry**
2. **Identify a Problem** — Recognize gaps in practice or healthcare delivery
3. **Search for credible sources of evidence** — Locate quality, right evidence
4. **Evaluate the findings** — Critically appraise the evidence
5. **Implement recommendations** — Apply evidence-based changes
6. **Assess outcomes** — Review effectiveness of implementation
7. **Disseminate the results** — Share findings and outcomes with others

### Identify a Problem — PICOT Question

- **P** — Population: Who is the target group?
- **I** — Intervention: What is being tested/done?
- **C** — Comparison: What is the alternative/control?
- **O** — Outcome: What is the desired result?
- **T** — Time: What is the timeframe?

**Example PICOT:**
- EBP version: "For nursing students in 2-year BSN programs (P), what is the importance of taking standardized exams (I) versus not taking standardized exams (C) to measure learning and improve test taking skills (O) each semester (T)?"
- Research version: "Can requiring students in a 2-year BSN program (P) to take standardized exams each semester (I) versus only taking instructor-made exams (C) improve NCLEX pass rates (O) over 2 years (T)?"

### Peer-Reviewed vs. Non-Peer-Reviewed

**Peer-Reviewed**
- Reviewed by other professionals in the field
- Feedback given and changes made
- Demonstrates rigor and expertise

**Non-Peer-Reviewed (Less Reliable)**
- Anecdotal information and social opinions without expertise/rigor
- Questions to ask: Who conducted research? Are they credentialed? Are there biases present?

### Search for Credible Sources of Evidence

**Scholarly Databases:**
- Medline
- Alt HealthWatch
- Academic Search Premier
- CINAHL
- Health Source
- PsycINFO
- ERIC

**Websites:** Look for ".edu", ".gov", or ".org" domains

**Database Search Tips:**
- Use Keywords
- Full Text filter
- Peer Reviewed filter
- Published Date filter

### Levels of Evidence (Hierarchy)

| Level | Strength | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Level I | Strongest | Systematic review or meta-analysis of all available randomized controlled trials |
| Level II | Strongest | At least one large, well-designed randomized controlled trial (RCT) |
| Level III | — | Well-designed controlled study with non-random assignment |
| Level IV | — | Well-designed cohort study |
| Level V | Moderate | Systematic review of qualitative studies |
| Level VI | — | Single qualitative study |
| Level VII | Weakest | Expert opinions, textbooks, credible websites, encyclopedias |

### Appraising the Evidence (Critical Appraisal)

- **Validity** — Does the evidence measure what it claims to measure?
- **Reliability** — Are results consistent and reproducible?
- **Applicability** — Can it be applied to your population/setting?

### Implementing Recommendations

Factors to consider when implementing EBP:
- Finances
- Time
- Training
- Attitudes
- Theory-practice gap

### Evaluating Effectiveness

- Addresses the **Outcome** portion of the PICOT question
- Measures success of the implemented change

### Disseminating Results

- Share the results of the evaluation of the intervention with others

### Types of Nursing Research

- Quantitative
- Qualitative
- Mixed Methods
- Translational

## Domain 7 — Systems-Based Practice

Source: `2030-7.pdf`

### Domain Overview
- 7.1 Apply knowledge of systems to work effectively across the continuum of care
- 7.2 Incorporate consideration of cost-effectiveness of care
- 7.3 Optimize system effectiveness through application of innovation and evidence-based practice

### AUCON Mission and Vision

**Mission:** To advance health and healthcare for all, through excellence in practice, teaching, research, and outreach.

**Vision:** To be nationally recognized as leaders in health, addressing the needs of diverse populations.

### State Regulation

- Healthcare organizations employ managers and directors to follow state regulations via policies and procedures
- States regulate how practitioners operate: **Nurse Practice Acts** and **Standards of Nursing Practice**

### Accreditation

- Health care facilities are accredited by outside organizations that assure quality of services and client safety
- **The Joint Commission (TJC)**:
  - Performs surveys of documentation, policies, interviews with administration and staff
  - Provides guidance on improving practices
  - Full accreditation given for 3 years

### Chain of Command

Organizational hierarchy identifying lines of authority within an organization: CEO → CNO → Director → Unit Director → Individual Nurse

**AUCON Chain of Command:**
- Dean (Gregg Newschwander)
- Associate Dean (Caralise Hunt)
  - Academic Issues → Course Leader and Student Success Coach → Academic Advisor → Person who taught the content
  - Progression Problems → Academic Advisor → Student Success Coach
  - Clinical Issues → Clinical Course Leader → Clinical Instructor

### Medicare

**Qualifications:**
- Age 65 or older
- Younger than 65 with disabilities
- Any age with end-stage renal disease (ESRD)

**Parts:**
1. **Part A** — Hospitalization, skilled nursing facilities, some hospice and home health care
2. **Part B** — Doctor's services, physical therapy, occupational therapy, some home health care
3. **Part D** — Prescription drugs

### Medicaid

- Based on income in relation to the poverty level
- Administered by states and federal government
- **Must supply:**
  - Inpatient and outpatient hospital services
  - Physician services
  - Laboratory and x-ray services
  - Home health services
- **Can choose to supply:**
  - Coverage for prescription drugs
  - Case management
  - Physical therapy
  - Occupational therapy

### Private Insurance

- Coverage NOT provided by a government agency
- Can be provided by employers; complex reimbursement rules
- Requires prior authorization; restrictions on where care received and how much is covered
- Examples: Blue Cross Blue Shield, Cigna, Aetna, HMOs (Health Maintenance Organizations)

### Shared Governance

- Shared-decision structure giving nurses control over their own practice
- Guides decisions toward accountability and ownership of clinical outcomes
- Takes nurses from point of care to the decision-making table

**Principle 1:** Professional governance is grounded in accountability
- Standards, code of ethics, federal/state regulations (AUCON, AACN Essentials, Accreditation, Alabama State Board of Nursing)

**Principle 2:** Organizational work and decision-making designed around accountability
- Accountable to populations we serve (Student and Faculty Handbook, Auburn University Handbook)

**Principle 3:** Professional governance structures demonstrate shared decision-making
- AUCON Faculty Committees: Technology, Undergraduate Curriculum, Simulation, Undergraduate Evaluation, Outreach, Diversity and Inclusion
- Student Committees: SGA, Student Organizations

**Shared Governance Model:** Faculty and Students → Mission, Vision, Values → Committees → Associate Dean for Academic Affairs → Dean

### Emory Healthcare Example

Key leadership structure includes:
- Board of Directors
- Executive Staff
- CEO, Chief Nurse Executive
- Chief Human Resources Officer, Chief Financial Officer
- Chief Medical Officer, Chief Strategy Officer
- Chief Compliance Officer, Chief Transformation Officer
- Chief Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion Officer
- Chief Risk Officer, Vice President of Strategic Planning

Centers and Programs include: Brain Health Care, Neurointensive Care Unit

## Interpersonal Communication Competence Survey

Source: `Interpersonal+Communication+Competence+Survey.pdf`

Course: NURS 2030 — Foundations to Professional Nursing
Module: Domain 2 — Person-Centered Care
Purpose: Self-reflection on communication skills essential to person-centered care

### Response Scale
- 1 = Almost never
- 2 = Seldom
- 3 = Sometimes
- 4 = Often
- 5 = Almost always

### Survey Dimensions and Items

**Self-Disclosure**
1. I allow friends to see who I really am. (1–5)
2. Other people know what I'm thinking. (1–5)
3. I reveal how I feel to others. (1–5)

**Empathy**
4. I can put myself in others' shoes. (1–5)
5. I don't know exactly what others are feeling. (1–5) *(reverse-scored)*
6. Other people think that I understand them. (1–5)

**Social Relaxation**
7. I am comfortable in social situations. (1–5)
8. I feel relaxed in small group gatherings. (1–5)
9. I feel insecure in groups of strangers. (1–5) *(reverse-scored)*

**Assertiveness**
10. When I've been wronged, I confront the person who wronged me. (1–5)
11. I have trouble standing up for myself. (1–5) *(reverse-scored)*
12. I stand up for my rights. (1–5)

**Altercentrism (Being Other-Centered)**
13. My conversations are pretty one-sided. (1–5) *(reverse-scored)*
14. I let others know that I understand what they say. (1–5)
15. My mind wanders during conversations. (1–5) *(reverse-scored)*

**Interaction Management**
16. My conversations are characterized by smooth shifts from one topic to the next. (1–5)
17. I take charge of conversations I'm in by negotiating what topics we talk about. (1–5)
18. In conversations, I perceive not only what they say but what they don't say. (1–5)

**Expressiveness**
19. Others can tell when I'm happy or sad. (1–5)
20. It is difficult to find the right words to express myself. (1–5) *(reverse-scored)*
21. I express myself well verbally. (1–5)

**Supportiveness**
22. My communication is usually descriptive, not evaluative. (1–5)
23. I communicate with others as though they are equals. (1–5)
24. Others would describe me as warm. (1–5)

**Immediacy**
25. Others believe that I genuinely care about them. (1–5)
26. I try to look others in the eye when I speak with them. (1–5)
27. I tell people when I feel close to them. (1–5)

**Environmental Control**
28. I accomplish my communication goals. (1–5)
29. I can persuade others to my position when appropriate. (1–5)
30. I feel confident guiding conversations toward productive outcomes. (1–5)

### Scoring

**Step 1:** Each circled number = its point value (1–5)

**Step 2:** Reverse-score questions 5, 9, 11, 13, 15, and 20:
| Circled | Change to |
|---------|-----------|
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 3 |
| 4 | 2 |
| 5 | 1 |

**Step 3:** Add all 30 scores. Total possible = 150.

### Score Interpretation

| Score | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|
| 121–150 | Strong communication skills — comfortable with empathy, emotion management, respectful engagement |
| 91–120 | Developing skills — strengths in some areas, growth opportunities in others; typical for early nursing students |
| 61–90 | Early development — limited experience, self-awareness, or discomfort; improves with practice and clinical exposure |
| 30–60 | Currently uncertain or uncomfortable — does not predict failure; nursing education builds these skills over time |

**Key Takeaway:** Communication skills are learned skills, not fixed traits. They improve with experience, confidence, self-awareness, and emotional regulation.

## Domain 8 — Informatics and Healthcare Technology

Source: `Untitled.pdf` (pages 1–10)

### Domain Overview
- 8.1 Describe various information and communication technology tools used in care of patients, communities, and populations
- 8.2 Use information and communication technology to gather data, create information, and generate knowledge
- 8.3 Use information and communication technologies and informatics processes to deliver safe nursing care to diverse populations in a variety of settings
- 8.4 Use ICT to support documentation of care and communication among providers, patients, and all system levels
- 8.5 Use ICT in accordance with ethical, legal, professional, and regulatory standards and workplace policies in the delivery of care

### Nursing Informatics

The use of information and technology to:
- Communicate
- Manage knowledge
- Mitigate error
- Support decision-making

### Types of Informatics Used in Nursing

- **Simulation**
- **Clinical Information Systems** — Computer systems that allow instant retrieval of client information directly or from data networks
- **Information Technology** — Technology and physical devices used to create and store information
- **Electronic Health Record (EHR)** — Real-time computerized form of a client's paper chart that can be shared among providers

### Aspects of Nursing Informatics

**Clinical Pathways**
- Also called "care maps" or "integrated pathways"
- Decision support tools that outline specific steps to a predetermined clinical problem

**HIPAA and Confidentiality**

**Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)**
- Federal law enacted to safeguard clients' medical health information when accessed or shared electronically without consent or knowledge

**Privacy Rule — Protected Health Information (PHI)**
- Any information held by a covered entity for healthcare that can be linked to an individual
- Unique client identifiers must be protected

**Patient Health Identifiers (HIPAA Examples)**

Direct Identifiers & Personal Numbers:
- Names
- Social Security numbers
- Medical record numbers
- Health insurance beneficiary numbers
- Account numbers
- Certificate or license numbers
- Any unique identifying number, characteristic, or code

Contact, Location, Digital & Biometric Identifiers:
- Telephone numbers
- Fax numbers
- Email addresses
- Web URLs
- Internet Protocol (IP) addresses
- Geographic data smaller than a state
- Dates related to an individual (except year)
- Vehicle identifiers and serial numbers (including license plates)
- Device identifiers and serial numbers
- Biometric identifiers (e.g., retinal scan, fingerprints)
- Full face photographs and comparable images

**Security Rule** — Specifically addresses the use and disclosure of electronic PHI

**Breach Notification Rule** — Covers the process of reporting and responding to each breach of PHI

**Enforcement Rule** — Includes compliance and investigations, administration of penalties for HIPAA violations, and procedures for investigation

## Chapter 19 — Preschool Child (NURS 2030 Lifespan Content)

Source: `Untitled (1).pdf` (pages 1–10)

### Overview — Preschool Child
- Age: 3–5 years
- More developed body structure
- Ability to control and use the body
- Increasing language development
- Mastery of self as independent — extends experience beyond family
- Beginning of kindergarten or 1st grade
- Many Healthy People 2030 goals aimed at preschoolers

### Biology and Genetics

**General Changes:**
- Protuberant abdomen of toddler disappears
- Pelvis begins to straighten; mild in-toeing may remain
- May show transient genu valgum (knock knee) and flat feet
- Growth: 4 lbs and 2 inches per year
- Skin matures; eccrine glands become functional; apocrine glands (axilla/groin) non-functional
- Teeth: finish primary dentition; start secondary tooth eruption

**Select System Changes:**
- Kidneys/GI: full maturity; voluntary control
- Heart/lungs: increased capacity
- Ears: change shape; less otitis media
- Tonsils/adenoids: large
- Immune: boosted immune response
- Musculoskeletal/neuro: advances in fine/gross motor skills; abdominal muscles develop

**Gender:**
- Boys experience more illnesses and more accidents

**Race Influences:**
- Healthcare access and cultural preferences
- Dietary choices
- Health-promoting behaviors

**Genetics:** Most commonly diagnosed in preschool years:
- Cystic fibrosis
- Duchenne muscular dystrophy
- Fragile X syndrome

### Gordon's Functional Health Patterns

1. Health perception–health management pattern
2. Nutritional-metabolic pattern
3. Elimination pattern
4. Activity-exercise pattern
5. Sleep-rest pattern
6. Cognitive-perceptual pattern
7. Self-perception–self-concept pattern
8. Roles-relationships pattern
9. Sexuality-reproductive pattern
10. Coping–stress tolerance pattern
11. Values-beliefs pattern

### Health Perception–Health Management Pattern

**Body Concepts:**
- Curious about body of opposite sex
- Vague/inaccurate ideas of internal body
- Anxiety: fear of mutilation, death

**Health Beliefs and Management:**
- Family influence is major factor
- Upset over minor injuries; pain/illness viewed as punishment
- Can contribute to health management (brushing teeth)

**Health Promotion:** Help families develop healthy lifestyle habits

### Nutritional-Metabolic Pattern

**Nutritional Guidelines:**
- Variety of foods; MyPlate (replaces food pyramid)
- Attention to iron and calcium intake
- Limit fat and sugar

**Behavioral/Motor:**
- Food preferences/refusals may cause conflicts
- Learning utensil use
- Helping with meal preparation

**Other:**
- Begin to eat meals away from home
- Preschool/childcare center requirements apply

## NURS 2030 Exam 2 Study Guide

Source: `nurs_2030 exam2.pdf`

### Key Concepts

**Healthcare-Associated Infections (HAIs)**
- More than 500 people acquire healthcare-related infectious deaths per year
- **SSI** = Surgical Site Infection (most common context for HAI)

**Restraints**
- Used to prevent patients from removing IVs, catheters, or other medical equipment

**Client Safety Event**
- An unexpected event with or without injury that had the potential to cause harm

**Process in Quality Measurements**
- The mechanism of care provided to patients (the process itself, not just outcomes)
