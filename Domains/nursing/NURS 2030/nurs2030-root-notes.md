### Nursing Research vs. Evidence-Based Practice
- **Research**: Generation of knowledge/experimentation to discover new treatments, interventions, or best practices — creates novel evidence through systematic investigation
- **Evidence-Based Practice (EBP)**: Integration of current evidence and clinical expertise with individual and family preferences

### Essential Elements of EBP
1. Spirit of Inquiry — Recognize gaps in practice/healthcare delivery
2. Identify a Problem — Locate quality, right sources
3. Search for credible sources of evidence — Critically appraise the evidence
4. Evaluate the findings — Apply evidence-based changes
5. Implement recommendations — Assess outcomes of implementation
6. Review their effectiveness — Share findings/outcomes with others
7. Disseminate the results

### PICOT Question Framework
- **P** — Population: Who is the target group?
- **I** — Intervention: What is being done/tested?
- **C** — Comparison: What is the alternative/control?
- **O** — Outcome: What is the desired result?
- **T** — Time: What is the timeframe?

**Example EBP PICOT**: For nursing students in 2-year BSN programs (P), what is the importance of taking standardized exams (I) versus not taking standardized exams (C) to measure learning and improve test-taking skills (O) each semester (T)?

**Example Research PICOT**: Can requiring students in a 2-year BSN program (P) to take standardized exams each semester (I) versus only taking instructor-made exams (C) improve NCLEX pass rates (O) over 2 years (T)?

### Peer-Reviewed vs. Non-Peer-Reviewed Sources
- **Peer-Reviewed**: Reviewed by other professionals; feedback given and changes made; demonstrates rigor and expertise
- **Non-Peer-Reviewed**: Anecdotal information and social opinions without expertise/rigor; less reliable
- Questions to ask: Who conducted the research? Are they credentialed? Are there biases present?

### Credible Sources / Scholarly Databases
- Medline, Alt HealthWatch, Academic Search Premier, CINAHL, Health Source, PsycINFO, ERIC
- Websites: .edu, .gov, .org preferred
- Searching: Keywords, Full Text, Peer Reviewed, Published Date filters

### Levels of Evidence (Strength of Evidence Hierarchy)
| Level | Strength | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Level I | Strongest | Systematic review or meta-analysis of all available RCTs |
| Level II | Strong | At least one large, well-designed RCT with random assignment |
| Level III | Moderate | Well-designed controlled study without random assignment |
| Level IV | Moderate | Well-designed cohort/group study |
| Level V | Moderate | Systematic review of qualitative studies |
| Level VI | Moderate | Single qualitative study |
| Level VII | Weakest | Expert opinion, textbooks, credible websites, encyclopedias |

### Critical Appraisal (Evaluate the Findings)
- **Validity**: Does the evidence measure what it claims?
- **Reliability**: Are results consistent and reproducible?
- **Applicability**: Can it be applied?

### Implementing Recommendations
Factors to consider: Finances, Time, Training, Attitudes, Theory-practice gap

### Disseminating Results
Sharing results of the evaluation of the intervention with others

### Types of Nursing Research
1. **Quantitative**: Using numerical data to evaluate outcomes; numbers, counts, measures analyzed with statistics; often divided into Intervention vs. Control groups
2. **Qualitative**: Seeks to increase understanding, describe, explore, or illuminate healthcare issues; narrative in nature; smaller sample sizes
3. **Mixed Methods**: Combines qualitative studies with quantitative designs; creates a holistic answer
4. **Translational**: [applying evidence to practice]

### Using EBP in Nursing Care Delivery
- **Clinical Practice Guidelines**: Evidence-based recommendations for medical management of disease and preventative care
- **Critical Pathways**: Clinical tools within healthcare organizations using EBP guidelines to manage care delivery
- **Standards of Care**: Reflect typical interventions for a client with a specific disorder

**Citations (Chapter 9) — 4 elements of a reference:**
- Author, Date, Title, Source

**Journal Article format:**
Author, A. & Author, B. (date). Title of article. *Title of Journal, Volume*(Number), pages. DOI
Example: D'Antonio, P., et al. (2010). Histories of nursing. *Nursing Outlook, 58*(4), 207-213.

### AUCON Mission and Vision
- **Mission**: Advance health and healthcare for all, through excellence in practice, teaching, research, and outreach
- **Vision**: Be nationally recognized as leaders in health, addressing the needs of diverse populations

### State Regulation
- Healthcare organizations employ managers/directors to ensure state regulation compliance through policies and procedures
- States regulate how practitioners operate via Nurse Practice Acts and Standards of Nursing Practice

### Accreditation
- Healthcare facilities accredited by outside organizations (assures policies/procedures maintain quality and safety)
- **The Joint Commission (TJC)**:
  - Performs survey of documentation, policies, interviews with administration and staff
  - Provides guidance on changing/improving practices
  - Full accreditation given for 3 years

### Chain of Command
Organizational hierarchy identifying lines of authority: CEO → Nursing Director → Unit Director → Charge Nurse → RN → CNA/AP

**AUCON Chain of Command:**
- Academic Issues: Dean → Associate Dean → Course Leader → Student Success Coach
- Progression Problems: Academic Advisor → Student Success Coach
- Clinical Issues: Clinical Course Leader → Clinical Instructor

### Medicare
**Qualifications:**
- Age 65 or older
- Younger than 65 with disabilities
- Any age with end-stage renal disease (ESRD)

**Parts:**
1. **Part A**: Hospitalization, skilled nursing facilities, some hospice/home health
2. **Part B**: Doctor's services, physical/occupational therapy, some home health
3. **Part D**: Prescription drugs

### Medicaid
**Qualifications:** Based on income relative to poverty level; administered by states and federal government

**Must supply:**
- Inpatient/outpatient hospital services
- Physician services
- Laboratory and x-ray services
- Home health services

**Can choose to supply:** Prescription drug coverage, case management, physical/occupational therapy

### Private Insurance
- Not provided by a government agency
- Often employer-provided
- Complex rules: prior authorization, location restrictions, coverage limits
- Examples: Blue Cross Blue Shield, Cigna, Aetna, HMOs

### Shared Governance
- Shared-decision structure giving nurses control over their own practice
- Guides decisions toward accountability and ownership of clinical outcomes
- Takes nurses from point of care to the decision-making table

**Principle 1:** Grounded in accountability (AUCON Governance, AACN Essentials, Accreditation, Alabama State Board of Nursing)
**Principle 2:** Decision-making designed around accountability to populations served (Student/Faculty Handbook, AU Handbook)
**Principle 3:** Structures demonstrate shared decision-making (Faculty/Student Committees: Technology, Curriculum, Simulation, Evaluation, Outreach, Diversity & Inclusion, SGA, Student Organizations)

### Collaborative Health Care
Client-centered approach in which members of different health care professions work together toward the common goal of improving or restoring a client's health

### Interprofessional Team (IPE Team)
2 or more professionals working together

**Benefits:**
- Improved access to and coordination of health care services
- Greater efficiency of client referral and care services
- Increased quality of community health services
- Decreased complications, length of stay, sentinel events, mortality, and staff turnover

### IPEC — Interprofessional Education Collaborative
- **Values and Ethics**: Maintain a climate of shared value, ethical conduct, and mutual respect
- **Roles and Responsibilities**: Use knowledge of one's role and team expertise to address health outcomes
- **Communication**: Communicate in a responsive, responsible, respectful, and compassionate manner
- **Teams and Teamwork**: Apply values and principles of teamwork science to adapt in various settings

### When to Use the IPE Team
- **Rounding**: Whole team walks through unit together to check on patients
- **Consultation**: One provider formally requests another's input/advice — original physician still manages patient
- **Referral**: Provider delegates responsibility of management to another provider entirely
- **Discharge Planning**: Begins upon admission; whole team determines what patient needs to go home
  - Use teach-back to ensure understanding
  - Key discharge teaching: medications, dietary/activity limits, complications to report, treatments, follow-up appointments/home care

### IPE Team Members
| Role | Scope |
|------|-------|
| APRN/NP | Can diagnose, prescribe meds, manage patient care independently or alongside physicians |
| Case Manager | Helps with discharge planning, insurance, connecting patients to resources |
| Dietitian | Manages nutritional needs (diabetes, eating disorders, kidney disease, post-surgery) |
| LPN | Works under RN; cares for stable patients; dressing changes, suctioning, catheterization, medications |
| Occupational Therapist | Helps patients regain ability to perform daily activities (bathing, dressing, cooking) after illness/injury |
| EMT | Pre-hospital emergency care and transport |
| Pharmacist | Medication expert; checks drug interactions, dosing errors; counsels patients |
| Physical Therapist | Focuses on mobility, strength, rehabilitation |
| Physician | Diagnoses; directs overall medical treatment plan |
| Physician Assistant | Similar to NP; works under physician supervision to diagnose/treat |
| Respiratory Therapist | Manages breathing treatments, ventilators, oxygen therapy, airway management |
| Registered Nurse | Assesses patients, develops care plans, administers medications, educates patients, coordinates care |
| Social Worker | Addresses social/emotional/financial barriers; housing, abuse situations, mental health referrals, discharge needs |
| Assistive Personnel (CNA/AP) | Performs basic non-invasive care tasks delegated by RN; cannot do assessments; can gather vitals |

### Delegation
- A nurse assigning a task to another person; standardized with clear scope-of-practice guidelines; RN remains accountable

**5 Rights of Delegation:**
1. **Right Task**: Is this task appropriate to delegate? Must be within the other person's scope of practice
2. **Right Circumstance**: Is the patient stable enough to delegate?
3. **Right Person**: Are you delegating to someone with proper training and competence?
4. **Right Directions/Communication**: Give clear, specific instructions
5. **Right Supervision/Evaluation**: RN must follow up to confirm task was done correctly and assess outcome

**Who to Delegate to:**
- **Assistive Personnel (CNA/AP)**: Basic care, no assessment, routine tasks, non-invasive procedures (skin care, ROM exercises, ambulation, grooming, hygiene)
- **LPN**: Everything the CNA/AP does plus some invasive tasks (dressing changes, suctioning, urinary catheterization, medication administration); can collect data and review teaching plans created by RN

### Care Transitions and Continuity of Care
- Increasingly complex; helped by EHRs
- When a patient moves from one level/setting of care to another (shift change, hospital to home, ER to inpatient)
- EHR is the main tool supporting continuity — all providers can access same up-to-date patient information

### Communication with Healthcare Team Members
**Breakdowns occur during:**
- Client handoffs, staff/shift changes, failing to establish clear responsibilities

**To improve:**
- Closed-loop communication (receiver repeats message back)
- Clear, concise information
- Standardized communication tools (ISBARR)

### AUCON ISBARR Format
- **I** — Introduction: Who you are, your role, where you're calling from
- **S** — Situation: What is happening with the patient right now; reason for the call
- **B** — Background: Relevant history, diagnosis, medications, allergies, recent labs/vitals
- **A** — Assessment: Your clinical judgment; what you think is going on
- **R** — Recommendation: What you are asking the provider to do; be specific
- **R** — Read Back/Questions: Repeat any orders back to the provider to confirm accuracy; ask for questions

### Handoff Report
**Should include:** Diagnosis/reason for admission, allergies, medications/last dose, current status/recent vitals/assessment findings, anticipated needs during next shift, current plan of care

**Should NOT include:** Personal feelings about patient or family; things that don't directly impact patient care

### Receiving and Transcribing Provider Orders
Orders received on phone, face-to-face, or through EHR. Read back required for: numbers, names, quantities, frequency, route

### Barriers to Collaboration
- Hierarchy of professions — feeling unable to speak up to higher-ranked members
- Lack of knowledge of team members' roles/scope
- Poor communication — vague, incomplete communication leads to misunderstandings and errors especially during handoffs/transitions
- Lack of trust in competence of others — team members may withhold tasks, refuse to collaborate, or redo work unnecessarily
- Lack of cultural competency
- Inability to resolve conflicts
- Structural factors such as time

### Incivility, Lateral Violence, Bullying
- **Incivility**: Gossiping, spreading rumors, being rude/discourteous, or refusing to help a coworker
- **Lateral Violence**: From peer to peer
- **Vertical Violence**: Between a supervisor and subordinate, or other way
- **Bullying**: Recurring behavior involving unwelcome actions intended to harm, humiliate, or distress another

### Standards of Professional Practice

- Standards of Practice

### State Practice Acts
- Current laws/regulations governing nursing practice in every state and US territory
- Identifies conditions required for licensure
- Vary by state with some universal terms

### Scope of Practice
- Created to alleviate client suffering and promote client healing through competent nursing practice
- Describes: Who, What, Where, When, Why, How
- Specific tasks, procedures, and responsibilities RNs are allowed to perform in any healthcare environment

### ANA Standards of Practice (Nursing Process)

2. Diagnosis
3. Outcomes Identification
4. Planning
5. Implementation
   - 5A: Coordination of Care
   - 5B: Health Teaching and Health Promotion
6. Evaluation

7. Ethics | 8. Culturally Congruent Practice | 9. Communication | 10. Collaboration
11. Leadership | 12. Education | 13. Evidence-Based Practice and Research
14. Quality of Practice | 15. Professional Practice Evaluation | 16. Resource Utilization | 17. Environmental Health

### Nursing Students
- Regulated by State Board of Nursing
- Accreditation by ACEN and CCNE

### QSEN — Quality and Safety Education for Nursing
Based on IOM core competencies. Six nurse-specific competencies:
1. Patient-Centered Care
2. Teamwork and Collaboration
3. Evidence-Based Practice
4. Quality Improvement
5. Safety
6. Informatics

### NLN — National League for Nursing Core Values
1. Human Flourishing
2. Nursing Judgment
3. Spirit of Inquiry
4. Professional Identity

### NCLEX and Licensure
- NCSBN creates the NCLEX (National Council Licensure Examination)
- Standardized, adaptive exam to determine if a candidate is safe to practice as an entry-level nurse
- After passing state Board of Nursing regulatory requirements, candidate gets authorization to test

### Code of Ethics
- Ideals, duties, and obligations nurses are expected to uphold
- Applies to licensed nurses AND nursing students
- Designated by ANA's Code of Ethics
- Provisions 1-3: Fundamental values of the nursing profession
- Provisions 4-6: Duty and loyalty to clients and themselves
- Provisions 7-9: Duty and loyalty beyond direct client encounters and to the nursing profession

### Ethical Principles
| Principle | Definition |
|-----------|------------|
| Autonomy | Nurse's obligation to respect the client's right to make their own decisions, including right to refuse care |
| Beneficence | Nurse's obligation to minimize harm and practice in a way that benefits the client; going above what is required |
| Nonmaleficence | Nurse's obligation to "do no harm"; do the benefits outweigh the harm? |
| Veracity | Nurse's obligation to provide truthful and accurate information |
| Fidelity | Nurse's obligation to demonstrate loyalty, keep promises, and uphold commitments |
| Justice | Nurse's obligation to provide impartial, fair, equitable treatment regardless of age, sex, race, or economic status |

### Values and the Nursing Profession
- **Altruism**: Selfless concern and actions for benefit of someone else; requires empathy; being a patient advocate
- **Human Dignity**: Intrinsic value of each individual regardless of race, religion, gender, sexual orientation, culture, ethnicity, socioeconomic status — maintained through privacy, respect, culturally competent care, confidentiality
- **Integrity**: Demonstrating honesty even when circumstances are challenging

### Ethical Decision Making
- Ethical dilemmas occur when nurse's personal values differ from the client's
- Important to stick to the Code of Ethics regardless of personal beliefs

### Ethical Issues Faced by Nurses
Stem cell research, late termination of pregnancy, medically assisted death, refusal/termination of care, substance use disorders, abuse situations, clients in legal custody, allocation of scarce resources

### Informed Consent
- Permission for care given by client after relevant information is provided
- Must be given voluntarily, without coercion
- Given by client or legal representative

### Implied Consent
- Client not required to express permission verbally or in writing
- Client can withdraw at any time

### Advanced Directives
Legal documents documenting client's healthcare decisions. Must provide documents, but client does not have to complete them.
- **Living Will**: Document where client states which life-sustaining treatments they wish if unable to make decisions
- **Durable Power of Attorney**: Client appoints an individual to make healthcare decisions if unable to do so

### Good Samaritan Laws
- Nurses have no legal obligation to provide care outside of employment obligations
- Laws protect and encourage people to provide aid in emergencies without fear of liability for well-intentioned actions

### Mandatory Reporting
Required to report maltreatment: physical, neglect/denial of care, verbal/emotional, sexual, financial exploitation, educational neglect, lack of protection from harm

### Reportable Diseases (to CDC)
Anthrax, Botulism, Chlamydia, Foodborne disease/outbreak, Hepatitis A-C, HIV, Influenza, Measles (Rubeola), Tuberculosis, Zika virus

### Incident Reporting
- **Sentinel Event**: Occurrence resulting in permanent disability, temporary severe injury, or death of a client
- Report: Any event outside routine care, workplace violence, near-miss events, adverse events, sentinel events

### Legal Implications — Workforce Issues
- **Client Abandonment**: Deserting a client for whom nurse-client relationship has been established without ensuring care continuation
- Staffing ratios
- Delegation

### Breach of Nursing Practice — Falsification of Health Records
When nurse documents inaccurate/incomplete/misleading data or fails to document required data.
Examples: Not documenting assessment, not documenting IPE communication, inaccurate assessment data, documenting care that didn't occur
