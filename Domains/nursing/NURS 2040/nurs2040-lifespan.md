## Infants (Birth–12 Months)
*Chapter 17 — Sources: infants-part-1.pdf, infants-part-2.pdf, infants-part-3.pdf*

### Overview
- Totally reliant on caretakers
- Developmental landmarks guide anticipatory guidance to parents

### Biology & Genetics
**Growth:**
- Height: increases ~50% by year 1
- Weight: doubles by 6 months, triples by 1 year
- Head circumference: reflects brain growth — measured at every visit
- Males: larger, more muscle mass, more motor activity
- Females: smaller but more physically mature at birth

**Developmental milestones (key):**
- 1 mo: notices faces
- 2 mo: smiles, holds head up, begins to follow objects
- 3–4 mo: distinguishes faces, follows objects fluidly
- 5 mo: rolls over with support
- 6 mo: starts creeping; eyes fully mature
- 7 mo: enjoys simple games
- 8 mo: begins crawling, stands with support
- 9 mo: pincer grasp, looks for hidden objects
- 10 mo: recognizes parents' faces → Separation Anxiety begins
- 11 mo: separation anxiety peaks
- 12 mo: first words, starts using things correctly

*(See Table 17.3 p. 410 hard copy)*

**Genetics:**
- Birth defect = abnormality of structure, function, or metabolism due to genetic or environmental issue
- Maternal age >35 → increased Down syndrome risk
- Eastern European Jews: Tay-Sachs disease
- Black population: Sickle cell anemia
- Hemophilia, mental retardation: X-linked
- Reproductive history red flags: spontaneous abortions, stillbirths, prior children with genetic conditions
- Maternal diseases: diabetes mellitus, seizure disorders increase risk
- Nurse's role: case finding, referral, family education during genetic counseling

### Gordon's Functional Health Patterns

**Nutritional-Metabolic:**
- Breast milk or formula first 6 months; solids at 6 months
- No cow's milk before 1 year
- Essential nutrients: water, protein (<20% — immature kidneys), CHO/fat (40–50% calories), vitamins/minerals
- Vitamin D supplement if breastfed; iron stores last 4–6 months → iron fortify by 6 months
- Vit C enhances iron absorption
- Fluoride: not recommended until age 6 months (check water source)
- **Breastfeeding: Breast is BEST** — exclusive preferred first 6 months, continue through year 1
  - Healthy People 2030: MICH-15 (exclusive BF through 6 mo), MICH-16 (BF at 1 yr)
- Solid food introduction at 6 months (lowers risk of allergies/obesity)
  - Sequence: cereal → fruits → vegetables → meats (Box 17.5)
- **Caution:** No honey (botulism); no choking hazards (grapes, raw carrots, popcorn, hot dogs, peanut butter); common allergens — eggs, peanuts, tree nuts, fish
- Baby bottle syndrome → tooth decay; avoid propping bottles (aspiration risk)
- Weaning: gradual, age 5–6 months; extrusion reflex must be absent; infant can sit slightly supported

**Elimination:**
- Breastfed stool: softer, mushy, golden yellow, clean/sour smell
- Bottle-fed stool: firmer, pasty, brown, smellier
- Voiding: 6–12 times/day first few months; involuntary
- Bladder sensation develops in 2nd year → avoid toilet training until ~18 months

**Activity-Exercise:**
- Solitary, repetitive play
- Parental stimulation: singing, music, rocking, mirrors, face-to-face interaction
- Benefits: language, cognitive, social, IQ development

**Sleep-Rest:**
- Newborn: 21–22 hours/day → decreases to 12 hours by 12 months
- Brief night arousals are normal; bedtime rituals helpful
- ABC: **Alone, Back, Crib** — safe sleep rules
- 1 hour before bed: quiet time; dark and quiet room

**SIDS (Sudden Infant Death Syndrome):**
- 3rd leading cause of infant death; must occur before 12 months
- Most occur before 6 months
- Risk factors: prone sleeping, tobacco smoke exposure, soft surfaces, hyperthermia, bed sharing, lack of breastfeeding, SIDS sibling, preterm birth
- **Safe to Sleep Campaign**: supine position → significantly reduced SIDS
  - Increased occipital flattening → supervised tummy time when awake; alternate head position
- ABC rule: Alone, Back, Crib

**Cognitive-Perceptual:**
- Vision: initially unfocused → fixed gaze/follows at 1–3 mo → coordinated by 3 mo → mature by 6 mo → looks for hidden objects at 9 mo
- Hearing: fully developed; 1–3 mo turns to sounds/startle reflex; 3–6 mo responds to mom/dad voice
- Smell: fully developed; differentiates mother's milk odor at 2 weeks
- Taste: present at birth; salivation at 3 months; prefers sweets
- Touch: well developed; touch relieves tension, speeds neuromuscular development; skin-to-skin, infant massage, cuddling
- Language: cooing at 2 mo, babbling at 6 mo, first words at ~12 months

**Self-Perception:**
- Separating "me" from "non-me"
- Differentiates self in mirror at 4 months
- Parental interaction becomes infant's view of self

**Erikson — Trust vs. Mistrust:**
- Birth–18 months
- Trust: needs consistently met → influences all future relationships
- Mistrust: if needs not met → anxiety and mistrust

**Piaget — Sensorimotor Period:**
- Learn through senses and motor activity
- Mastering simple coordination
- Object permanence developing

**Roles-Relationships:**
- Attachment and bonding: skin-to-skin is key
- Freud: mother meets all infant "desires"
- Social Learning Theory: meet needs, decrease discomfort, increase pleasure
- Difficulties → child abuse, failure-to-thrive, behavior problems
- Paternal engrossment: father also needs time with baby
- Separation Anxiety: peaks ~8–10 months
  - Protest → Despair → Withdrawal (when separated)

**Coping-Stress:**
- Developmental "crises" are necessary for growth (e.g., frustration with crawling)
- Situational: Separation Anxiety; little initial coping ability, learns over time

### Child Abuse
- All forms of physical/emotional ill-treatment, sexual abuse, neglect, exploitation resulting in actual or potential harm
- Most common under age 2
- Women abuse more frequently; men more severely / sexual abuse
- Intergenerational cycle
- 1 in 7 children/infants are victims; 1,700 children die from abuse/neglect
- Abused children commonly become abusing parents

### Environmental Safety

**Leading causes of unintentional injury death:**
Motor vehicle crash | Falls | Poisoning | Suffocation | Drowning | Fire and Burns

**Falls:** most common after 4 months (rolling); never leave unattended on raised surface

**Burns:** deaths from smoke/toxic gases

**Choking:** grapes, hot dogs, balloons, popcorn, peanut butter — childproof environment (Box 17.12)

**Car seats:**
- Rear-facing as long as possible (infants)
- Forward-facing with harness next
- Booster with 3-point restraint ages 8–12
- All children <13 yrs: back seat
- Heat stroke risk: 15–30 min unattended → ambient 86°F → car interior 120–140°F; **"Look before you leave"**

**Chemical Agents:**
- Meds: leading cause of child poisoning (1 child every 10 min)
- Poison Control: **1-800-222-1222**
- Childproof packaging, high shelf/locked cabinet, never throw in home trash
- Cleaners, household supplies: keep locked
- Many plants are poisonous

**Toxins:**
- Pesticide exposure: foods/produce
- Lead: affects CNS, effects irreversible; lead paint used before 1977 (paint chips sweet); also in water, old toys, dirt, dust, lead-glazed pottery

**Biological Agents:**
- Unavoidable bacteria and viruses; immunizations help
- Indoor pollutants: kerosene, oil, gas, tobacco; damp carpets, cleaners
- Infants breathe more O2 relative to body weight than adults → more susceptible

**Radiation:**
- Natural: soil, water, air
- Human-made: microwaves, electronics, X-rays
- UV exposure → immune system alteration → immunosuppression as adult

### Immunizations
**Schedule:**
| Age | Vaccines |
|-----|----------|
| Birth | Hep B |
| 1 mo | Hep B |
| 2 mo | DTaP, Hib, PCV, IPV, RV |
| 4 mo | DTaP, Hib, PCV, IPV, RV |
| 6 mo | DTaP, Hib, PCV, IPV, RV, Hep B, Influenza |
| 12–15 mo | Hib, MMR, RV, Hep A, Varicella |
| 15–18 mo | DTaP |

- Active immunity: body makes antibodies (e.g., MMR)
- Passive immunity: transient maternal antibodies passed in utero
- Flu vaccine recommended >6 months
- Defer vaccines if moderate/severe illness
- Adverse reactions: local (redness), systemic (fever), allergic (anaphylaxis — epinephrine)
- Egg allergy → precaution with inactivated flu vaccine
- Thimerosal-free options available

**Abbreviations:** Hep B, DTaP (diphtheria/tetanus/acellular pertussis), Hib (Haemophilus influenza), PCV (pneumococcal), IPV (inactivated polio), RV (rotavirus), MMR (measles/mumps/rubella), VAR (varicella), Hep A

**Healthy People 2030:** increase vaccination rates; reduce children with no vaccines by age 2; maintain MMR coverage in kindergarten

### HIV/AIDS
- Transmission: pregnancy, delivery, breastfeeding
- Symptoms usually first year: infections, developmental delays, failure to thrive
- Nurse's role: education, prevention of transmission

### Pain Assessment
- **FLACC Scale** for nonverbal infants/children and nonverbal adults
  - F = Face, L = Legs, A = Activity, C = Cry, C = Consolability
- **FACES Scale** for verbal children
- EBP: sugar water for babies <6 months old during immunizations → distraction

### Nursing Application
- Primary role: educating families
- Anticipatory guidance to parents
- Routine well visits and immunizations
- Health promotion for optimal development
- Accident prevention and safety
- Infection control (good handwashing)
- Support evidence-based care (EBP)

## Toddlers (1–3 Years)
*Chapter 18 — Source: nurs_2040-toddlers.pdf*

> Note: The toddler PDF is largely image-based (handwritten slides). Limited text was extractable. Key content captured below.

### Overview
- Age: 1–3 years
- Motility begins; developing sense of self
- "Terrible twos" — normal developmental stage

### Growth
- Height: grows ~2–4 inches/year
- Weight: gains ~4–6 lbs/year; birthweight quadrupled by 2.5 years
- Respirations: 25–30 breaths/min at age 2; 25 breaths/min at age 3
- Small upper respiratory tract → susceptible to obstruction

### Developmental Theories
- **Erikson:** Autonomy vs. Shame & Doubt (ages 1–3)
- **Piaget:** Preoperational stage begins
- Bowel control happens before urinary control
- Toilet training readiness: ~18 months (physiologic readiness)

### Safety — Unintentional Injuries
**Major hazards:** Falls, drowning, burns, motor vehicle injuries, choking

**Car seat:** Forward-facing with harness; rear-facing as long as possible

**Drowning:** Fencing around pools; supervision; flotation; swim lessons

**Burns:** Hot tap water — set water heater to 120°F; smoke detectors; keep away from flames

**Falls:** Gates on stairs; anchor furniture; lock cabinets

**Helmet** use starts with transition to bike/tricycle

**Car heat stroke:** Temp can become dangerous in 15–30 min; "Look before you leave"

**Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222**
- Lead poisoning: affects CNS, irreversible; check blood levels
- Lead sources: old paint, furniture, toys, houses built before 1977
- Medications: leading cause of child poisoning; 10x more toxic at certain doses

### Daycare / Social
- Illness can spread quickly in daycare centers
- Accreditation important; unannounced inspections
- Culturally competent, values-sensitive care

### Poverty
- Head Start program; Medicaid; SNAP nutrition assistance
- Mandatory safety regulations; services for handicapped children

### Immunizations
- Hepatitis B series continues
- DTaP boosters
- Development screening: assess for ASD (M-CHAT)

## Pre-Schoolers (3–5 Years)
*Chapter 19 — Source: Pre-schoolers-2040-1.pdf*

### Overview
- Age: 3–5 years; kindergarten entry
- More developed body structure
- Increasing language skills
- Mastery of self as independent; extends experience beyond family
- Many Healthy People 2030 goals aimed at preschoolers

### Biology & Genetics
- Protuberant "toddler belly" disappears
- Pelvis straightens; mild in-toeing may remain; transient genu valgum (knock-knees), flat feet
- Growth: 4 lbs and 2 inches per year
- Skin matures; eccrine glands functional; apocrine glands (axilla/groin) not yet functional
- Teeth: finish primary teeth; secondary tooth eruption begins
- Kidneys/GI: fully mature; voluntary control of urinary tract
- Heart/lungs: increased capacity
- Ears: change shape → less otitis media
- Tonsils/adenoids: large/enlarged
- Immune system: boosted response
- Musculoskeletal/neuro: fine and gross motor skill advances; abdominal muscles develop
- Boys: more illnesses and accidents than girls
- Most common genetic diagnoses: Cystic fibrosis, Duchenne muscular dystrophy, Fragile X syndrome

### Gordon's Functional Health Patterns

**Health Perception:**
- Curious about body (opposite sex)
- Vague/inaccurate ideas of internal body
- Anxiety: fear of mutilation and death
- View pain/illness as punishment
- Family influence on health beliefs
- Upset over minor injuries
- Contribute to health management (tooth brushing)

**Nutritional-Metabolic:**
- MyPlate (replaced pyramid): variety of foods
- Iron, calcium intake important
- Limit fat and sugar; half plate vegetables
- Food preferences/refusals may cause conflict
- Learning utensil use; helping with meal prep
- Begin eating away from home (preschool requirements)
- Oral health: fluoride toothpaste, fluoridated water, dental sealants
- Food allergies: most develop before age 2; high-risk: milk, eggs, peanuts
  - Allergy prevention, exposure prevention, action plan

**Elimination:**
- Toilet training: developmentally ready at ~18 months; must be physically ready
- Insist on performing skill independently when ready
- Supervise handwashing and flushing
- Soiling/accidents: no punishment; handle gently; preschooler responsible for changing clothes

**Activity-Exercise:**
- 3–4 yo: **Associative play** (like parallel, but child starts engaging with others — e.g., both building blocks, may borrow from each other)
- 4–5 yo: **Cooperative play** (organized games with roles and rules, working toward shared goal)
- Imitation play (lawn mower toy, vacuum) — learning to be social
- Fantasy play: imaginary friends/scenarios
- Screen time: limit to 1 hour/day
  - Disengages mind, less learning, less active → obesity risk
- Socialization via group activities; requires outlets for high energy

**Sleep-Rest:**
- 8–12 hours/night; nap or "quiet time"
- Bedtime ritual (storytime); be firm and consistent
- Night terrors: not fully awake, no recall in morning
- Nightmares: frightening dreams, can describe and recall
- Help child understand "real" vs "pretend"

**Cognitive-Perceptual — Piaget Preoperational Stage (ages 3–5):**
- Ability to function symbolically with language
- Concrete thought processes; still egocentric
- No conservation (quantity stays same regardless of shape/container)
- Animism: belief that inanimate objects have feelings/intentions
- Magical thinking: belief that own thoughts/wishes can influence external events
- Beginning to internalize family rules; emerging sense of time

**Vision:**
- Amblyopia (infancy to age 4): lazy eye
- Refractive errors; myopia (nearsighted) → glasses
- Strabismus (crossed-eyes): cover test
- Color blindness can be diagnosed by this age
- **Snellen E chart**: visual acuity screening for preschoolers who can't read letters
- **Ishihara's test**: color blindness screening

**Hearing/Language:**
- Audiometry screening for speech delays
- Receptive/expressive skill development
- Speech maturation: watch for fluency issues
- Memory enhanced by grouping items, verbally naming, rehearsal

**Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD):**
- CDC: 1 in 68 children on spectrum
- 4–5 times more common in boys
- Prevalence increasing (NOT caused by vaccines)
- Genetically determined; rarely nongenetic factors
- Risk factors: atypical birth weight, low Apgar, neonatal jaundice, respiratory distress, advanced parental age
- **M-CHAT** (Modified Checklist for Autism in Toddlers): screening tool, normally around 18 months

**Self-Perception — Erikson: Initiative vs. Guilt (ages 3–6):**
- Sense of initiative: vigorous activity and active imagination
- Develops sense of purpose
- Guilt if child is overly criticized or restricted
- Parental role: promote initiative; nurture ideas; encourage positive behaviors; let kids take lead in play

**Roles-Relationships:**
- Peers becoming increasingly significant
- Children begin to internalize societal gender roles
- Older siblings serve as role models
- Play with peers → learn to negotiate rules, take turns, share
- Preparing for group situations, following directions, demonstrating independence
- Parental divorce → regression, confusion, irritability common; need closeness, patience, consistency
- Child abuse: complex factors; family stressors as trigger; mandatory reporting

**Sexuality-Reproductive:**
- Gender identification
- Curiosity about bodies/sexual functioning → answer simply and factually
- Many good children's books available as resource

**Coping-Stress:**
- Better temperament than toddler
- More problem-solving abilities
- Use of fantasy; fewer tantrums
- Gentle, consistent adult direction recommended

**Values-Beliefs:**
- Age 4–5: some internal controls developing
- Moving from specific to general rules
- Lack fully developed conscience
- Control behavior to retain parental love/approval
- Fascinated by life/death concepts → needs support, possible counseling for loss

### Environmental Safety
- Injuries: leading cause of death
- MVA: car seats; back seat; forward-facing car seat with 5-point harness → transition to booster when Ht/Wt indicated
- Household furniture/tools/equipment hazards
- Firearm safety: child access protection laws
- **Burns:** scald/direct flame; teach danger of matches, hot objects; set bathtub temp
- **Drowning:** swimming pool greatest risk for preschooler (bathtub less risky after age 3)
  - Fencing, flotation device, supervision, water survival lessons

**Biological Agents:**
- Immunization boosters:
  - DTaP: 4th year
  - MMR: between ages 4–6
- Religious/safety exemptions exist; catch-up schedule available

**Chemical Agents:**
- Lead: folk remedies, old paint, pottery
- Poisonings: >half occur in children <6 years
  - Solids: pills, vitamins, aspirin, lipstick
  - Liquids: cleaning products, fuel, alcohol
  - Sprays: furniture polish, oven cleaner
  - Invisibles: carbon monoxide, space heater fumes

**Cancers in Preschool Children:**
- **Acute lymphocytic leukemia (ALL):** most common childhood cancer; incidence rises ages 2–5
- **Wilms' tumor:** <age 5; kidney; correlated with congenital genitourinary anomalies
- **Retinoblastoma:** <age 5; 1/3 genetic mutation; detected by absence of red reflex
- **Neuroblastoma:** <age 2; sympathetic nervous system; often in adrenal gland
- Nurse's role: identify significant history/physical findings; secondary prevention; support to child/family

**Asthma:**
- More rapid increase in preschoolers than other ages
- Higher in Black and Hispanic populations
- Genetic predisposition + allergens
- Triggers: infections, exercise, weather, stress, smoke, pollutants, allergens, dust mites, pet/rodent dander, cockroach particles
- Poverty significantly contributes to disability and death

### Determinants of Health
- Home vs. preschool: more social contacts; skill development for group interaction
- Culture/ethnicity: family culture may differ from playmates → confusion
- Health care: EPSDT (Early and Periodic Screening, Diagnosis and Treatment) — Medicaid program; ACA ensures coverage; CHIP for all children

### Nursing Application
- Preschoolers interested in tools, procedures, equipment
- Explain tests in age-appropriate language; expect cooperation for most of visit
- Play with stethoscope, otoscope
- Include child in history-taking (diet, health habits, friends)
- Parents needed close during injections
- Reduce stress through play

## Adolescents (10–24 Years)
*Chapter 21 — Source: Adolescents.pdf*

### Overview
- Age span: 10–24 years
- Psychosocial, emotional, cognitive, and moral transition from child to adult
- **Puberty:** onset ~11–13 years; signals start of adolescence
  - Females hit puberty ~2 years earlier than males
  - Rapid changes in physical, cognitive, psychosocial, moral aspects
  - African American girls earlier than white girls

### Biology & Genetics

**Physical growth:**
- Accelerated growth spurt
- Most noticeable: secondary sexual characteristics — mediated by hormonal regulation
- Menarche (first period) appears late in puberty

**Primary sexual characteristics (essential for reproduction):**
- Male: penis and testes
- Female: uterus and vagina

**Secondary sexual characteristics:**
- Breast development, facial and pubic hair, voice changes
- **Tanner Staging**: rates sexual maturity/development

**Male puberty signs:**
- Thinning of scrotal sac, enlargement of testes
- Ejaculation (precedes fertility by a few months)
- Nocturnal emissions (wet dreams)

**Female puberty:**
- Breast buds → growth spurt → menstruation appears ~2 years after breast buds

**Cardiac:** heart grows in size → BP increases, HR decreases

**Respiratory:** lung volume and vital capacity increase → respiratory rate decreases; larynx and vocal cords grow → voice changes

**Dental:** all permanent teeth by 13–14 yrs; third molars (wisdom teeth) appear, often problematic

**Skin:**
- Sweat/sebaceous glands become active → body odor (axillary, genital, periumbilical)
- Acne: 80% of all adolescents; topical meds, stress management, soap/water

**Scoliosis:**
- S-shaped curvature of spine
- 10% of adolescents have mild asymmetry
- More common in females
- Untreated → disfigurement, impaired mobility, cardiopulmonary complications
- Screening: if >5 degrees → referral

**Genetic syndromes sometimes diagnosed in adolescence:**
- **Turner syndrome (female):** short stature, webbed neck, low-set ears, infertile, cardiac/renal anomalies, learning disabilities; hormonal therapy
- **Klinefelter syndrome (male):** tall, thin, lack of sexual development, gynecomastia, learning/behavior problems; hormonal therapy; sterile

### Gordon's Functional Health Patterns

**Health Perception:**
- Less acute and chronic illness vs younger ages
- Less often in health facilities
- Sense of invincibility → risk-taking behaviors, experimentation
- Partnering approach: support autonomy, focus on strengths

**Nutritional-Metabolic:**
- Pressure to achieve ideal body image
- Fast foods, soda, sweets, alcohol prevalent
- Increased caloric needs with growth spurt
- Dietary intake as control/independence
- Eating disorders more common in females but occur in males too:
  - **Anorexia nervosa:** distorted body image
  - **Bulimia nervosa:** high calorie intake then purge
  - **Binge eating disorder:** no purging component
- Type 2 Diabetes: increasing prevalence; risk with obesity/inactivity
- Individual wellness plans and body reassurance needed

**Elimination:**
- Renal and GI systems functionally mature
- Abnormal variation with eating disorders
- Privacy issues → may inhibit elimination in public places (e.g., school bathrooms)

**Activity-Exercise:**
- Increase in lean muscle mass → increased strength/endurance
- Regular exercise important; many participate in organized sports
- Preparticipation sports physical = opportunity for health teaching
- Risk for overuse/sports-specific injuries
- Sleep deprivation + insufficient rest between sports events is common

**Sleep-Rest:**
- Need ≥9 hours/night; sleep deprivation is common
- Multiple activities, responsibilities; "too much on their plate"
- Stay up late; early school start; texting/devices after bedtime
- Nurses: help adolescents balance responsibilities

**Cognitive-Perceptual — Piaget: Formal Operations:**
- Abstract thinking groundwork laid
- Introspection; reemergence of egocentrism
- Intolerance of "status quo"
- Risk-taking: "I can get drunk and not develop a problem," "I won't get pregnant"
- More complex thinking; future orientation; ability to delay gratification
- Receptive/expressive vocabulary increases; slang, electronic communication

**Erikson: Identity vs. Role Confusion:**
- Restaging of each previous developmental stage
- Threats to identity → delinquent behavior and mental health issues
- Idealism → rejection of traditional ideas

**Self-Perception:**
- Acne problematic (80% of adolescents)
- Tattoos and body piercing → health risks
- Self-esteem prominent; peer pressure; societal norms
- Anticipatory guidance, education, counseling for healthy choices

**Roles-Relationships:**
- Changing roles: stressful family time
- Increasing independence; parents learning to "let go"
- Positive strategies: listening, affection, acceptance, negotiating limits
- Peers: strong influence; moving from child in family to group member
- Gangs: problematic in urban and rural areas

**Sexuality-Reproductive:**
- Emergence of secondary sexual characteristics
- Sexual experimentation normal
- Heterosexual or same-sex activity
- Guidance needed: sexual decisions, contraception, STI prevention
- Adolescent pregnancy: high-risk maternal/infant outcomes; educational/economic impact
- Hazards: dating violence, LGBTQ issues, sex trafficking, homelessness

**Coping-Stress:**
- Ability to cope tested in adolescence
- **Depression:** depressed/irritable mood or diminished interest and pleasure; assess for signs, refer for treatment
- **Suicide: 2nd leading cause of death ages 10–24** — recognition of warning signs and prevention essential
- NSSI (Non-suicidal self-injury): self-harm behaviors

**Values-Beliefs:**
- Expanded understanding of good/bad
- Kohlberg's moral development: transition to postconventional stage (justice, universal principles)
- Struggling to learn who they are (own sense of self)
- Provide positive role modeling; reinforce positive behaviors

### Environmental Processes

**Accidents/Unintentional Injuries:**
- Motor vehicle accidents: most likely vs other age groups (inexperience, drugs/ETOH, distracted driving)
- Sports injuries: immature coordination, judgment, musculature, epiphyses; performance-enhancing substances
- Violence: weapons, testing limits, witnessing violence → increase in violent behavior

**Biological/Chemical Agents:**
- Infections: infectious mononucleosis, meningococcal disease, STIs, HPV
- Need for immunizations and safe sexual practices
- Alcohol and drug use: ETOH, marijuana, Rx, inhalants, opioids
- Tobacco: e-cigs and vaping on the rise; most start in adolescence
- Focus on preventing non-smokers from starting; helping smokers quit

**Cancer:**
- Continued risk: leukemia, osteogenic sarcoma, lymphomas, CNS tumors
- **Testicular cancer:** affects males ages 15–35
- PAP test for cervical cancer (rare in teens); pelvic exams delayed until age 21
- **HPV vaccine:** prevents 4 types; precursor to cervical cancer and genital warts; recommended for all adolescents (male and female)

### Determinants of Health
- **School:** primary setting for expectations/standards; peer group importance; increased social adjustments
- **Culture/ethnicity:** teens may question, modify, or reject family influences if different from dominant culture; increased challenges for minority adolescents
- **Economics:** poverty and economic pressures create adolescent stress
- **Emancipated minors:** legally permitted to make certain health decisions
- **Confidentiality:** particularly important for sexual issues, STIs

### Nursing Application — HEADSSS Assessment
- **H** = Home
- **E** = Education, employment, eating
- **A** = Activities
- **D** = Drugs
- **S** = Sexuality
- **S** = Suicide/depression
- **S** = Safety

Nursing roles: health promotion, preventive screening, disease prevention, education

## Young Adults (18–35 Years)
*Chapter 22 — Source: Chapter_022-2040 young adults.pdf*

> Note: The file "Chapter_023-2040-middle aged.pdf" was not found in the source folder. Chapter 22 (Young Adults) was the adjacent file available. If middle-age content is needed, it will need to be sourced separately.

### Overview
- Age: 18–35 years
- Transition to adult roles and responsibilities
- Tasks: educational choices, full-time employment, independent home, finding partner, possible parenthood
- Risk-taking behaviors peak
- Lifelong health behaviors emerge

### Biology & Genetics
- Greater complexity of thinking; more educated, more diverse
- Growth completed by age 20; optimal strength 25–30 years → gradually declines
- Period of relatively good health
- Potential hazards: poor eating, STIs, less physical activity, higher drug/ETOH use
- Major milestones delayed vs other generations (45% reached milestones in 1975 vs 24% in 2016)
  - Milestones: marriage, parenthood, full-time work, living independently
- Economic and educational disparities persist
- US maternal mortality rate high vs other developed countries

### Gordon's Functional Health Patterns

**Health Perception:**
- Majority consider health good to excellent
- Transition from pediatric to adult care provider
- High rates of uninsured (age 19–25)
- More likely to engage in risky behavior (men > women)
- Poor eating, binge drinking, sedentary lifestyle
- Prevention is key

**Physical Exam:**
- Height, weight, BMI, blood pressure, blood tests
- Social history: tobacco, alcohol, sexual activity
- Cervical cancer screening: start age 21 → every 3 years; HPV screen every 5 years beginning age 30

**Nutritional-Metabolic:**
- Body-conscious; value thinness, muscle tone, athletic ability
- Abundant food sources; larger portion sizes
- Low-income communities: food deserts
- Female considerations: iron, folic acid, calcium

**Elimination:**
- Patterns well established
- Eating disorders may persist
- Common complaints: constipation, hemorrhoids, occasional diarrhea
- Colorectal cancer rates for young adults 20–39 have increased

**Activity-Exercise:**
- Aerobic exercise recommendations
- Sun/radiation exposure: **melanoma most common cancer ages 25–29** → avoid excessive UV
- Fitness programs, gym memberships popular
- Alternative transportation (biking): helmets decrease head/brain injury
- Accidental deaths from drowning common (swimming, boating, scuba)

**Sleep-Rest:**
- Age 18–21: need 8 hours; age 22+: 7 hours
- Subject to fatigue: work, stress, inactivity, electronics
- Recommendations: consistent schedule; avoid ETOH/caffeine before bed; no electronics 30 min before bed; moderate to vigorous activity

**Cognitive-Perceptual:**
- Executive decision-making better developed: calculating risks vs rewards, prioritization, self-evaluation, long-term planning
- Piaget: intellectual growth; recall peaks at age 20, memory diminishes at age 30
- Erikson: **Intimacy vs. Isolation**

**Self-Perception:**
- Series of transitional events; themes of work and financial independence
- Temporary/reversible exploration vs firm commitments
- Full-time employment and residential independence as milestones

**Roles-Relationships:**
- Social networking prevalent (potential compulsive/addictive use)
- Romantic relationships; childbearing decisions
- Gender/sexual orientation: cisgender, non-binary, transgender; heterosexual, homosexual, bisexual
- LGBTQ+ challenges: legal protection, stigma, harassment/violence, healthcare barriers
- Career decisions; more time in post-secondary education
- Divorce rates declining for younger adults; increasing number do not marry but have children

**Sexuality-Reproductive:**
- Menstrual cycle typically well established
- Infertility: lack of conception for 12 months; can be male or female; ART available
- Unintended pregnancies: half of all pregnancies; barriers to family planning access
- STIs: since 2014 some have increased
  - Chlamydia: annual screening for sexually active women age 24 or younger
  - Gonorrhea: common in women 20–24

**Coping-Stress:**
- Job/school-related stress
- Suicide: 2nd leading cause of death in young adults; depression is risk factor
- Behavioral health integration needed

**Values-Beliefs:**
- Habits, values, beliefs from childhood/adolescence
- Waiting longer to have children
- Genetic testing/counseling concerns

### Environmental Processes
- Racism and discrimination prevalent
- Accidents: leading cause of death — MVA greatest; texting/phone use while driving; driving while impaired
- Noise pollution: hearing loss, stress, sleep disturbances; use ear protection
- Air pollution: respiratory disease, preterm birth
- Occupational hazards and stressors

**Drug Use:**
- Peak illicit drug use in young adulthood
- Opioid crisis: synthetic opioids (fentanyl); naloxone for prevention
- Marijuana: most commonly abused drug in US; health risks: lung/cardiovascular, anxiety/depression, memory/learning deficits

**Alcohol:**
- Alcohol-related accidents: leading cause of death ages 15–24
- Binge drinking very prevalent: 5+ drinks in 2 hrs (male); 4+ (female)
- Heavy use: liver, heart, cancers; contributes to falls, drowning, burns, homicide, suicide

**Tobacco:**
- Leading cause of preventable death in US
- Most start during adolescence
- E-cigs and vaping on rise
- Multiple carcinogens: food, cleaners, lawn care; industrial chemicals

### Health Services
- ACA: 18–34 most likely uninsured group
- Often use ER, urgent care, retail care vs preventive care
- Healthy People 2030: increase young adults with usual primary care provider

### Nursing Application
- Peak physical condition
- Preventive care: health screenings, education to avoid hazards
- Worksite wellness programs, group fitness
- Counseling: STIs, HIV, skin cancer, sports injuries, contraception, prenatal care, smoking cessation, weight loss, exercise
