## Module 1 — Ch. 1: Health Promotion and Prevention

### Definitions of Health
- **WHO:** Complete physical, mental, and social well-being — not merely absence of disease
- **Disease:** Focus only on physiological changes caused by pathology (nurse is "disease-focused" if only addressing physical changes from a diagnosis)
- **Illness:** Subjective experience of not feeling well
- **Health:** A level of functioning influenced by multiple factors (most comprehensive professional definition)
- **Wellness:** Active process of maximizing potential across all dimensions

### Wellness-Illness Continuum
- Ranges from premature death → high-level wellness
- Health is dynamic — moves along the continuum

### Healthy People Framework
- **Healthy People 2020:** 4 overarching goals — attain high quality long lives, achieve health equity, create healthy environments, promote quality of life across all stages
- **Healthy People 2030:** Eliminate health disparities, achieve health equity, promote healthy development/behaviors across all life stages
  - New emphasis: **Engagement in Community** as a core goal

### Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) — 5 Domains
1. Economic stability
2. Education access and quality
3. Health care access and quality
4. Neighborhood and built environment
5. Social and community context

### Social Ecological Model
- Levels: Individual → Interpersonal → Community → Societal
- Health behaviors influenced at all levels simultaneously

### Levels of Prevention
| Level | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Primordial | Prevent risk factors from developing | Healthy community planning |
| Primary | Prevent disease before it occurs | Vaccines, health education |
| Secondary | Early detection/treatment | Screening (Pap smear, BP check) |
| Tertiary | Reduce disability, restore function | Rehab after stroke |
| Quaternary | Prevent over-medicalization | Avoid unnecessary tests/treatment |

### Nurse Roles in Health Promotion
- Educator, advocate, case manager, community organizer, policy advocate
- ANA: nurses are ethically obligated to address SDOH

## Module 1 — Ch. 2: Vulnerable Populations and Health

### Key Concepts
- **Health equity:** Everyone has a fair opportunity to attain highest level of health
- **Health disparity:** Preventable differences in health burden between groups
- **Harm reduction:** Strategies that reduce negative consequences of risky behaviors without requiring abstinence

### Vulnerable Populations

#### Immigrants
- Healthy immigrant effect — arrive healthier, health declines over time
- Barriers: language, documentation status, cultural mistrust, cost

#### Arab Americans
- Fastest growing Muslim population in US
- Barriers: discrimination post-9/11, cultural stigma around mental health, gender roles in healthcare

#### Asian Americans
- Diverse subgroups — not monolithic
- Barriers: language, cultural beliefs (traditional medicine), model minority myth masks health disparities

#### Native Hawaiian / Pacific Islanders
- High rates of obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease
- Barriers: geographic isolation, cultural disconnect with Western medicine

#### Latino / Hispanic
- Fastest growing US population
- Barriers: language, immigration status, low insurance rates, machismo cultural norms

#### Black / African American
- Highest disparities in cardiovascular disease, maternal mortality, infant mortality
- Historical medical mistrust (Tuskegee study)
- Nurse outreach: **Church** is the most effective community access point for predominantly Black communities

#### Native American / Alaska Native
- High rates of diabetes, substance use, suicide
- Barriers: geographic isolation, underfunded IHS, historical trauma

#### LGBT+
- Higher rates of depression, anxiety, substance use, suicide
- Barriers: stigma, discrimination, lack of provider competence, fear of disclosure
- Nurse must not assume heterosexuality; use inclusive language

#### People Experiencing Homelessness (Unhoused)
- **Critical misconception to correct:** Not all unhoused clients struggle with substance abuse — this is a stereotype/bias
- True barriers: lack of address for insurance, no consistent provider, exposure, trauma
- Nurse approach: non-judgmental, trauma-informed, harm reduction model

## Module 1 — Ch. 3: Health Policy and Delivery System

### Historical Context
- **Florence Nightingale:** Environmental theory — clean air, water, sanitation improve health outcomes
- **Lillian Wald:** Founded public health nursing, Henry Street Settlement (1893)
- **WHO (1948):** Defined health as complete physical, mental, social well-being

### US Health System Structure
- **Private sector:** Employer-sponsored insurance, private hospitals
- **Public sector:** Government-funded programs

### Key Insurance Programs
| Program | Population | Key Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Medicare** | Age 65+, certain disabilities | Federal; grandmother just retired = Medicare, NOT Medicaid |
| **Medicaid** | Low-income individuals/families | State + federal; means-tested |
| **ACA (2010)** | Uninsured, marketplace plans | Expanded Medicaid eligibility; removed pre-existing condition exclusions; allowed coverage to age 26 on parent plan |

- Medicare = retirement/age-based
- Medicaid = poverty-based

### US Health System: Strengths and Challenges
**Successes:**
- Advanced technology and research
- High specialization
- Emergency care infrastructure

**Challenges:**
- Highest per-capita spending among developed nations
- High maternal mortality compared to other developed countries
- Lack of universal coverage
- Fragmented care, not prevention-focused

### Nursing Role in Policy
- Nurses must be politically active — largest healthcare workforce
- Can influence policy at local, state, and federal levels
- ANA supports universal access, safe staffing ratios, SDOH addressing

## Module 4 — Ch. 7: Health Promotion and the Family

### Definition of Family
- Two or more people who are joined together by bonds of sharing and emotional closeness who identify themselves as family
- Does not require biological relation or cohabitation

### Family Systems Theory
- Family as a system — each member affects all others
- Change in one member = change in the whole system
- Boundaries: open (flexible, exchange with outside) vs. closed (rigid, isolated)

### Duvall and Miller: Family Development Stages
1. Beginning family (married couple, no children)
2. Early childbearing (oldest child birth–30 months)
3. Family with preschool children (oldest 2.5–6 yrs)
4. Family with school-age children (oldest 6–13 yrs)
5. Family with teenagers (oldest 13–20 yrs)
6. Family launching young adults (first to last child leaves)
7. Middle-age parents (empty nest to retirement)
8. Family in retirement/old age (retirement to death)

### Gordon's 11 Functional Health Patterns — Family Assessment
1. **Health perception–health management:** How family views and manages health; preventive behaviors; use of healthcare
2. **Nutritional-metabolic:** Food preparation, eating habits, nutrition knowledge, special diets
3. **Elimination:** Bowel/bladder patterns, plumbing access, hygiene
4. **Activity-exercise:** Exercise habits, leisure activities, ADL capacity
5. **Sleep-rest:** Sleep schedules, rest quality, sleep environment
6. **Cognitive-perceptual:** Decision-making, communication patterns, sensory/cognitive function
7. **Self-perception–self-concept:** Family identity, self-worth, emotional tone
8. **Roles-relationships:** Role structure, communication, power dynamics, relationship quality
9. **Sexuality-reproductive:** Sexual health, reproductive choices, family planning
10. **Coping–stress tolerance:** Stressors, coping strategies, crisis response
11. **Values-beliefs:** Cultural/religious beliefs, what guides family decisions

### Indicators of Healthy Families
- Open communication
- Shared responsibilities
- Adaptability to change
- Emotional support among members
- Connection to community/social support
- Shared values and rituals

### Structural-Functional Perspective
- Examines family structure (who is in the family) and functions (what family does)
- Functions: affection, socialization, reproduction, economic support, health care

### Risk-Factor Perspective
- Identifies biological, psychological, social, environmental risk factors
- Guides nursing assessment and intervention priorities

### Genogram
- Diagram of family structure across 3+ generations
- Documents: relationships, chronic illnesses, causes of death, patterns of disease
- Used to identify genetic/health risks

### Environmental Factors in Family Assessment
- **Home:** Safety hazards, overcrowding, lead, mold, sanitation
- **Neighborhood:** Violence, access to parks/grocery stores, pollution
- **Community:** Healthcare access, schools, social services

### Nursing Process with Families (APIE)
- **Assessment:** Gordon's 11 patterns + genogram + environmental factors
- **Planning:** Set mutually agreed-upon goals with family
- **Implementation:** Education, referrals, care coordination
- **Evaluation:** Measure outcomes against goals

### 5 Measures of Family Evaluation
1. Goal attainment
2. Functional status
3. Family satisfaction
4. Cost-effectiveness
5. Health behaviors change

### Family Case Study: Celia and Hector Sanchez
- Latino family — cultural considerations in assessment
- Language and acculturation factors
- Role of family support in health management
- Applied Gordon's patterns to real family scenario

## Ch. 15: Overview of Growth and Development Framework

### Life Stages with Age Ranges
| Stage | Age Range |
|---|---|
| Prenatal | Conception to birth |
| Neonate | Birth–1 month |
| Infant | 1 month–1 year |
| Toddler | 1–3 years |
| Preschool | 3–6 years |
| School-age | 6–12 years |
| Adolescent | 12–18 years |
| Young adult | 18–35 years |
| Middle adult | 35–65 years |
| Older adult | 65+ years |

### Growth vs. Development
| Concept | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Growth** | Quantifiable, measurable (height/weight); non-linear; plotted on CDC/WHO growth charts |
| **Development** | Qualitative, functional changes; follows sequential patterns |

### Developmental Trends
- **Cephalocaudal:** Head-to-toe progression (head control before walking)
- **Proximodistal:** Center-to-periphery (trunk control before fine motor)
- **Differentiation:** Simple → complex, general → specific responses

### Learning vs. Maturation
- **Maturation:** Biologically driven, age-related changes (independent of environment)
- **Learning:** Acquired through experience/environment
- Both interact — maturation sets the stage, learning builds on it

### Developmental Theories Overview

#### Erikson — Psychosocial Development (8 Stages)
| Stage | Age | Task vs. Crisis |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Infant | Trust vs. Mistrust |
| 2 | Toddler | Autonomy vs. Shame/Doubt |
| 3 | Preschool | **Initiative vs. Guilt** |
| 4 | School-age | Industry vs. Inferiority |
| 5 | Adolescent | Identity vs. Role Confusion |
| 6 | Young adult | **Intimacy vs. Isolation** |
| 7 | Middle adult | Generativity vs. Stagnation |
| 8 | Older adult | Ego Integrity vs. Despair |

#### Piaget — Cognitive Development
- Sensorimotor (0–2): Object permanence develops
- Preoperational (2–7): Symbolic thinking, egocentrism
- Concrete Operational (7–11): Logical thinking about concrete objects; illness seen as punishment
- Formal Operational (12+): Abstract reasoning

#### Vygotsky — Social Learning
- Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD): what child can do with guidance vs. alone
- Learning is social — language drives cognitive development

#### Kohlberg — Moral Development
- Preconventional: Rules = consequences (punishment/reward)
- Conventional: Rules = social norms/authority
- Postconventional: Universal ethical principles

#### Gilligan — Moral Development (Feminist Critique of Kohlberg)
- Kohlberg's model male-biased
- Female moral development centers on care, relationships, and context — not just justice/rules

## Exam Notes — Pediatric Content (Handwritten Review)

### School-Age (6–12 years)
- Depression prevalence: 2–3%
- External locus of control common
- **Ossification:** Bones hardening — heavy backpacks can cause spinal problems
- Vision: Transition from farsighted (hyperopic) in early childhood toward 20/20
- Cognitive: Concrete operations — understands cause-effect; **illness perceived as punishment**
- **Enuresis:**
  - Primary: Never achieved nighttime continence
  - Secondary: Achieved continence, then regressed (more clinically significant)
- **Tympanogram:** Tests middle ear pressure; used to diagnose chronic otitis media (fluid behind eardrum)
- Depression signs in school-age: social withdrawal, declining grades, somatic complaints, irritability

### Adolescent / Puberty
**Female pubertal sequence:**
1. Breast buds (thelarche)
2. Growth spurt
3. Pubic hair
4. Menarche (typically last major event)

**Male pubertal sequence:**
1. Scrotal sac enlargement
2. Pubic hair and facial hair
3. Nocturnal emissions
4. Ejaculation

**Tanner Staging:** 5 stages of sexual maturation; Stage 1 = prepubertal, Stage 5 = adult

### ADHD Diagnostic Criteria
- Symptoms present for **6+ months**
- Symptoms in **2+ settings** (home AND school)
- Onset **before age 7**
- Impairs functioning in at least one domain

### Learning Styles
- **Kinesthetic:** Learn by doing/movement
- **Auditory:** Learn by hearing
- **Visual:** Learn by seeing

### Preschool (3–6 years)
- **Most common childhood cancer (ages 2–5):** Acute lymphocytic leukemia (ALL)
- **Autism spectrum:** 4–5x more prevalent in males
- **Iron and calcium:** Key nutritional needs in preschool
- **Poisonings:** Most common in children under 6 — household chemicals, medications
- **Retinoblastoma:** Eye cancer; classic sign = **absent red reflex** on fundoscopic exam
- **Erikson stage:** Initiative vs. Guilt

### Vaccines
| Vaccine | Age |
|---|---|
| Meningococcal | 11–12 years |
| HPV | Both genders, 11–12 years |
| Covid-19 | Age 12+ |

## Ch. 22: Young Adult (Ages 18–35)

### Overview
- Age range: 18–35 years
- Period of transition to adult roles and responsibilities
- Risk-taking behaviors peak
- Lifelong health habits emerge (this is when patterns are set)
- Growth completed by age 20; optimal physical strength at 25–30 years, then gradually declines
- US has high maternal mortality compared to other developed countries

### Major Milestones — Delayed Trend
- Traditional milestones: marriage, parenthood, full-time work, living independently
- 1975: 45% of young adults had achieved milestones; 2016: only 24%
- Economic and educational disparities contribute
- Young adults 24–34 without college degrees have poorer cardiovascular health

### Healthy People 2030 for Young Adults
- Focus: health promotion and disease prevention
- Goal: increase proportion of young adults with a usual primary care provider

### Gordon's 11 Functional Health Patterns — Applied to Young Adults

#### 1. Health Perception–Health Management
- Majority self-rate health as good to excellent
- Transition from pediatric to adult care provider
- Insurance: ACA — high uninsured rates age 19–25
- Men > women for unhealthy behaviors
- More likely to engage in risky behavior than previous generations

#### Health Screenings for Young Adults
- Physical exam: height, weight, BMI, BP, blood tests
- Social history: tobacco, alcohol, sexual activity
- Cervical cancer screening: start age 21 (or when sexually active), then every 3 years; HPV screen every 5 years beginning age 30

#### 2. Nutritional-Metabolic
- Body-conscious: value thinness, muscle tone, athletic ability
- Hazards: poor eating habits, food deserts in low-income communities, increased portion sizes
- Female nutritional needs: iron, folic acid, calcium

#### 3. Elimination
- Patterns well-established
- Eating disorders may persist
- Common complaints: constipation, hemorrhoids, occasional diarrhea
- Colorectal cancer rates for ages 20–39 have increased

#### 4. Activity-Exercise
- Aerobic exercise recommended
- Sun/radiation: melanoma most common cancer in ages 25–29; avoid excessive UV
- Sports hazards: extreme sports, drowning (swimming, boating, scuba diving)
- Bike helmets decrease brain and head injury

#### 5. Sleep-Rest
- Need 8 hours (age 18–21); 7 hours (age 22+)
- Subject to fatigue from work, stress, electronics
- Recommendations: consistent schedule, avoid ETOH/caffeine before bed, no electronics 30 min before bed, moderate-vigorous activity

#### 6. Cognitive-Perceptual
- Executive decision-making better developed vs. adolescence
- Recall peaks at age 20; memory begins diminishing at age 30
- Piaget's Theory: intellectual growth (formal operations stage)
- Erikson's Theory: **Intimacy vs. Isolation**

#### 7. Self-Perception–Self-Concept
- Two emotional themes: work and financial independence
- Temporary/reversible phase: exploration and experimentation
- Preparation phase: firm commitments
- Full-time employment and residential independence are key milestones

#### 8. Roles-Relationships
- Social networking: prevalent, can become compulsive/addictive
- Romantic relationships: childbearing decisions influenced by finances, safety, family support
- LGBTQ+: cisgender, non-binary, transgender; challenges = legal protection, stigma, harassment, barriers to healthcare
- Career decisions: more time in post-secondary education; majority are women; undergraduate enrollments declining
- Divorce rates declining for younger adults; increasing numbers have children without marrying

#### 9. Sexuality-Reproductive
- Menstrual cycle typically well-established
- Infertility: lack of conception for 12 months; can be male or female cause; ART available
- Unintended pregnancies: ~50% of all pregnancies are unintended
- STIs: rates increasing since 2014
  - Women 15–19: highest chlamydia rates
  - Women 20–24: highest gonorrhea rates
  - Chlamydia screening: annually for sexually active women age 24 or younger
  - Gonorrhea and chlamydia often occur together (annotation note)

#### 10. Coping–Stress Tolerance
- Assess stress at every health encounter
- Major stressors: job, school, finances
- **Suicide: 2nd leading cause of death in young adults**
- Depression is a significant risk factor for suicide
- Behavioral health integration important

#### 11. Values-Beliefs
- Enter 20s with values/habits from childhood and adolescence
- Parenting decisions delayed
- Prenatal genetic issues: abnormal chromosomes → genetic impairments; genetic testing/counseling available

### Environmental Processes
- Racism and discrimination affect educational and work decisions
- **Accidents: leading cause of death in young adults**
  - Motor vehicle accidents = greatest single cause of death
  - Cell phone use implicated: texting while driving, driving impaired, also causes tripping/falling/colliding

### Biologic Agents
- **Noise pollution:** Traffic, construction, occupational → hearing loss, stress, sleep disturbance; ear protection essential
- **Air pollution:** Automobile, industrial, fires → respiratory disease, higher lipids, preterm birth; climate change worsens
- **Occupational hazards:** Many risks; preventive interventions essential

### Substance Use

#### Drug Use
- Young adulthood = peak illicit drug use period
- Opioid crisis: synthetic opioids (fentanyl); prevention = RX guidelines + naloxone
- Marijuana: most commonly abused drug in US; health risks = lung/cardiovascular disease, anxiety/depression, memory deficits, accidents

#### Alcohol Use
- Alcohol-related accidents: leading cause of death for persons age 15–24
- Contributes to falls, drownings, burns, homicide, suicide
- Heavy use most common in this age group
- **Binge drinking:** 5+ drinks in 2 hours (males); 4+ drinks in 2 hours (females)
- Associated with sexual assault

#### Tobacco
- Leading cause of preventable death in US
- Most start during adolescence
- E-cigs and vaping as emerging forms
- Healthy People 2030: goal to decrease tobacco use

### Carcinogens
- Sources: food, household cleaners, lawn care, industrial chemicals
- Cancer risk factors: multiple sex partners, tobacco, heavy ETOH, industrial chemical exposures

### Social Factors
- Challenges: transportation, housing, health coverage
- Young adults have less accumulated wealth than prior generations
- Politically engaged: concerns include gun violence, health care reform, racism, education, immigration, reproductive rights

### Health Services
- Ages 18–34: most likely group to be uninsured
- Often use ED, urgent care, retail clinics instead of primary care
- Less preventive care utilization
- HP 2030 goal: increase proportion with a usual primary care provider

### Nursing Application for Young Adults
- Physical peak — focus on maintaining, not just restoring
- Preventive care: health screenings (BP, BMI, STI, cancer screening)
- Health education: hazard avoidance, lifestyle habits
- Worksite wellness, group fitness
- Counseling areas: STIs, HIV, skin cancer, sports injuries, contraception, prenatal care, smoking cessation, weight management, exercise
