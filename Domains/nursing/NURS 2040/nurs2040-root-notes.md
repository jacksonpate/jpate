## Source: 2040 exam.pdf — Exam Notes: School Age, Preschool, Adolescents (3 pages)

### School Age
- Depression at school age (noted as concern around age 22 developmentally?)
- External Locus of Control: Belief that fate/luck controls outcomes (vs. internal — belief that one controls outcomes)
- IQ Test Limitation: Does not test all forms of intelligence
- Ossification: Bone replaces cartilage — avoid heavy backpacks
- School Age: Transition from farsighted to normal vision
- School Age Cognitive: Concrete operations; views illness as punishment for wrongdoing
- School Age Activities: Fun, connection, peers and families
- School Age Growth: Slower, steadier growth with alternating growth spurts
- **Primary vs. Secondary Enuresis**: Primary = never gained control; Secondary = gained control then stopped
- Tympanogram: Detects hearing deficits resulting from chronic otitis media
- Warning signs: Declining grades, social isolation, somatization

### Adolescents (Females)
- **Menarche**: Requires sufficient body fat; influenced by genetic factors
- Sequence: Breast buds first → Growth spurt second → Pubic hair third → Menarche last (2 years after breast buds)
- Females lose teeth earlier than males

### Adolescents (Males)
- First sign of puberty: Thinning of scrotal sack
- Second: Pubic hair
- Third: Ejaculation / facial hair
- Fourth: Nocturnal emissions
- Note: Ejaculation precedes fertility by a few months

### ADHD
- Controversy around diagnosis
- Difficult assessments; multiple treatment approaches
- Learning styles: Kinesthetic (play/do), Auditory (listening), Visual (watching)
- Symptoms must be present for at least 6 months in two or more settings, before age 7, with impairment in social or academic areas

### Preschool Age (3-5)
- Preschool: Initiative vs. Guilt — may develop doubt of abilities if criticized or restricted
- Vigorous activity, active imagination
- Poverty contributes to disability and death from asthma
- Lymphocytic Leukemia: Most common cancer, ages 2-5
- 4.5x more males than females with autism
- Iron and calcium require particular attention
- More than half of poisonings occur in children under 6
- Retinoblastoma: Concern is absence of red reflex in eye
- Meningococcal vaccine: Ages 11+; HPV both male and female
- COVID-19 vaccine: 12 and older
- Lice/Scabies: Return to school after treatment has started
- Tanner Staging: Rates sexual maturity development progression

## Source: NRS2040-1.pdf — Module 1: Health Promotion and Prevention (47 pages)
*Instructor: Mr. Perry-Maddox*

### Module 1 Objectives
1. Describe Healthy People 2030 and discuss the role of the nurse in achieving it
2. Recognize the impact of Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) on health maintenance and prevention
3. Compare levels of prevention with services across the lifespan
4. Discuss the influence of health legislation and policy across the lifespan
5. Differentiate between Medicare, Medicaid, and other government-sponsored programs in achieving health equity

### What is Health?
- Core social concept ranging from Excellent to Poor
- Calculated based on: demographics, comparison group, current health/physical conditions, past conditions, socioeconomic situation, geographical location, demands of social role(s)
- **Epidemiology**: The study of health and disease in society

### Historical Evolution of Health Definitions
- **Pre-1940s**: Health = absence of disease; physicians independent (private practice); beginning of government support
- **1940s**: Health tied to ability to perform a social role; physicians linked to hospital-based services; Dept. of Health and Human Services established
- **1960s**: Health tied to adaptation and reaction to the environment; person-centered care; government control and cost escalation

### Wellness-Illness Continuum
- High/Positive: Well-being, life satisfaction, quality of life
- Low/Negative: Adaptation to condition(s)

### Developmental Perspective on Health
- Expand consciousness; pattern/meaning recognition; personal transformation; self-actualization
- "Health is an outcome of ongoing patterns of person and environment interactions"

### Social Ecological Model
- Individual: KSAs (Knowledge, Skills, Attitudes)
- Interpersonal: Social network
- Organizational: Environment
- Community: Culture
- Public Policy: Systemic regulations and support

### Social Determinants of Health (SDOH)
Non-medical factors affecting health outcomes:
- Economic Stability
- Education Access and Quality
- Health Care Access and Quality
- Neighborhood and Built Environment
- Social and Community Context

### Current Healthcare
- Advanced Practice Nurses; interdisciplinary; emphasis on quality of life
- Individual responsibility; functional health; resiliency

### 2025 Definitions
- **Health**: "The state of physical, mental, spiritual, and social functioning that realizes a person's potential and is experienced within a developmental context"
- **Disease**: "Failure of a person's adaptive mechanisms to counteract stimuli and stresses adequately, resulting in functional or structural disturbances"
- **Illness**: "Composed of the subjective experience of the individual and the physical manifestation of the disease"
- Key: Can feel ill without disease; can have disease without feeling ill

### Public Health
- Individual responsibility and collective action
- Disease prevention vs. promotion of health
- Healthy People 2020/2030: Instigated 1979; currently objective-led

### Healthy People 2020 Goals
1. Attain high-quality, longer lives free of preventable disease, disability, injury, premature death
2. Achieve health equity, eliminate disparities, improve health of all groups
3. Create social/physical environments promoting good health for all
4. Promote quality of life, healthy development, healthy behaviors across all life stages

### Healthy People 2030 Goals
1. Attain healthy, thriving lives and well-being, free of preventable disease/disability/injury/premature death
2. Eliminate health disparities, achieve health equity, attain health literacy to improve well-being of all
3. Create social, physical, and economic environments promoting full potential for health and well-being
4. Promote healthy development, behaviors, and well-being across all life stages
5. Engage leadership and public across sectors to take action and design health-improving policies

### Health Disparities
Unfair differences in health outcomes and access between groups of people — related to: race, gender, ability to pay, urban vs. rural, insurance coverage, Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement
Example: Black men die of prostate cancer more frequently than any other racial/ethnic group

### Personal Values Influence on Healthcare
- **Ethnocentrism**: Assumption that one's own perspective is correct and shared by others
- **Cultural Competence**: Knowledge and ability to understand other cultures' differences
- **Cultural Humility**: Ongoing self-reflection; recognizing you don't know everything; attitude-based
- **Empathy**: Ability to view other people's situations from their perspective

### Levels of Prevention
| Level | Time Frame | Description | Example |
|-------|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Primordial | Before risk factor develops and before disease | Prevents risk factors from developing | Fluoride in drinking water |
| Primary | Before disease occurs | Prevents disease or dysfunction; health promotion and specific protection | Alabama Seat Belt Law |
| Secondary | Early disease stage | Screening; treating early stages; delaying consequences of advanced disease | PHQ-9 depression screening |
| Tertiary | Disease is permanent/irreversible | Minimize effects; surveillance; prevent complications and deterioration | Monitoring chronic conditions |
| Quaternary | Prevention of overmedicalization | Prevention of harm by medical intervention | Avoiding overdiagnosis/overtreatment |

### Evolving Demands
- Shift from acute/hospital-based care to community-based care
- Non-traditional settings
- Changing client demographics
- Necessity of evidence-based practice

### The Role of the Nurse
- **Advocate**: Speaks for patients' needs and rights
- **Care Manager**: Coordinates care across settings and providers
- **Consultant**: Provides expert guidance
- **Deliverer of Services**: Provides direct nursing care
- **Educator**: Teaches patients, families, communities — "The problem is not a lack of knowledge, but rather the lack of application"
- **Healer**: "The nurse understands that healing resides in the ability to glimpse, or intuit the 'interior' of an individual..."
- **Researcher**: Generates evidence to improve practice (quantitative, qualitative, applied research)

### Key Statistic
Approximately 40% of the US population has difficulty securing basic necessities of housing, food, utilities, and healthcare

## Source: NRS2040-7.pdf and nurs_2040-module7.pdf — Chapter 7: Health Promotion and the Family (53 pages)

### The Family
- **Definition**: Interacting individuals related by blood, marriage, cohabitation, or adoption
- Enduring influence on health: patterns diet, activity, hygiene, coping; provides resources; fosters resilience; individualizes within cohesiveness
- Influences children's lifestyle choices
- Healthy People 2030: Families as means for health promotion

### Nursing Process and the Family
- Comprehensive family assessment: observe interactions as group; home visits in natural environment; recognize patterns; assess potential for positive change
- Family health status: functional / potentially dysfunctional / dysfunctional (actual problem)
- Nurse's role: Role model; collaborate; provide information, decision-making, problem-solving; liaison for services

### Family Systems Theory
- Patterns of living: individuals/families
- Families have unique culture, value system, history
- Family is unified whole vs. sum of parts
- Changes in one member affect whole family
- Family assessment is a tool to foster health promotion
- Living systems are open systems: change in one part affects entire system, requires adaptation

### Family Development Stages (Duvall and Miller)
1. Married couples — no children
2. Childbearing families — birth to 30 months
3. Families with preschool children — 2.5 to 6 years
4. Families with school children — 6-13 years
5. Families with teenagers — 13-20 years
6. Families launching young adults
7. Middle-aged parents
8. Aging family members

### Indicators of Healthy Families
- Nurturing relationships
- Establishing consistency
- Patterns promoting health and safety
- Adapting to challenges in growth
- Care and respect during crisis
- Connecting to community
- Socialize children; support members as they age

### Structural-Functional Perspective
- Structure: family composition
- Function: exchange information
- Changing structures: single parenting, cohabitation, same-sex unions, etc.

### Risk-Factor Perspective
Categories: Lifestyle, Biological, Environmental, Social/psychological/cultural/spiritual, Healthcare system
Families can help reduce: Nutrition, smoking, exercise, alcohol use, stress reduction

### Gordon's 11 Functional Health Patterns for Families
1. **Health Perception–Health Management**: Perceived health, management, and preventive practices; health beliefs/behaviors; environmental safety; use of healthcare
2. **Nutritional-Metabolic**: Typical food/fluid consumption; growth/development; eating patterns; risk factors for eating disorders, obesity, diabetes; food diary, shared meals, food types
3. **Elimination**: Regularity; toilet training; changes from usual pattern; age-specific developmental assessment
4. **Activity-Exercise**: Activity level; daily activities; family member involvement; screen time; frequency/type of exercise; family fun activities
5. **Sleep-Rest**: Sleeping patterns; usual habits; sleep disruption; naps; shiftwork considerations
6. **Cognitive-Perceptual**: Language, cognitive skills, perception; decision-making power; access to health information; lifestyle knowledge/choices; alternative/traditional practices
7. **Self-Perception–Self-Concept**: Family self-worth and feeling; aspirations, values, expectations, fears; family handling of stress
8. **Roles-Relationships**: Characteristics of roles/relationships; structure; function; family resilience; domestic violence assessment; high-risk behaviors
9. **Sexuality-Reproductive**: Sexuality fulfillment and reproductive patterns; family planning; contraceptives; pregnancy histories; sexuality practices
10. **Coping–Stress Tolerance**: Family adaptation to pressures; coping vs. stress; family strengths; history of stresses; family resources; dysfunctional adaptive strategies
11. **Values-Beliefs**: Family values, beliefs, goals, spirituality; role of religion; cultural/ethnic practices; traditions; value conflicts; effect of values on health

### Family Resilience
- Strategies for prevention during challenges
- Crises may reveal strengths and useful abilities
- Strong relationships, shared values, collaboration foster resilience
- Healthy People 2030 addresses bullying
- Key risk factors for disruption

### Genogram
- Family diagram showing membership, structure, and three generations
- Identifies family health patterns
- Represents each family member with connections between generations

## Source: nurs_2040-toddlers.pdf — Toddlers Chapter (9 pages)
*Note: Largely handwritten annotation overlay; limited extractable typed text*

### Toddlers (1-3 years)
- Motility increases; sense of self begins to develop
- Growth: 2-4 inches/year, 4-6 lbs/year (approx.)
- Birthweight reaches 4x by 2.5 years
- **Bowel control** happens before urinary control
- Age 2: 30 breaths/min | Age 3: 25 breaths/min
- Toddlers have small upper respiratory tract — risk for obstruction

### Unintentional Injuries in Toddlers
- Key risks: Drowning, Burns, Falls (head injuries), exploring unfamiliar environments
- Higher risk: Black and Native American children
- Safety measures: Gates, anchor furniture, locked storage, car restraints (rear-facing until 4 ft 9 in for booster)
- Helmets for bikes; forward-facing car seat when larger; fenced/supervised pools; flotation devices
- Medication safety: Minimize unnecessary meds; side effects; never give medications 10x dosage
- Lead exposure: Blood tests; concern with toys, furniture, older houses
- Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222

### Health/Developmental Concerns
- Iron, calcium, fat intake require attention
- Accreditation of childcare facilities
- Sensitivity and cultural competence in healthcare
- Medicaid/safety net programs; SNAP nutrition assistance; Head Start programs
- 5th percentile head circumference a concern
- Immunization schedule: HepB, IPV, Hib, Varicella, DTaP, influenza, PCV

## Source: infants-part-1.pdf — Chapter 17: Infants Part 1 (45 pages)
*NURS 2040*

### Overview
- Infant: Birth to 12 months
- Totally reliant on caretaker(s)
- Developmental tasks and anticipatory guidance are key

### Developmental Landmarks (Table 17.3)
| Age | Milestone |
|-----|-----------|
| 1-2 months | Smiles, recognizes faces, holds head up |
| 3-4 months | Focuses, follows objects fluidly |
| 5-6 months | Rolls over, sits with support |
| 7-9 months | Crawls, pincer grasp (9 months) |
| 12 months | Various motor/cognitive milestones |

### Developmental Tasks
- **Physiological**: Breathing, sucking, swallowing, elimination, sleep — oral stage (pacifier, thumb-sucking)
- **Stimulation**: Essential for development; auditory/visual stimuli critical
  - Music, spoken voice, mobiles, touch
  - Reading, singing, holding/comforting
  - Benefits: Language, cognitive, social, IQ development

### Developmental Theories for Infants
- **Erikson**: Trust vs. Mistrust (Birth to 18 months) — baby learning whether world is safe and reliable based on whether caregiver meets needs. Met needs → trust; Unmet needs → mistrust and anxiety. Influences all future relationships
- **Piaget**: Sensorimotor period — learning through senses and motor activity; mastering simple coordination
- **Reflexes**: Rooting and sucking assist survival

### Growth Charts
- CDC growth charts: height, weight, head circumference; plotted on standardized grid
- Body Mass Index (BMI)
- Growth curves/percentiles aid diagnosis of over/under nutrition

### Sex Differences
- Male: Larger, more muscle mass, more motor activity
- Female: Smaller but more physically mature at birth
- Height increases ~50% by 1 year
- Weight doubles by 6 months, triples by 1 year
- Head circumference reflects brain growth; measured at each visit

### Genetics
- **Birth defect**: Abnormality of structure, function, or metabolism due to genetic or environmental issue
- Genetic counseling provides guidance
- Risk factors:
  - Maternal age >35: Down syndrome risk increases
  - Eastern European Jews: Tay-Sachs disease risk
  - Black populations: Sickle cell anemia risk
  - Family history: Hemophilia, intellectual disabilities
  - Reproductive history: Previous miscarriages, stillbirths, previous children with genetic conditions
  - Maternal disease: Diabetes mellitus, seizure disorder
- **Nurse's role in genetic counseling**: Case finding, referral, family education

### Attachment and Bonding

### Sample NCLEX-Style Question

- **A. Singing lullabies to the baby** — CORRECT

- C. Keeping top of crib free of hanging mobiles — Mobiles are good for development
- D. Providing a toy that emits animal sounds when buttons pushed — Too young for this type of toy

### Child Abuse
**Definition**: All forms of physical and/or emotional ill-treatment, sexual abuse, neglect, negligent treatment, or commercial/other exploitation resulting in actual or potential harm to the infant/child's health, survival, development, or dignity

**Key Facts:**
- Most common under age 2
- Parenting is not instinctive; often related to inadequate parental coping; socially isolated parents
- Women abuse more frequently; men abuse more severely; sexual abuse more often by men
- Active or passive abuse by parents or caregivers
- Profound long-term effect on child; intergenerational cycle
- 1 in 7 children or infants are victims of abuse
- 1,700 children die from abuse or neglect annually
- Abused children commonly become abusing parents
- Occurs in every race, creed, or socioeconomic status

**Nurse's Prevention Role**: Identification and intervention in families at risk; protect abused/at-risk children

### Leading Causes of Death in Infants
1. Motor Vehicle Crash
2. Falls (most common after 4 months when infant starts rolling)
3. Poisoning
4. Suffocation
5. Drowning
6. Fire and Burns

### Environmental Safety
- **Falls**: Do not leave infant unattended on raised surfaces; most common after 4 months (rolling over)
- **Burns**: Deaths from smoke/toxic gases
- **Choking**: Food, liquids, balloons, small objects; childproof environment
  - Choking hazards: grapes, hot dog pieces, balloons, peanut butter, popcorn
- Infant CPR: Essential for parents and caregivers

## Source: Pre-schoolers-2040-1.pdf — Chapter 19: Preschool Child (46 pages)

### Overview — Preschool Child (Ages 3-5)
- More developed body structure; ability to control and use body
- Increasing language; mastery of self as independent being
- Experience extends beyond family; Kindergarten/1st grade start
- Many Healthy People 2030 goals aimed at preschoolers

### Biology and Genetics
- Protuberant toddler abdomen disappears; pelvis begins to straighten
- May have mild in-toeing, transient genu valgum (knock knee), and flat feet
- Growth: 4 lbs and 2 inches per year
- Skin matures; eccrine glands functional; apocrine glands (axilla/groin) non-functional
- Teeth: Complete primary (baby) teeth; secondary teeth eruption starts

**System Changes:**
- Kidneys/GI: Full maturity; voluntary bladder control
- Heart/lungs: Increased capacity
- Ears: Change shape; less otitis media
- Tonsils/Adenoids: Large (may be enlarged)
- Immune: Boosted immune response
- Musculoskeletal/Neuro: Advances fine/gross motor skills; abdominal muscles develop

### Gender and Genetics
- Boys: More illnesses and accidents
- Race influences: healthcare access, cultural preferences, dietary choices, health-promoting behaviors
- Most commonly diagnosed genetic conditions: Cystic fibrosis, Duchenne muscular dystrophy, Fragile X syndrome

### Gordon's Functional Health Patterns — Preschool Application
**Cognitive Notes:**
- **Animism** (Preoperational): Attributing characteristics, emotions, intentions to inanimate objects
- **Magical Thinking**: Belief that one's own thoughts, wishes, or actions can influence external events

**Health Perception–Health Management:**
- Curious about body of opposite sex
- Vague/inaccurate ideas of internal body
- Anxiety: Fear of mutilation, death
- Upset over minor injuries; pain/illness viewed as punishment
- Can contribute to health management (brushing teeth)

**Nutritional-Metabolic:**
- MyPlate replaces food pyramid
- Attention to iron and calcium intake
- Limit fat and sugar
- Half plate of vegetables
- Food preferences/refusals may cause conflicts; learning utensil use; helping with meal preparation
- Begin eating meals away from home (preschool/childcare requirements)

## Source: Chapter_022-2040 young adults.pdf — Chapter 22: Young Adult (31 pages)

### Overview — Young Adult (Ages 18-35)
- Transition to adult roles and responsibilities
- Tasks: navigating education, employment, establishing home, finding partner/parenthood
- Risk-taking behaviors peak; lifelong health behaviors emerge
- Growth completed by age 20; optimal strength at 25-30 (gradually declines)
- Major milestones delayed vs prior generations: 45% reached milestones in 1975 vs. 24% in 2016
- Milestones: marriage, parenthood, full-time work, living independently

### Biology and Genetics
- Period of relatively good health
- Potential hazards: poor eating habits, STIs, less physical activity, higher drug/alcohol use
- Maternal mortality rate: US has high mortality vs. other developed countries
- Young adults 24-34 without college degrees have poorer cardiovascular health than those with degrees

### Health Perception–Health Management
- Majority consider health good to excellent
- Transition from pediatric to adult care provider
- Insurance access: ACA; high rates of uninsured age 19-25 (2018)
- Men more likely than women to engage in unhealthy behaviors

**Health Management for Young Adults:**
- Physical exam: Height, weight, BMI, BP, blood tests, social history (tobacco, alcohol, sexual activity)
- Cervical cancer screening: Start age 21 (or when sexually active), q 3 years; HPV screen q 5 years beginning age 30

**Important Healthcare Concerns:**
- Unmet social needs (food insecurity, lack of transportation, poverty)
- Risk-taking behavior remains problematic
- Communicable diseases: Influenza, COVID, TB, Hepatitis B & C, HIV, meningitis

### Nutritional-Metabolic
- Body-conscious; value thinness, muscle tone, athletic ability
- Abundant food sources; portion sizes have increased
- Low-income communities are food deserts
- Female considerations: Iron, Folic acid, Calcium

### Elimination Pattern
- Eating disorders may persist
- Common complaints: Constipation, hemorrhoids, occasional diarrhea
- Colorectal cancer rates increasing in ages 20-39

### Activity-Exercise
- Aerobic exercise recommended
- Melanoma: Most common cancer in those age 25-29 — avoid excessive UV radiation
- Sports/Fitness: Gym memberships, alternative transport (bike), helmets
- Accidental deaths from drowning common (swimming, boating, scuba)

### Sleep-Rest
- Need 8 hrs (18-21); 7 hrs (22+)
- Subject to fatigue: work, stress, inactivity
- Avoid electronics 30 min before bed; consistent sleep schedule; avoid alcohol/caffeine before bed

### Cognitive-Perceptual
- Executive decision-making better developed; calculation risks vs. rewards; long-term planning
- **Piaget**: Intellectual growth; recall peaks at 20, memory diminishes at 30
- **Erikson**: Intimacy versus isolation

### Self-Perception–Self-Concept
- Two emotional themes: work and financial independence
- Temporary/reversible: exploration and experiment
- Preparation: Firm commitments; transitioning to full-time employment and residential independence

### Roles-Relationships
- Social networking very prevalent; can lead to compulsive/addictive use
- Romantic relationships; childbearing decisions
- Same-sex unions, non-binary/transgender identities; challenges: legal protection, stigma, harassment, barriers to healthcare
- Divorce rates declining for younger adults; increasing number have children without marrying

### Sexuality-Reproductive
- Menstrual cycle typically well established
- Infertility: Lack of conception for 12 months; Assisted Reproductive Technologies (ART)
- Half of all pregnancies are unintended
- STIs: Since 2014, some have increased (chlamydia in women 15-19; gonorrhea in women 20-24)
- Chlamydia: Annual screening for sexually active women age 24 or younger

### Coping–Stress Tolerance
- Job and school-related stress common
- **Suicide**: 2nd leading cause of death in young adults; depression is risk factor
- Behavioral health integration important

### Environmental Processes
- Racism and discrimination prevalent
- Accidents: Leading cause of death; motor vehicle accidents greatest cause
- Noise pollution: Hearing loss, stress, sleep disturbances
- Air pollution: Respiratory disease, higher lipids, preterm birth
- Occupational hazards; essential preventive interventions

### Drug Use
- Peak illicit drug use during young adult period
- Opioid crisis: Synthetic opioids (fentanyl); prevention: Rx guidelines, naloxone
- Marijuana: Most commonly abused drug; health risks include lung/cardiovascular disease, anxiety/depression, memory/learning deficits, risky behavior, accidents

### Alcohol Use
- Alcohol-related accidents: Leading cause of death ages 15-24
- Heavy alcohol use most common in this age group
- Binge drinking: 5+ drinks in 2h (males); 4+ drinks in 2h (females)
- Contributes to falls, drownings, burns, homicide, suicide, sexual assault

### Health Services
- 18-34 most likely group to be uninsured
- Often use ED, urgent care, retail care — less preventive care
- Affordable Care Act covers young adults on parents' insurance to age 26

## Source: Chapter_023+-2040-middle aged.pdf — Chapter 23: Middle-Aged Adult (33 pages)

### Overview — Middle-Aged Adults (Ages 35-65)
- Significant biological, physiological, social changes occur
- Expanded responsibilities and increased productivity = sense of accomplishment
- Comprises 40% of US population

### Biology and Genetics
- Body changes: Thinning/graying hair, drier skin/wrinkles, increased fat, decreased muscle mass and bone density, increased BMI
- Hormonal changes
- Leading causes of mortality: Heart disease, cancer, accidents
- Morbidity and mortality influenced by lifestyle behaviors
- Greater risk of genetic disease vs. young adults: DM, HTN, gout, heart disease, alcoholism, some cancers (breast, colorectal)

### Health Perception–Health Management
- Consequences of self-destructive habits surface (smoking, alcohol, overeating)
- Leading cause of death ages 45-64: Heart disease or cancer
- Other causes: Accidents, COPD, CVA, DM, Alzheimer's, suicide, flu/pneumonia, liver disease, kidney disease, septicemia

### Nutritional-Metabolic
- High saturated fat diet = high cholesterol; 43 million taking lipid-lowering drugs
- Often need to supplement Calcium and Vitamin D
- High sodium diet contributes to HTN; avg caffeine consumption 4-5 g/day
- Dental hygiene essential; oral cancers begin with changes to mucosa

### Elimination
- Gradual decrease in tone of large intestine
- Constipation: Sedentary lifestyle, lack of fiber, inadequate fluid intake — dietary fiber preferable to laxatives
- Renal function gradually decreases
- Incontinence risk: Women (multiple births), Men (prostate surgery complication)

### Activity-Exercise
- Regular physical activity increases life expectancy and quality of life
- Only small fraction of adults get enough exercise
- Options: Brisk walking, jogging, bicycling, swimming
- Caution: Hot weather, dehydration, age nearing 65, dizziness, chest pressure, shortness of breath
- Moderation key; increase gradually

### Sleep-Rest
- May get less sleep than young adults due to more demanding lifestyle
- Insomnia common; sleep apnea common — associated with MI, CVA, CHF, arrhythmia
- Cognitive-behavioral therapy helpful for insomnia
- Avoid exercise within 2h of bedtime; avoid excess caffeine

### Cognitive-Perceptual Changes
- Capacity to learn constant ages 35-65; reaction time and flexibility may decrease
- **Perceptual changes**:
  - Presbyopia: Farsightedness
  - Glaucoma: Increased intraocular pressure
  - Cataracts: Opacity of lens
  - Diabetic retinopathy
  - Macular degeneration: Deterioration of retina
  - Presbycusis: Loss of higher-frequency hearing
  - Diminished sense of taste

### Self-Perception–Self-Concept (Erikson)
- **Generativity vs. Stagnation**: Generativity = sense of productivity, creativity, caring for others; Stagnation = lack of accomplishment, self-absorption
- Middle age is time of critical self-review
- Women: Menopause/perimenopause — mood swings, anxiety, fatigue, depression; hormonal therapy limited after 2002 (WHI)

### Roles-Relationships
- Frequently time of reassessment; complex responsibilities (family, work, civic)
- Empty nest syndrome; children now interact with parents on adult-to-adult level
- Divorce and death of partner = major disruption
- **SDOH**: Wealth (net worth) better health indicator than income; great disparities along ethnic lines (2016 median family wealth: Black = $17,600, Hispanic = $20,700, White = $171,000)

### Sexuality-Reproductive
- High proportion remain sexually active
- Pregnancy: 30% unintended in women >35; 77% of perimenopause pregnancies unintended; higher risk of maternal death and fetal complications
- Changes: Slower arousal, vaginal dryness, decreased libido, erectile dysfunction
- STIs: HPV causes 90% of cervical cancers; HIV/AIDS adults >50 are "hidden" risk group

### Coping–Stress Tolerance
- Mind-body connection can mitigate negative effects of stress: yoga, meditation, tai chi, deep breathing
- Coping with death of parent: Preparation helps feelings of control (living will, advanced directive, durable POA)

### Developmental Theorists
- **Erikson**: Caring is the middle-aged virtue, often developed during crisis
- **Kohlberg**: Conventional and post-conventional phases; Stage 3 (personal wants/interpersonal), Stage 4 (duties demanded by society/law and order), Stage 5 (principles to live by/sacrifice for others)

### Hazardous Environmental Issues
- Ionizing radiation, water pollution (benzene, chlordane), air pollution, noise pollution
- **Occupational hazards**: Pneumoconiosis (black lung disease), asbestos (mesothelioma), organic carcinogens (benzene, PVC)
- **Tobacco**: Leading cause of preventable death; 2,000 known chemicals in cigarettes

### Biological Agents
- Hepatitis A (fecal-oral), Hepatitis B (blood-borne — occupational hazard for health professionals)
- Pneumonia and Herpes zoster (shingles): vaccines available

### Levels of Policy Making
- Many cannot afford to retire in mid-50s to early 60s
- Quality healthcare expensive; many wait for Medicare before retiring
- Consumer debt and college debt necessitate continued work
- Health insurance becoming increasingly costly; loss of job = loss of insurance
