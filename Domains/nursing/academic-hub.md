#### 1. Central Dogma & Key Terms

**Central Dogma**

**DNA → RNA → Protein**

- **Replication** — DNA copies itself
- **Transcription** — DNA → RNA
- **Translation** — RNA → Protein

> Exception: Retroviruses (like HIV) go **RNA → DNA → RNA → Protein** using reverse transcriptase

**Key Definitions**

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Gene** | Segment of DNA coding for a functional product (usually a protein) |
| **Genome** | ALL genetic information in a cell |
| **Genetics** | The study of genes |
| **Chromosome** | Structure containing DNA and genes |
| **Operon** | Group of related genes transcribed together under one promoter |
| **Monocistronic** | One mRNA = one protein |
| **Polycistronic** | One mRNA = multiple proteins (prokaryotes only) |
| **Nucleoid** | Region in prokaryotes where DNA is located (no membrane) |

---

#### 2. Types of Mutations

| Mutation | What Happens | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Missense** | One base changed → different amino acid | Altered protein |
| **Nonsense** | One base changed → STOP codon | Truncated/no protein |
| **Silent** | Base changed → same amino acid | No change |
| **Frameshift** | Base inserted or deleted → reading frame shifts | Usually nonfunctional protein |

#### 3. Microbial Genomes

**Genome Size Comparison**

| Organism | Genome Size | Noncoding DNA |
|---|---|---|
| **Prokaryotes** | 106–16,000 kb | <15% |
| **Eukaryotes** | 2,900–100,000,000+ kb | >90% |
| **Humans** | ~3,000,000 kb | ~95% |

> Bigger genome = more complex organism

**Prokaryote vs Eukaryote Chromosomes**

| Feature | Prokaryote | Eukaryote |
|---|---|---|
| Shape | **Circular** | **Linear** |
| Location | Nucleoid (no membrane) | Nucleus |
| Histones | Histone-like (HU, H-NS) | True histones |
| Noncoding DNA | Very little | Lots (introns, pseudogenes) |
| Origins of replication | **One (oriC)** | **Multiple** |

**Archaeal Genomes — The Hybrid**

Like bacteria: Single circular chromosome, operons, no nuclear membrane, asexual reproduction
Like eukaryotes: DNA replication, transcription, and translation machinery

**Plasmids**

- Extra-chromosomal DNA — separate from main chromosome
- Found in bacteria, archaea, and some eukaryotic microbes
- Usually **circular** and **double-stranded**
- Can carry **antibiotic resistance genes**
- Replicate by **Rolling Circle Replication**
- Can be transferred **horizontally** between cells

#### 4. DNA Structure & Supercoiling

**Base Pairing Rules**

DNA → DNA
- A pairs with T (2 hydrogen bonds)
- G pairs with C (3 hydrogen bonds)

DNA → RNA (Transcription)
- Template A → RNA U
- Template T → RNA A
- Template G → RNA C
- Template C → RNA G

> High G-C sequences require more energy to denature because G-C has 3 hydrogen bonds vs A-T which has only 2

**DNA Supercoiling**

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Positive supercoil** | DNA is **overwound** |
| **Negative supercoil** | DNA is **underwound** |

> Most bacteria, eukaryotes, and archaea = **negatively supercoiled**
> Exception: *Sulfolobus* (extreme heat/acid) = **positively supercoiled**

**Topoisomerases**

| Type | Cuts | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Type I** | ONE strand | Relieves negative supercoils | — |
| **Type II** | BOTH strands | Introduces negative supercoils | **DNA Gyrase** |

> **DNA Gyrase** is targeted by **quinolone antibiotics**

---

#### 5. Horizontal Gene Transfer

**Two Types of Gene Transfer**
- **Vertical** — parent to offspring (normal cell division)
- **Horizontal** — DNA moves between different cells (3 mechanisms)

**The 3 Mechanisms**

| Method | Contact Needed? | Vector | Key Detail |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Transformation** | No | None (naked DNA) | Griffith 1928, *S. pneumoniae* |
| **Conjugation** | Yes | Pilus (F-factor plasmid) | Lederberg 1958 |
| **Transduction** | No | Bacteriophage (virus) | Virus carries DNA between cells |

**Griffith Experiment = Transformation**

| Strain | Capsule | Virulent |
|---|---|---|
| **S strain (smooth)** | Yes | Deadly |
| **R strain (rough)** | No | Harmless |

**Key experiment:** Heat-killed S + Live R → Mouse **died**
→ Naked DNA from dead S transformed harmless R into killer = **transformation**

**Conjugation Details**
- **F+ cell** = donor (has F-factor plasmid)
- **F- cell** = recipient
- When F-factor integrates into chromosome → cell becomes **Hfr (High-Frequency Recombination)**
- Hfr can transfer chromosomal DNA to F- cells

#### 6. DNA Replication

**Key Rules**
- **Semiconservative** — each daughter cell gets one old + one new strand
- **Bidirectional** — starts at **oriC**, two forks move in opposite directions
- **5'→3' only** — DNA pol can ONLY add nucleotides to the 3' end

**Leading vs Lagging Strand**

| Strand | Synthesis | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Leading** | Continuous | Runs toward fork 5'→3' |
| **Lagging** | Discontinuous (Okazaki fragments) | Runs away from fork, made in chunks |

> Both strands synthesized **5'→3'** — the lagging strand just has to do it in fragments

**Replication Proteins — MEMORIZE ALL**

| Protein | Function |
|---|---|
| **DnaA** | Initiates replication at oriC (gas pedal) |
| **DnaB (Helicase)** | Unwinds the double helix |
| **Primase** | Makes the RNA primer to start synthesis |
| **DNA Pol III** | Main enzyme — adds nucleotides 5'→3' |
| **DNA Pol I** | Removes RNA primers, fills gaps with DNA |
| **DNA Gyrase** | Relieves positive supercoiling ahead of fork |
| **DNA Ligase** | Joins Okazaki fragments together |
| **SeqA** | Prevents DnaA from restarting too soon (brake) |
| **Tus** | Stops replication at terminus (ter) |
| **Topoisomerase IV** | Separates two chromosomes after replication |
| **XerCD** | Resolves chromosome dimers |

**Termination**
- Ends at **terminator sequences (ter)**
- **Tus** protein binds ter and stops the fork
- **Topoisomerase IV** separates the two newly made chromosomes

**PCR — Polymerase Chain Reaction**

Makes **1 million+ copies** of target DNA outside the cell

3 Steps Per Cycle:

| Step | Temp | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| **Denaturation** | 95°C | DNA strands separate |
| **Annealing** | 55°C | Primers bind to target |
| **Extension** | 72°C | Taq pol builds new DNA |

Required ingredients:
- DNA template
- Forward + reverse primers
- **Taq DNA polymerase** (from *Thermus aquaticus* — heat resistant)
- All 4 nucleotides (dNTPs)

> No RNA primers or Okazaki fragments in PCR!
> The **template determines the DNA sequence**, not the polymerase

**Bacterial vs Eukaryotic Replication**

| Feature | Bacteria | Eukaryotes |
|---|---|---|
| Chromosome | Circular | Linear |
| Origins | One (oriC) | Multiple |
| End problem | None | Yes → solved by **telomerase** |
| Main pol | DNA Pol III | Pol ε (leading) / Pol δ (lagging) |

#### 7. Transcription & Regulation

**What is Transcription?**

Synthesis of RNA from a DNA template by **RNA polymerase (RNA pol)**

**RNA Polymerase Components**

| Component | Role |
|---|---|
| **Sigma factor (σ)** | Needed for **initiation** — finds the promoter |
| **Core polymerase (α₂, β, β')** | Needed for **elongation** — builds the RNA |

Together = **holoenzyme**

**Sigma-70 (σ70)**
- The **housekeeping** sigma factor in *E. coli*
- Recognizes **−35 region** (TTGACA) and **−10 region** (TATAAT) on the promoter
- After initiation begins → sigma factor is **released**

**3 Phases of Transcription**

| Phase | What Happens |
|---|---|
| **Initiation** | RNA pol + sigma bind promoter; DNA unwinds |
| **Elongation** | RNA pol moves along template, builds RNA ~45 bases/sec |
| **Termination** | RNA pol detaches, transcript released |

**Termination — Two Methods**

| Method | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Rho-dependent** | Rho protein chases RNA pol and causes it to stop |
| **Rho-independent** | Inverted repeat on RNA forms a **stem-loop (hairpin)** that stalls RNA pol |

**Products of Transcription**

| RNA Type | Function |
|---|---|
| **mRNA** | Carries protein-coding message |
| **rRNA** | Structural part of ribosome |
| **tRNA** | Carries amino acids to ribosome |
| **sRNA** | Regulates transcription or translation |

**Gene Regulation — Operons**

Operon parts: **Promoter + Operator + Structural genes**

| Operon Type | Example | Normally | Turned OFF by | Turned ON by |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Repressible** | trp operon | ON | Tryptophan binding repressor | Absence of tryptophan |
| **Inducible** | lac operon | OFF | lac repressor blocks operator | Lactose present |

Lac operon max activation requires BOTH:
- Lactose present (repressor released)
- Glucose absent (CAP activator binds)

**Antibiotics Targeting Transcription**

| Antibiotic | Target | Selective? | Used clinically? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rifamycin B** | Bacterial RNA pol | Yes | Yes |
| **Actinomycin D** | DNA (any cell) | No | No (cancer only) |

#### 8. Translation & Proteins

**Key Components**

| Component | Role |
|---|---|
| **mRNA** | Carries the codons (message) |
| **tRNA** | Has anticodon + carries amino acid at 3' end |
| **rRNA** | Structural part of ribosome |
| **Ribosome** | Machine that reads mRNA and builds protein |

**The Ribosome**
- Bacterial ribosome = **70S**
- Made of: **30S** (small subunit) + **50S** (large subunit)
- 30S contains **16S rRNA**
- 50S contains **23S rRNA**

> S = Svedberg unit (measures sedimentation rate, NOT size — that's why 30S + 50S = 70S not 80S)

**The Genetic Code**
- 64 possible codons total
- **61** code for amino acids
- **3** are stop codons: **UAA, UAG, UGA**
- Code is **redundant/degenerate** — multiple codons can code for same amino acid
- Start codon: **AUG** (Methionine)

**tRNA Structure**
- **Cloverleaf shape**
- **Anticodon loop** — 3 bases that pair with mRNA codon
- **3' CCA acceptor end** — where amino acid attaches

> The amino acid ALWAYS attaches at the **3' end** of tRNA

**Shine-Dalgarno Sequence**
- Purine-rich sequence on mRNA: **5'-AGGAGGU-3'**
- Located 4–8 bases **upstream** of the start codon
- Binds to **16S rRNA** of the 30S subunit
- This is how the ribosome finds the correct reading frame

**3 Phases of Translation**

| Phase | What Happens |
|---|---|
| **Initiation** | 30S + 50S come together at AUG start codon; first tRNA enters P site |
| **Elongation** | New tRNA enters A site → peptide bond forms → ribosome shifts → old tRNA exits E site |
| **Termination** | Stop codon (UAA/UAG/UGA) reached → release factor enters → protein released |

**Ribosome Sites**

| Site | Function |
|---|---|
| **A site** (Aminoacyl) | New tRNA enters here |
| **P site** (Peptidyl) | Growing peptide chain |
| **E site** (Exit) | Empty tRNA exits here |

**Coupled Transcription & Translation (Prokaryotes Only)**
- Prokaryotes have **no nucleus** so transcription and translation happen in same compartment
- Ribosomes bind the 5' end of mRNA **before** RNA pol even finishes making it
- Eukaryotes **cannot** do this — transcription in nucleus, translation in cytoplasm

**Antibiotics Targeting Translation**

| Antibiotic | Target | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Streptomycin** | 70S ribosome formation | Inhibits initiation |
| **Tetracycline** | 30S subunit (A site) | Blocks tRNA from entering A site |
| **Chloramphenicol** | 23S rRNA of 50S subunit | Blocks peptide bond formation |
| **Erythromycin** | 23S rRNA of 50S subunit | Blocks translocation |

#### 9. Codon Translation Practice

**Base Pairing Cheat Sheet**

| Template DNA | → | mRNA |
|---|---|---|
| A | → | U |
| T | → | A |
| G | → | C |
| C | → | G |

**How to Work a Translation Problem**

If given TEMPLATE DNA (3'→5'):
1. Read template 3'→5'
2. Build mRNA complementary to it (5'→3')
3. Look up each codon in the codon table

If given CODING DNA (5'→3'):
1. Coding DNA = same as mRNA but T instead of U
2. Just replace T with U to get mRNA
3. Look up each codon

If given mRNA:
1. Read 5'→3' in triplets
2. Look up each codon directly

> TAA on coding DNA → UAA on mRNA → **STOP CODON** (translation stops immediately)

#### 10. DNA Sequence Analysis & BLASTn

**Gel Electrophoresis**
- Separates DNA fragments by size
- DNA is negatively charged → runs toward positive pole
- **Smaller fragments** travel **farther** (faster)
- Each **band** = all fragments of same size
- **Marker (M) lane** = known sizes for comparison

**BLAST Types**

| Tool | Compares |
|---|---|
| **BLASTn** | Nucleotide sequences (DNA) |
| **BLASTp** | Protein sequences |

**16S rDNA — Why It's Used**
- Found in **all bacteria and archaea**
- Highly conserved (changes slowly) = good **molecular clock**
- Used by **Carl Woese** to build the **tree of life**
- Standard gene for bacterial identification and classification

**BLASTn Results — THE GOLDEN RULES**

| % Identity | Query Coverage | Conclusion |
|---|---|---|
| **≥ 97%** | 100% | **Same species — already known** |
| **< 97%** | 100% | **Novel species** |
| **100%** | 100% | **Exact match to known species** |
| Any % | **< 100%** | **Cannot make any conclusion** |

> Query coverage MUST be 100% to draw ANY conclusion at all!

**Metagenomics vs Genomic DNA**

| | Genomic DNA | Metagenomic DNA |
|---|---|---|
| Source | Single cultured organism | Environmental sample |
| Culturing required | Yes | No |
| What you get | One organism's genome | ALL organisms in sample |

> Most microbes can't be cultured in a lab — metagenomics lets you study them anyway!

**Sanger Sequencing**
- Method to **read/determine** a DNA sequence
- Uses **ddNTPs** (dideoxynucleotides) that terminate synthesis at specific bases
- Produces fragments of different lengths separated by size
- Gives you the actual sequence readout

### Chapter 14 — Antimicrobial Chemotherapy: Antibiotics

**Key Points:**
- Antibiotics target specific bacterial structures or processes including cell walls, protein synthesis, DNA replication, and cell membranes
- Major antibiotic classes include beta-lactams, aminoglycosides, tetracyclines, macrolides, and fluoroquinolones, each with distinct mechanisms
- Bactericidal agents kill bacteria directly, while bacteriostatic agents inhibit growth and rely on host immunity to clear infection
- Broad-spectrum antibiotics target many bacterial types; narrow-spectrum antibiotics target specific organisms, reducing collateral microbiome damage
- Antibiotic resistance arises through enzymatic inactivation, altered target sites, efflux pumps, and reduced membrane permeability

**Key Concepts**

- **Antimicrobial Chemotherapy** — The use of chemical agents to selectively inhibit or kill microorganisms without causing significant harm to the host
- **Selective Toxicity** — The ability of an antibiotic to target microbial cells while sparing host eukaryotic cells
- **Bactericidal** — Directly kills bacteria (e.g., penicillins, fluoroquinolones, aminoglycosides)
- **Bacteriostatic** — Inhibits bacterial growth without killing, relying on host immune system to clear the infection (e.g., tetracyclines, macrolides, chloramphenicol)
- **Broad-Spectrum Antibiotic** — Effective against a wide range of both gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria
- **Narrow-Spectrum Antibiotic** — Effective against only a specific group or type of bacteria, reducing disruption of normal flora
- **Beta-Lactams** — Inhibit bacterial cell wall synthesis by binding to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) and blocking peptidoglycan cross-linking (e.g., penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems)
- **Aminoglycosides** — Bind the 30S ribosomal subunit, causing misreading of mRNA and inhibiting protein synthesis; bactericidal (e.g., streptomycin, gentamicin)
- **Tetracyclines** — Broad-spectrum bacteriostatic; bind the 30S ribosomal subunit and block aminoacyl-tRNA from entering the A site
- **Macrolides** — Bind the 50S ribosomal subunit and inhibit translocation during protein synthesis; bacteriostatic (e.g., erythromycin, azithromycin)
- **Fluoroquinolones** — Inhibit DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV, blocking DNA replication and transcription; bactericidal (e.g., ciprofloxacin)
- **Beta-Lactamase** — Bacterial enzyme that cleaves the beta-lactam ring, rendering it ineffective; major resistance mechanism
- **Efflux Pumps** — Membrane proteins that actively transport antibiotics out of bacterial cells
- **Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC)** — Lowest concentration of an antibiotic that visibly inhibits bacterial growth
- **Superinfection** — Secondary infection caused by disruption of normal flora from broad-spectrum antibiotic use
- **Peptidoglycan** — Structural polymer of bacterial cell walls; primary target of beta-lactam and glycopeptide antibiotics like vancomycin

**Mnemonics**
- Antibiotic targets — CWPM: Cell Wall (beta-lactams, vancomycin), Cell membrane (polymyxins), Protein synthesis (aminoglycosides, tetracyclines, macrolides), DNA/RNA (fluoroquinolones, rifampin)
- 30S ribosome inhibitors — AT 30: Aminoglycosides and Tetracyclines target the 30S subunit
- 50S ribosome inhibitors — MEAL at 50: Macrolides, Erythromycin, Azithromycin, Linezolid target the 50S subunit
- Bacteriostatic drugs — CSTEM: Chloramphenicol, Sulfonamides, Tetracyclines, Erythromycin (macrolides), and clindaMycin
- Resistance mechanisms — EASE: Enzymatic inactivation, Altered target sites, Switching to efflux pumps, Entry reduction (decreased permeability)
