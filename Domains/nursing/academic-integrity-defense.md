## My Statement

On February 24, 2026, I called Rainbow City Family Medicine to report that I was ill with nausea and to schedule an appointment. The clinic generated an excuse note based on that phone call and emailed it to me at 12:37 PM on 02/24. The note reflected the call date of 02/24 rather than my actual visit date, which is a clerical practice of the clinic — not anything I created or altered.

On February 25, 2026, I attended my scheduled appointment and was evaluated and treated by Ivonne Joiner, CRNP, who prescribed Zofran (ondansetron) for my nausea. The clinic then emailed me a corrected note at 12:21 PM on 02/25 — before Dr. Miller even sent his escalation email at 12:52 PM that same day.

I forwarded the original clinic note to Dr. Miller without closely reviewing the specific date, as I had no reason to believe it contained an error. I was simply passing along what the clinic sent me.

When Dr. Miller contacted the clinic, the front desk and Ivonne Joiner repeatedly told him I had an appointment on 02/25 — which was accurate. He interpreted their redirection as evasion. In reality, they were telling him the truth: I was seen on 02/25, not 02/24. The clinic did not lie to him. There was no cover-up.

I did not fabricate, alter, or misrepresent any documentation. My account has been completely consistent from my very first response. The corrected clinic note, the pharmacy record, and the clinic's own records all corroborate everything I have stated.

### Questions they might throw at you — and how to answer

**"Why didn't you check the date on the note before sending it?"**
I had no reason to think the note contained an error. I received it directly from the clinic and forwarded it as-is. The note came from a trusted medical office — I trusted that it was accurate.

**"Your mother works at this clinic. Did she have anything to do with this?"**
My mother works there but was completely uninvolved in my visit, my scheduling, or any of the documentation. I wouldn't even tell her I was skipping class because she'd be upset. I went there as a patient, the same as anyone else.

**"Why did the clinic seem to avoid Dr. Miller's questions?"**
They weren't avoiding anything — they kept telling him I had an appointment on February 25th, which is the truth. He was asking about the 24th specifically, and they correctly confirmed I wasn't seen on the 24th. The confusion came from him asking the wrong date. Once I contacted the clinic directly, they immediately issued a corrected note the same day.

**"Dr. Miller says Ivonne Joiner confirmed you were not seen on the 24th. How do you explain that?"**
She was right — I was not seen on the 24th. I was seen on the 25th. Her own prescription record confirms this. She prescribed me Zofran on 02/25/2026. That record exists independently in the pharmacy system and cannot be altered by anyone.

**"Why did the clinic send a note before you were even seen?"**
This is a practice some clinics use — when a patient calls in sick, they generate documentation at the time of the call and schedule the visit. The note was generated from my 02/24 phone call. My appointment was the next morning. The virtual visit confirmation email sent at 9:37 AM on 02/25 confirms the appointment was always scheduled for that day.

**"Why should we believe the corrected note wasn't also fabricated?"**
The corrected note came directly from the clinic's own email address — Keri Landry at gadsdenregional.com. More importantly, the pharmacy record is entirely independent. It lives in the pharmacy's system, not the clinic's. It shows Zofran filled on 02/25, prescribed by Ivonne Joiner. Those two records — from completely separate systems — tell the same story.

**"You submitted a note with the wrong date. Doesn't that itself constitute misrepresentation?"**
Misrepresentation requires intent to deceive. I had no knowledge that the date was incorrect. I received a document from a medical office and forwarded it in good faith. The moment I was made aware of the error, I immediately contacted the clinic and submitted corrected documentation — before Dr. Miller even decided to escalate.

**"How do we know you didn't actually just skip class on the 24th?"**
I was sick with nausea on 02/24, which is why I called the clinic. I was excused for 02/24 due to illness — that's what the corrected note states. Whether I physically went to the clinic on the 24th is irrelevant; I was ill and the clinic documented that. The visit occurred on 02/25 as all four independent pieces of evidence confirm.
