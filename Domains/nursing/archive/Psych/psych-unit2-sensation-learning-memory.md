# Psych Unit 2 — Sensation, Learning & Memory

## The Eye

**Fovea**
- Area of highest visual acuity
- Contains only cones

**Transduction**
- Rods and cones contain photopigment
- Triggers action potentials in neurons that get input from rods and cones
- These are carried by **ganglion cells** → axons form the **optic nerve**
- **Blind spot** — where axons leave the eye

---

## Visual Pathways

**Optic Chiasm** — crossing over half of fibers
- Left visual field → processed by the **Right Hemisphere** (LV → Right)
- Right visual field → processed by the **Left Hemisphere** (RV → Left)

**Primary Visual Cortex** — in occipital lobes

**Two Visual Pathways:**
- **Ventral pathway** ("What") — identifying objects and object features
- **Dorsal pathway** ("Where") — locating objects and perceiving motion

---

## Vision: Seeing Color

- Color is determined by wavelength

**Trichromatic Theory**
- Three cone types (three types of photopigment)
- Sensitive to different wavelengths
- Pattern of activation across cone types determines perceived color

**Opponent Process Theory**
- Some color vision phenomena that trichromatic theory cannot explain
- Color pairings in the visual system: Red–Green, Blue–Yellow
- Cells sensitive to Red and Green; other cells sensitive to Blue and Yellow

**Which theory is correct? → Both**
- Trichromatic: cones in retina
- Opponent Process: cells that get input from cones

## Auditory Perception — How We Hear

**Sound Wave Properties:**
- **Amplitude** → corresponds to perceived loudness
- **Frequency** → corresponds to perceived pitch
  - Measured in Hertz (Hz)
  - Normal human range: 20 Hz – 20,000 Hz

**Mechanical Pathway of Hearing (Order of Structures):**
1. Outer ear (pinna) → auditory canal → pushes on eardrum
2. Middle ear (ossicles — hammer, anvil, stirrup) → oval window vibrates
3. Inner ear — fluid-filled cochlea → wave pushes hair cells → fires neuron signal
   1. Sound wave causes eardrum to vibrate
   2. Vibration of eardrum causes ossicles in middle ear to vibrate
   3. This causes oval window to vibrate, creating a wave
   4. Wave moves through basilar membrane as well
   5. Hair cells on top of basilar membrane move up and down, causing "hairs" (cilia) to bend
   6. Bending of hair cells releases neurotransmitter → stimulates auditory nerve

**Semicircular Canals**
- Found in inner ear but NOT involved in hearing
- Role: sense orientation in space
- Involved with balance

---

## Sound Localization

**Most important cues: Interaural differences**
- **Interaural time difference** — when a sound reaches one ear vs the other (opposite)
- **Interaural intensity difference** — difference of intensity between left and right
- Differences are largest for sounds to the left or right
- No difference for sounds directly in front or behind

---

## Pitch Perception

**Temporal Coding (Low Freq)**
- Basilar membrane vibrates at a rate that matches frequency
- Hair cells generate action potential at a rate that matches frequency

**Place Coding (High Freq)**
- Wave in cochlea causes a wave in the basilar membrane
- Where the wave "peaks" depends on frequency
- High freq: peak near the base
- Low freq: peak near the tip of cochlea
- Works best for high freq (4,000 Hz)
- **Temporal and place coding work together** to cover entire range of human hearing

## Gustation — How We Taste

- Chemical process: molecules from food (or other things) dissolved in fluid on tongue
- Stimulate taste receptors within taste buds
- Action potentials travel to thalamus, then to frontal lobes
- Tastes also rely on other senses: smell + texture

**Five Taste Sensations:**
Sweet, Salty, Bitter, Sour, **Umami** (savory — based on glutamate)
- Distributed uniformly across the tongue

**Supertasters**
- About 25% of the population
- Experience flavors more intensely
- Often sensitive to bitter foods (broccoli, coffee, beer)
- Possibly more taste buds
- Genetic factors

## Olfactory — How We Smell

- Chemical process
- Chemicals (odorants) enter nasal cavity → stimulate factors in olfactory
- **Only sense that does NOT involve the thalamus**
- Sends directly to the **olfactory bulb** + **amygdala & hippocampus**
- → Odor-related memories

## Learning

**Definition:** A relatively enduring change of behavior
- Non-associative: such as in class
- **Associative:** learning about how things go together
  - In the real world — "If I do this, this is the result"
  - Observational learning

## Classical Conditioning (Pavlov / Pavlovian Conditioning)

- Pavlov studied salivary response in dogs
- Dogs salivated when food was in front → over time, salivated when lab assistant entered room
- Dogs learned that lab assistant and food were associated
- Metronome → food → metronome → food (over and over) → dog learns **conditioned response** to salivate when metronome starts

**Principles of Classical Conditioning:**
- **Acquisition** — learning portion (e.g., metronome–food pairing)
- **Second-order conditioning**
- **Extinction** — after taught to salivate, stop giving food → will eventually unlearn to salivate to metronome
- **Spontaneous Recovery** — after extinction, if you bring back the metronome another day, it will salivate
- **Stimulus Generalization** — when learned, will apply that knowledge to other situations (ticking of a clock instead of metronome) → Natural/Innate Learn
- **Stimulus Discrimination** — teach the dog two different stimuli means two different things (Metronome A = Food) (Metronome B = No food) → Unnatural/Human Learn

**Evolutionary Significance:**
- Some pairings are easier to associate
- Taste of soup → nausea (sushi and food poisoning)

## Classical Conditioning and Phobias

**John Watson**
- Behaviorist
- Rejected Freud's idea of unconscious primitive phobias
- Thought fears come from learning
- **Little Albert** experiment:
  - Baby not fearful of anything (rat, rabbits, dogs, monkeys)
  - Loud sound = fear → unconditioned fear
  - Rat = neutral stimulus → paired with loud noise → **conditioned response** (learned to be afraid)
  - **Very Unethical**
  - He became afraid of things with fur
  - Into adulthood he was still fearful of animals
  - Produced a phobia that affected his life

## Operant Conditioning

**Thorndike**
- Puzzle Box — cat presses lever → gets food → would probably do it again
- Cat presses lever → gets shocked → not going to do again

**B.F. Skinner**
- **Reinforcer** — reinforces behavior: Pos–Pos / Neg–Neg
- Used "Skinner Box" — lever delivers food inside box

**Principles of Reinforcement** (makes behavior more likely):
- **Positive Reinforcement** — giving something (reward) → rat pushes button for food
- **Negative Reinforcement** — removes something they don't like (e.g., blast music until room is clean → turn off music when done) — **This is NOT punishment**

**Punishment** (stops behavior):
- **Positive Punishment** — providing an unpleasant stimulus (yelling)
- **Negative Punishment** — taking away a positive stimulus / something they like (take away phone from teen)

**Effective Punishment:**
- Must be unpleasant, reasonable, and immediately after bad behavior
- Physical punishment is not effective — unreasonable and normally way after the bad behavior

---

## Schedules of Reinforcement

**Continuous Reinforcement (Positive)**
- Every time rat presses lever → gets food pellet
- Fast to learn, very first way to train

**Partial Reinforcement**
- Only give pellet sometimes when it presses lever
- More realistic in real world; slower learning

**Ratio vs. Interval:**
- **Ratio:** reinforce every Nth time (e.g., every 5th time)
- **Interval:** reinforce after a certain amount of time
  - Fixed vs. Variable

| Schedule | Rate | Extinction |
|----------|------|-----------|
| Fixed Interval | Low rate; scallop shape (bursts right before reward) | Fast |
| Variable Interval | Unpredictable; rat continuously presses lever | Slow |
| Fixed Ratio | Faster rate of behavior; faster behavior → faster payoff | - |
| Variable Ratio | Extremely high rate of behavior; continuous | Slowest |

**Continuous vs. Partial:**
- Continuous → doesn't take long for rat to learn; very fast
- Partial → learning takes longer; food is more unpredictable

## Observational Learning

**Albert Bandura**
- Seeing someone be aggressive → choose to be aggressive
- Also seeing punishment toward someone → won't do the same thing they did
- Punishment → stops behavior
- Reinforcement → makes behavior (more likely)

## Memory

**What is Memory?**
- The ability to retain information
- The ability to recognize certain things
- Encoding → Consolidation → Storing → **Retrieval** (recalling things when you need)

**Where is Memory in the Brain?**
- Hippocampus — responsible for forming and strengthening new memories
  - But does NOT store memories
  - Severe memory loss after removal of hippocampus
  - Can't form new memories without hippocampus
  - Needed to build and strengthen connections in memory
- Types of memory are in different parts of the cortex

**Sensory Memory**
- Stored briefly
- High capacity, high accuracy, but **short-lived**

**Short-term Memory and Working Memory**
- Temp storage of a small amount of info over a short interval
- Duration is variable and can be increased through rehearsal
