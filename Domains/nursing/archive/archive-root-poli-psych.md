## Psychology — Exam 1 Notes (Handwritten)

### Schools of Thought
- **Structuralism**: break consciousness into elements (Wundt, Titchener)
- **Gestalt**: whole > sum of parts; perception is holistic
- **Behaviorism**: only observable behavior; stimulus-response (Watson, Skinner)
- **Cognitive**: mental processes — thinking, memory, problem-solving

### Neuroscience Basics
- Neurons: cell body, dendrites (input), axon (output), myelin sheath, synapse
- Brain structures: cortex, limbic system, brainstem
- **Fight-or-flight**: sympathetic nervous system activation; epinephrine/norepinephrine release

## Psychology — Exam 2 Study Guide (Kanect Review)

### Sensation & Perception
- Sensation = detection of stimuli by sensory receptors
- Perception = interpretation of sensory info by the brain
- Top-down processing: expectations/knowledge shape perception
- Bottom-up processing: raw sensory data drives perception

### Classical Conditioning
- Pavlov → neutral stimulus paired with unconditioned stimulus → conditioned response
- **Little Albert** (Watson & Rayner): infant conditioned to fear white rat; generalized to similar stimuli; demonstrates fear acquisition through conditioning

### Observational Learning
- **Bobo Doll Study** (Bandura): children imitated aggressive behavior modeled by adults; learning without direct reinforcement

### Memory
- **Consolidation**: process of stabilizing a memory after initial encoding
- **Working memory**: ~7 items (±2) held in short-term; Miller's Law
- Memory stages: encoding → storage → retrieval

## Psychology — Unit 3

### Emotions, Feelings, Moods
- **Emotions**: short-lived, high-intensity, tied to specific trigger
- **Feelings**: subjective conscious experience of emotion
- **Moods**: longer-lasting, lower intensity, not necessarily tied to a cause
- **Circumplex model**: emotions organized on two dimensions — valence (positive/negative) and arousal (high/low)

### Emotion Theories
| Theory | Key Idea |
|--------|----------|
| **James-Lange** | Physiological change → emotion (you see bear → heart races → you feel fear) |
| **Cannon-Bard** | Physiological change and emotion happen simultaneously, independently |
| **Schachter-Singer (Two-Factor)** | Physiological arousal + cognitive label = emotion |

### Motivation
- **Yerkes-Dodson Law**: performance increases with arousal up to an optimal point, then decreases; inverted U curve
- **Delayed gratification (Marshmallow Test)**: children who waited had better life outcomes; tied to self-regulation capacity

### Development
- **Piaget's Stages**: Sensorimotor (0-2) → Preoperational (2-7) → Concrete Operational (7-11) → Formal Operational (12+)
- **Kohlberg's Moral Development**: Preconventional → Conventional → Postconventional

### Personality
- Personality is **40–60% heritable** (twin studies); environment accounts for remainder

## Chapter 3 — Federalism

### Government Structures
- **Unitary**: central government holds power (UK)
- **Confederation**: states hold power; weak center (Articles of Confederation)
- **Federal**: power shared between national and state governments (US)

### Key Constitutional Clauses
- **National Supremacy Clause**: federal law supreme over state law
- **Commerce Clause**: Congress regulates interstate commerce
- **Necessary and Proper (N&P) Clause**: Congress can make laws to carry out enumerated powers
- **Full Faith and Credit**: states must honor other states' legal acts/records
- **Privileges and Immunities (P&I)**: states can't discriminate against citizens of other states

### Grants
- **Categorical grants**: specific purpose, strict conditions
- **Block grants**: broad purpose, more state flexibility
- **Unfunded mandates**: federal requirements on states without funding

### Eras of Federalism
1. **Dual Federalism** ("layer cake"): strict separation of federal/state roles
2. **Cooperative Federalism** ("marble cake"): intertwined, shared functions
3. **New Federalism**: devolution — return power to states (Reagan era)
4. **Ad Hoc Federalism**: selective application depending on political goals

## Chapter 4 — Political Socialization and Public Opinion

### Key Definitions
- **Political culture**: shared values and beliefs about government
- **Political socialization**: process by which people acquire political beliefs (family, school, media, peers)
- **Public opinion**: collective views of citizens on political issues

### Surveys and Polling
- Random sample of ~1,000 → margin of error ±3.1% (adequate for national polling)
- Party ID: Democrat / Independent / Republican spectrum
- Ideology spectrum: liberal ← center → conservative

### Voter Types (Philip Converse)
- Only ~10% of voters are true **ideologues** with consistent, constraint-based belief systems
- 5 types: ideologues, near-ideologues, group benefits voters, nature of times voters, no issue content

### Polarization
- **Elite polarization**: politicians and activists have become more ideologically extreme
- **Mass polarization**: public is not as polarized as elites — more centrist distribution
- **Partisan sorting**: people increasingly align party ID with ideology, geography, education
- **Social identity/sorting**: in-group bias; party ID as social identity, not just policy preference
- **Affective polarization**: emotional hostility toward the opposing party
- **Folk theory of democracy**: assumes voters hold coherent ideological preferences and vote accordingly — empirically weak

## Chapter 5 — The Mass Media and the Political Agenda

### Media History
1. **Partisan Press era**: newspapers openly backed political parties
2. **Muckraker era**: investigative journalism; expose corruption
3. **Objectivity era**: professional norms of neutrality; wire services spread standardized content

### Regulation
- **FCC / Communications Act 1934**: federal regulation of broadcast media
- **Telecom Act 1996**: deregulation; allowed consolidation of media ownership

### Media Effects
- **Horse race coverage**: focus on who's winning rather than policy substance
- **Agenda setting**: media determines what the public thinks *about* (not what to think)
- **Framing**: how a story is presented shapes how audience interprets it
- **7 frame types**: conflict, human interest, economic, morality, attribution of responsibility, etc.
- **Iyengar & Kinder (1987)**: classic agenda-setting study — TV news exposure shifts issue importance for viewers
- **Bernard Cohen quote**: "The press may not be successful in telling people what to think, but it is stunningly successful in telling people what to think about."

### Decline of Local News
- 2,000+ local newspapers closed since 2004
- **Darr et al. (2018)**: newspaper closures → increased partisan polarization in voting
- **Moskowitz (2020)**: local news presence → more split-ticket voting (less nationalization)
- **Martin & McCrain (2019)**: Sinclair media ownership → more conservative/nationalized content

### Solutions to Nationalization (4 proposed)
1. Public funding for local journalism
2. Tax incentives for local news outlets
3. Nonprofit news models
4. Platform accountability (tech companies share ad revenue)

## Chapter 7 — Political Parties

### Definition
A political party is an organization that nominates candidates, contests elections, and seeks to control government.

### Party History Timeline
- Federalists vs Democratic-Republicans → Democrats vs Whigs → Democrats vs Republicans (Civil War realignment) → Progressive era reforms → New Deal alignment → post-1960s sorting

### Electoral Structure
- **Duverger's Law**: single-winner plurality systems → two-party systems (third parties structurally disadvantaged)
- **Affective polarization + zero-sum thinking**: downsides of strong partisan identity

### 3 Ways to Think About Parties
1. As electoral organizations (run campaigns)
2. As parties in the electorate (voters who identify with party)
3. As parties in government (elected officials)

### Party Structure
- Pyramid structure: national committee → state parties → local parties
- Decline of state and local party organizations in recent decades

### Ideology Asymmetry (Grossman & Hopkins)
- **Democrats**: coalition of social groups (women, minorities, unions, LGBTQ+) — pragmatic
- **Republicans**: ideological movement — more internally consistent conservative ideology

### Data
- **DW-NOMINATE** scores show increasing ideological distance between parties since 1970s
- New Deal alignment traces still visible in current coalitions

## Chapter 9 — Congress

### Structure
| | Senate | House |
|---|---|---|
| Members | 100 | 435 |
| Term | 6 years | 2 years |
| Classes | 3 rotating classes | N/A |
| Election | Direct (17th Amendment) | Direct |

### Legislative Process (Article I, Sec. 7)
Bill introduction → Committee → Rules Committee (House) → Floor debate → Conference Committee (if needed) → President

### Filibuster
- Senate only: unlimited debate can block legislation
- **Cloture**: requires 60 votes to end debate
- 41 senators can block a bill

### Key Roles
- **Speaker of the House**: sets agenda, controls Rules Committee, leads majority
- **Policy Gatekeepers**: committee chairs, Rules Committee, leadership

### Representation (Pitkin's 4 Types)
1. Formalistic (authority to act)
2. Descriptive (looks like constituents)
3. Symbolic (stands for something)
4. Substantive (acts in constituents' interests)

### Impediments to Representation
- **Constitutional**: staggered terms, bicameralism, veto, filibuster, federalism
- **Practical**: gerrymandering, pork barrel spending, incumbency advantage, casework, money

### Electoral Connection (Mayhew, 1974)
Members of Congress primarily motivated by reelection:
- **Advertising**: name recognition
- **Credit claiming**: taking responsibility for benefits (especially pork/casework)
- **Position taking**: public stances on issues to appeal to constituents
- Incumbency reelection rate: **85–99%**

## Chapter 10 — The Presidency

### Presidential Powers
- **Expressed**: explicitly in Constitution (commander-in-chief, veto, treaties, appointments)
- **Delegated**: granted by Congress through legislation
- **Inherent**: implied from broad constitutional language or historical practice

### War Powers
- **War Powers Resolution (1973)**: president must notify Congress within 48 hours of deploying troops; must withdraw within 60 days without congressional authorization

### Executive Tools
- **Executive orders**: directives with force of law; don't require congressional approval
- **Veto**: reject legislation; **pocket veto**: inaction when Congress adjourns (no override possible)
- **OMB (Office of Management and Budget)**: manages federal budget; part of Executive Office of the President
- **"Going public"**: strategy of appealing directly to public to pressure Congress (Kernell)

### Executive Office of the President
- OMB, NSC, Council of Economic Advisers, White House staff

## Chapter 14 — Civil Rights

### Reconstruction Amendments
- **13th (1865)**: abolished slavery
- **14th (1868)**: equal protection and due process; citizenship by birth
- **15th (1870)**: right to vote cannot be denied based on race

### Key Cases and Laws
- **Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)**: "separate but equal" doctrine — legalized segregation
- **Brown v. Board of Education (1954)**: overturned Plessy; segregated schools unconstitutional
- **Civil Rights Act of 1964**: prohibited discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin in public accommodations and employment
- **Voting Rights Act of 1965**: outlawed discriminatory voting practices (literacy tests, poll taxes)

### Women's Rights
- **4 waves of feminism**: 1st (suffrage), 2nd (workplace/legal equality), 3rd (intersectionality), 4th (digital/MeToo era)
- **Title IX (1972)**: prohibited sex discrimination in federally funded education programs
- **Equal Rights Amendment (ERA)**: passed Congress 1972; never ratified by enough states
